# Form 1040 — Line 1b — Household employee wages not reported on Form(s) W-2

**Tax year: 2025**

This document explains how the dollar amount that goes on **Line 1b** of Form 1040 is calculated. Line 1b reports wages a person earned working as a *household employee* (someone who works in or around a private home — for example a nanny, caregiver, housekeeper, gardener) **when the employer did not issue a Form W-2 for those wages**.

The document is organized in three parts:

1. **The IRS rule.** What the controlling IRS publications say must happen. This is the source of truth.
2. **The code implementation.** What the application's backend Java code currently does.
3. **The comparison.** A side-by-side check showing whether the code follows the IRS rule honestly, and where the two diverge.

A note on vocabulary used in this document:

- **Household employee**: a worker who performs domestic services in or around a private home (such as a nanny, caregiver, cook, gardener, driver) and whose work is *directed* by the homeowner. The homeowner controls *what* is done and *how* it is done. This is the IRS "common-law employee" relationship.
- **Self-employed (independent contractor)**: a worker who controls the methods and means of their own work — they decide how to do the job, often serve multiple clients, and may provide their own tools. Income from this type of work belongs on a different schedule (Schedule C — Profit or Loss From Business), which is out of scope for this application. It is **not** Line 1b income.
- **Form W-2 (Wage and Tax Statement)**: the wage statement a regular employer sends to an employee at year end. Box 1 holds the year's taxable wages.
- **Form 8919 (Uncollected Social Security and Medicare Tax on Wages)**: the IRS form used by an employee whose employer wrongly treated them as a contractor (and issued a Form 1099 instead of a W-2). Wages reported on Form 8919 land on a different line (Line 1g), so they must not also be counted on Line 1b — that would double-count the same dollars.

## Part 1 — The IRS rule (the source of truth)

### 1.1 Authority

The IRS publications used to write this document are in `C:\us-tax\docs\books\`:

- **2025 Instructions for Form 1040 / 1040-SR** (`i1040gi_2025.pdf`) — the controlling rule for the Line 1b label and the $2,800 W-2-obligation threshold for tax year 2025.
- **Publication 17 — Your Federal Income Tax** (`p17.pdf`) — the master guide for individuals filing Form 1040. Chapter 5 (Wages, Salaries, and Other Earnings) reinforces the rule that household wages without a Form W-2 are reported as ordinary wages.
- **Publication 525 — Taxable and Nontaxable Income** — defines what types of pay are taxable. Household-employee wages are taxable and must be reported.
- **Instructions for Schedule H (Form 1040)** — *Household Employment Taxes*. This document defines the *employee* versus *self-employed* relationship in plain terms (*"Self-employed workers are also not your employees. A worker is self-employed if only the worker can control how the work is done."*) and explains the employer's Form W-2 filing obligation when federal income tax has been withheld.
- **Publication 926 — Household Employer's Tax Guide** — the companion document for the *employer side* and the authoritative reference for the $2,800 threshold, the definition of household work, and the federal-tax-withholding rules. Currently NOT in `docs/books/` (only the i1040gi PDF, p17, and p525 are present locally); cited indirectly through Schedule H instructions and the Form 1040 instructions.

### 1.2 The basic rule

From the 2025 Instructions for Form 1040, line 1b:

> Enter the total of your wages received as a household employee that was not reported on Form(s) W-2.

In plain English: Line 1b is the **total of household-employee wages for which no Form W-2 was issued**, summed across the taxpayer and (on a joint return) the spouse.

```text
Line 1b = sum of all household-employee wages
          earned during the tax year
          that were NOT reported on any Form W-2
```

That is the entire rule. Line 1b is a **reporting location**, not a threshold. There is no minimum dollar amount that triggers the line, and there is no maximum.

### 1.3 What counts as an "eligible wage" for Line 1b

A wage amount belongs on Line 1b only when **all four** of the following are true:

1. **It is compensation for personal services performed as a household employee.**
   - Examples of household work: babysitting, child care, elder care, housekeeping, cleaning, cooking, gardening, yard work, household driving, personal assistance in a private home.
   - The work is performed *in or around* a private home (which may include a local college club or fraternity / sorority chapter house).

2. **The relationship is one of employer and employee (the homeowner controls what work is done and how it is done).**
   - If the worker controls how the work is done — they set the schedule, choose the methods, run their own business — the relationship is *self-employed* and the income belongs on Schedule C (out of scope here).
   - The IRS distinction is explained in the Instructions for Schedule H: *"Self-employed workers are also not your employees. A worker is self-employed if only the worker can control how the work is done."*

3. **The wages were not reported on any Form W-2.**
   - If a Form W-2 was issued and reports those wages in Box 1, the amount belongs on Line 1a — not Line 1b. Double-counting is not allowed.

4. **The wages are not being reported on Form 8919.**
   - Form 8919 is the path for an employee whose employer mis-classified them as a contractor (gave them a Form 1099 instead of a W-2). Those wages flow to Line 1g, not Line 1b. Including them on Line 1b would double-count.

### 1.4 Why a Form W-2 might be missing — the optional threshold explanation

The 2025 Form 1040 Instructions explain that a household employer is **not required** to issue a Form W-2 when the cash wages paid during the year were below a certain amount. For 2025 that amount is **$2,800**. (For tax year 2024 the same threshold was $2,700.) Below this threshold the employer can choose not to send a Form W-2.

It is critical to understand that this threshold:

- **Does NOT determine whether Line 1b applies.** The wages still belong on Line 1b — regardless of amount.
- **Only explains why** a household worker might not have a Form W-2 to begin with.

So the IRS instruction is: even a small amount of household-employee wages must be reported on Line 1b when no Form W-2 was issued for them.

### 1.5 Federal income tax withholding — a strong signal that a W-2 was required

If the household employer voluntarily withheld federal income tax from the worker's pay, the Instructions for Schedule H indicate that the employer was required to file a Form W-2 regardless of the $2,800 threshold. In that situation a missing Form W-2 is a compliance failure on the employer's side. The worker should still report the wages on Line 1b, but the application warns the worker about this and points them at Form 4852 (Substitute W-2) as a path to obtain one in writing.

This rule has no effect on the *amount* on Line 1b — it is purely a user-interface warning. The amount of wages flows in the same way regardless.

### 1.6 What does NOT go on Line 1b

| Type of income | Goes on |
|---|---|
| Wages reported on a Form W-2 (Box 1) | Line 1a |
| Wages reported on Form 8919 (employer mis-classified the worker as a contractor — issued a Form 1099 instead of a W-2) | Line 1g |
| Self-employment income from running an independent domestic-services business | Schedule C (out of scope here) |
| Tips not reported to employer | Line 1c |
| Dependent care benefits | Line 1e |

### 1.7 The complete IRS equation (plain English)

Putting all parts together, the IRS rule for Line 1b is:

```text
For each separate household employer the worker had during the tax year:
    if the worker is genuinely an employee of that household
    (the homeowner controls what work is done and how it is done):
        if NO Form W-2 was issued for those wages:
            if the worker is NOT filing Form 8919 for those wages:
                include those wages in the running total

For a Married Filing Separately return:
    include only the filer's own household-employee wages.
    Do not aggregate the spouse's household-employee wages.

Line 1b = the resulting total (rounded to whole dollars at the very end
          if the taxpayer is rounding their return — see the Form 1040
          instructions on "Rounding Off to Whole Dollars").
```

### 1.8 Whole-dollar rounding

The Form 1040 Instructions allow whole-dollar rounding (drop amounts below 50 cents, round amounts of 50 cents through 99 cents up to the next whole dollar). Rounding is optional but, if used, must be applied consistently throughout the return. Computed sums should add the precise amounts first and round only the final result.

### 1.9 Reference data (constants that may change year to year)

For Line 1b itself **there is no tax-year-specific numeric constant used in the computation**. The computation is a pure sum.

The threshold below which a household employer is not required to issue a Form W-2 is a tax-year constant, but it is used **only for user-interface guidance and warnings** — never inside the math.

| Reference constant | 2025 value | 2024 value | Where it lives |
|---|---|---|---|
| Household-employee Form W-2 obligation threshold | $2,800 / year / employer | $2,700 / year / employer | `ReferenceData.HOUSE_HOLD_EMPLOYEE_THRESHOLD` in the backend; also exposed by `GET /api/reference-data` for the front end |

Whenever the IRS updates this number for a future tax year, the constant should be updated.

## Part 3 — Comparison: IRS rule vs. code implementation

This section maps each IRS rule from Part 1 onto the code from Part 2, marking each as honestly implemented, intentionally stricter, or a known gap.

| IRS rule | Code behavior | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Include only household-employee wages (not self-employment income) | The `householdEmployeeUnderControlTest` gate at `:15708` returns `null` for the person when the answer is not exactly `true`; a blocking flag forces the user to resolve the inconsistency before the return can be computed. | **Honest** |
| Include only wages not reported on a Form W-2 | The `householdReceivedW2` gate returns `null` for the person when the answer is not exactly `false`. (If the worker received a W-2 the wages belong on Line 1a; if the answer is missing the code is conservatively unwilling to assume "no W-2".) | **Honest** |
| Exclude wages reported on Form 8919 (avoid double-count with Line 1g) | Per-employer flag `employerTreatedAsContractor`: when `true`, that one employer's wages are skipped in the Line 1b sum and instead routed to Form 8919 → Line 1g. | **Honest** — and at a finer granularity than the IRS instructions describe. The user can have multiple household employers and only the mis-classified ones are diverted; the legitimate household-employer wages still appear on Line 1b. |
| Sum wages across all household employers | The loop at `:15708` accumulates `entry.wages` for rows that pass the per-row contractor guard. | **Honest** |
| On a Married Filing Separately return, do not include the spouse's household wages | The `sumHouseholdEmployeeWages` aggregator at `:15696` skips the spouse call when the derived `isMfs` flag is `true`. | **Honest** |
| On a Married Filing Jointly return, include both spouses' household wages | The same aggregator includes both subtotals when `isMfs` is `false`. | **Honest** |
| Whole-dollar rounding at the very end of the computation | `roundMoney(rawTotal)` is applied as the last step before storing the field. The internal sum keeps original precision. | **Honest** |
| The $2,800 threshold is informational only and does not gate the computation | The code never reads `HOUSE_HOLD_EMPLOYEE_THRESHOLD` inside the Line 1b math. It is exposed to the user interface for hint text only. | **Honest** |

### 3.1 Items the code does NOT support (intentional out-of-scope decisions)

1. **Self-employed household-service businesses** — Schedule C is out of scope, so the application refuses to process a household worker who fails the control test (emits a blocking flag and excludes the wages from Line 1b).
2. **Form 4852 (Substitute W-2) attached to Line 1b** — Form 4852 is used to substitute for a missing W-2 when wages should have appeared on Line 1a. It is not part of the Line 1b path. A household worker who *should* have received a Form W-2 (for example because the employer withheld federal income tax) is encouraged through UI guidance to use Form 4852 — but those wages then flow through Line 1a, not Line 1b.

### 3.2 Items where the code is STRICTER than the IRS rule (defensive measures)

1. **Outer `hasEmploymentIncome` gate.** The IRS rule does not require the worker to first declare "I had employment income"; it just says household wages without a W-2 go on Line 1b. The application introduces this declaration as a UI gate so the household-work questions are surfaced in the right context. The backend re-checks the gate in `householdEmployeeAmount(...)` so that a malformed direct-API submission (for example `hasEmploymentIncome = false` together with a populated `householdEmployers` list) cannot inflate Line 1b. Purely defensive — when the user uses the official UI, the gate is enforced there too.
2. **Conservative handling of the W-2 answer.** The code only includes the wages on Line 1b when `householdReceivedW2` is *explicitly* `false`. An unanswered question (`null`) is not treated as "no W-2" — the wages stay out of Line 1b until the user has affirmatively confirmed.

### 3.3 Items captured in the input form but not used by the compute math

| Field | Used by | Why captured |
|---|---|---|
| `employerName` (per-employer) | UI display, validator warnings | Helpful for the worker's own records and for any warning message that needs to name a specific employer. |
| `federalTaxWithheld` (per-employer) | UI warning + `validateWithholdingOnContractorHouseholdFirm` (the §6.8 cross-form check) | When `true`, the front end shows a named-employer warning. The compute math never reads this field for the Line 1b sum itself. |

These are honest, intentional non-uses for the Line 1b sum — they participate in adjacent validators.

### 3.4 Missing fields or gaps

None identified for Line 1b itself. Specifically:

- The four IRS gating conditions from §1.3 each have a corresponding answer field on the Employment Income form (`householdWork`, `householdEmployeeUnderControlTest`, `householdReceivedW2`, `employerTreatedAsContractor`).
- The output field `householdEmployeeWages` on the Form 1040 model is wired to the data-flow diagram for the line (`c:/us-tax/diagrams/1b.drawio`) and to the front-end Form 1040 preview.
- The blocking-flag pair `HOUSEHOLD_WORK_SELF_EMPLOYMENT_*` is documented and tested.
- The Married Filing Separately guard is implemented; locked in by unit tests in `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.java`.
- The Form 8919 collision guard is implemented (per-employer `employerTreatedAsContractor`) and tested.

The diagram at `c:/us-tax/diagrams/1b.drawio` is a basic five-box data-flow figure. It accurately depicts the high-level Inputs → Computation → Output relationship but does not show the four-gate decision tree, the blocking-flag node, or the Form 8919 collision guard. Refreshing it to match the decision-tree style of `1a.drawio` is tracked as a low-priority cosmetic item in `outstanding.md` — no compute impact.

## Part 4 — Summary

**Form 1040 Line 1b = sum of household-employee wages received during the tax year, across the taxpayer and (on a non-Married-Filing-Separately return) the spouse, where for each wage entry: the worker is genuinely a household employee (homeowner controls what work is done and how), the wages were not reported on any Form W-2, and the wages are not being routed to Form 8919 (and therefore Line 1g).**

The code in `householdEmployeeAmount` (`TaxReturnComputeService.java:15708`) plus `sumHouseholdEmployeeWages` (`:15696`) plus the call site in `buildIncome` (`:6033-6265`) implements this rule honestly. All four IRS gating conditions are enforced in the same order as the IRS rule, and one extra defensive gate (`hasEmploymentIncome`) is enforced first as a guard against malformed input. The $2,800 household-employer Form-W-2-obligation threshold is correctly treated as informational only — it never enters the Line 1b math. The Married Filing Separately guard and the Form 8919 collision guard are both implemented and tested. The only fields captured by the input form that the compute code does not read (`employerName`, `federalTaxWithheld`) are deliberately UI-only and do not affect the dollar amount on the line.

## Part 5 — Worked example: misclassified household employee (1099 instead of W-2)

**Scenario.** A homeowner hired someone to perform household work (for example a nanny, housekeeper, gardener) and paid them. Instead of issuing a Form W-2 at year end, the homeowner wrongly treated the worker as an independent contractor and issued a Form 1099-MISC or 1099-NEC. The worker believes they were actually an employee — the homeowner directed what work was done and how it was done — and they want to file their tax return correctly.

The question is: **does the worker file Form 8919 in this situation, and where do the wages land on Form 1040?**

### 5.1 Yes — Form 8919 is the right form, and the wages move from Line 1b to Line 1g

The 2025 IRS Form 8919 instructions list four "must-file" tests. A misclassified household worker meets all four:

1. **You performed services for a firm.** Yes — a private household qualifies as a "firm" per the Form 8919 instructions, which define *firm* as "any individual, business enterprise, company, nonprofit organization, state, or other entity for which you performed services. This firm may or may not have paid you directly for these services."
2. **You believe your pay wasn't for services as an independent contractor.** Yes — by hypothesis the worker is a household employee, not a contractor.
3. **The firm didn't withhold your share of Social Security and Medicare taxes.** Yes — because the homeowner treated the worker as a contractor and issued a 1099, no FICA was withheld from the worker's pay.
4. **A reason code applies.** Yes — typically reason code G (see §5.2).

When all four are met the worker files Form 8919 and pays their share of Social Security and Medicare tax. The numbers flow as follows:

- The wages from Form 8919 line 6 → **Form 1040 line 1g** (Wages from Form 8919, line 6), NOT Line 1b.
- The uncollected FICA from Form 8919 line 13 → **Schedule 2 line 13** → Form 1040 line 23 (other taxes from Schedule 2) → adds to total tax on Form 1040 line 24.

Putting the wages on Line 1b in this situation would double-count them, because they are already being captured on Line 1g via the Form 8919 path. This is exactly why the application carries the per-employer collision guard described in §1.3(4), §2.4 (Step 3.5), and Part 3 above: when `employerTreatedAsContractor = true` is set on a household-employer row, the code's `householdEmployeeAmount` skips that row in the Line 1b sum, leaving the wages to be reported once on Line 1g via Form 8919.

### 5.2 Which Form 8919 reason code applies

The 2025 IRS Form 8919 instructions list four reason codes. For the specific 1099-only scenario above:

- **Reason code H** does *not* apply. Code H is reserved for the situation where the worker received BOTH a Form W-2 AND a Form 1099-MISC or 1099-NEC from the same firm in the same tax year (and the amount on the 1099 should have been on the W-2). In this scenario the worker received only a 1099, so Code H is wrong.
- **Reason code A** applies if the worker has filed Form SS-8 (Determination of Worker Status) and has *received* a determination letter from the IRS stating they are an employee of this firm.
- **Reason code C** applies if the worker has received other IRS correspondence stating they are an employee.
- **Reason code G** applies if the worker has filed Form SS-8 but has not yet received a reply.

The Form 8919 instructions are explicit about the fallback path: *"If none of the reason codes apply to you, but you believe you should have been treated as an employee, enter reason code G and file Form SS-8 on or before the date you file your tax return."*

For a worker who has not previously interacted with the IRS about their classification, the standard sequence is:

1. **File Form SS-8** with the IRS separately from the tax return.
2. **Attach Form 8919** to the Form 1040 with reason code G, with the firm details filled in.

### 5.3 How the application handles this scenario today

1. On the Employment Income form (`form-employment-{taxpayer|spouse}.xlsx`):
   - Set `hasEmploymentIncome = true`.
   - Set `householdWork = true`.
   - Set `householdEmployeeUnderControlTest = true`.
   - Set `householdReceivedW2 = false`.
   - Add an entry under `householdEmployers[]` with the firm name and the wage amount.
   - **Set `employerTreatedAsContractor = true`** on that household-employer entry — the collision-guard flag.
2. On the Form 8919 input form (`form-uncollected-ss-medicare-{taxpayer|spouse}.xlsx`):
   - Add a firm row with the same firm name and EIN/SSN, reason code `G` (or A/C if an SS-8 determination already exists), `received1099MiscOrNec = true`, and `wagesNoFicaNotW2` = the 1099 amount.

Backend compute then:

- The Line 1b computation sees `employerTreatedAsContractor = true` on that row and **excludes** the wages from Line 1b.
- The Form 8919 computation processes the firm row, produces the per-row wages amount for line 6, and computes the uncollected FICA on line 13.
- Form 8919 line 6 → Form 1040 **line 1g**.
- Form 8919 line 13 → Schedule 2 line 13 → Form 1040 line 23 → adds to total tax on line 24.

### 5.4 Cross-form coherence checks for the §5 scenario

Two blocking flags protect the worker from common silent-loss failure modes when this scenario is set up across multiple forms:

- **`MISMATCH_CONTRACTOR_HOUSEHOLD_NEEDS_FORM_8919_*`** — fires when the user toggled `employerTreatedAsContractor = true` with positive wages but didn't add a matching Form 8919 firm row. Without this, the wages would be excluded from Line 1b by the collision guard but have no path to Line 1g either — silent **under-reporting**. Blocking; HTTP 409 from compute. Resolution: either add the matching Form 8919 row, change the contractor flag back to `false`, or pass `overrideFlags=true` (accepting the under-reporting consequence — rare).

- **`WITHHOLDING_ON_CONTRACTOR_HOUSEHOLD_FIRM_NEEDS_1099_STATEMENT_*`** — fires when the user toggled BOTH `employerTreatedAsContractor = true` AND `federalTaxWithheld = true`, but no matching 1099-MISC / 1099-NEC statement is on file. Without this, the worker would lose their withholding credit (potentially thousands of dollars) because Line 25b sources from the 1099 statements, not from the household-employer row. Blocking; HTTP 409. Resolution: add the matching 1099 statement with the actual Box 4 withholding amount, change `federalTaxWithheld` to `No`, or `overrideFlags=true` (when the withholding is captured elsewhere — rare).

Match heuristic for both: substring case-insensitive comparison of `firmName` / `payerNameAddress` against the household-employer `employerName`. The validators do not compare amounts — only that *some* matching record exists.

### 5.5 Variant: worker received BOTH a W-2 AND a 1099 from the same household

If the homeowner issued a W-2 for some of the year's pay AND a 1099 for the rest, reason code H applies on Form 8919 — *not* code G. In that case:

- The W-2 wages go on Line 1a through the normal W-2 path.
- The 1099 wages go on Line 1g through the Form 8919 path with reason code H, column (e) checked.
- No SS-8 is required for code H (the instructions explicitly say: *"Don't file Form SS-8 if you select reason code H"*).

This W-2-plus-1099 variant is much less common for household workers but is handled by the same Form 8919 input form using a different reason code.

### 5.6 Variant: 1099 instead of W-2 WITH federal income tax withheld

A household employer who issued a 1099 instead of a W-2 may have *also* withheld federal income tax from the worker's pay. The federal income tax withholding changes the *severity* of the employer's error but does not change the basic routing for the worker.

- **Federal income tax withheld** flows from the 1099-MISC/NEC (Box 4) → Form 1040 **Line 25b** ("Federal income tax withheld from Form(s) 1099"). It does NOT go to Line 25a — even though a W-2 was what was actually owed, the form-of-record is the 1099, and the IRS matches Line 25b withholding to 1099 Box 4 amounts.
- **Wages** still flow through Form 8919 → Form 1040 **Line 1g**, exactly as in §5.1–§5.3.

So the wages and the withholding take *separate paths* on the return: the wages go through Form 8919 (Line 1g), and the withholding goes through the 1099 statement (Line 25b).

**Reason code is still G**, not H. Code H requires the worker to have received BOTH a W-2 AND a 1099 from the same firm in the same tax year. Here the worker received only a 1099 (with withholding), so reason code G applies and the worker files Form SS-8 separately on or before filing the 1040.

**Do NOT use Form 4852** in this scenario. Form 4852 (Substitute W-2) is for substituting a missing or incorrect W-2 *when the worker is claiming the income as W-2 wages on Line 1a*. Once the worker takes the Form 8919 route, the wages are on Line 1g (not Line 1a), and Form 4852 is no longer the right vehicle.

### 5.7 Current UI gating for the §5.6 variant

The Employment Income form's `*ngIf` template gate hides the "Did this employer withhold federal income tax from your wages?" question whenever `employerTreatedAsContractor === true`. As a result, the `federalTaxWithheld` field can never be set to `true` through the production UI for a contractor-flagged row.

Consequence: the `WITHHOLDING_ON_CONTRACTOR_HOUSEHOLD_FIRM_NEEDS_1099_STATEMENT_*` flag is effectively **unreachable from the UI today**. Its validator and unit tests still function — they exercise the data state directly via API and prove the backend behavior is correct — but they are exercising a state the UI does not currently produce.

The validator is retained as a defensive guard against:
1. **Direct API submissions** that bypass the UI gate.
2. **Future UI changes** that intentionally surface the question in both branches.
3. **Data import / migration** scenarios where boolean values flow in from another system.

If the UI is later updated to expose the §5.6 scenario, the smallest change is to remove the `*ngIf` gate on the `federalTaxWithheld` field and split the existing guidance message into two conditional blocks (Form 4852 path when `contractor=false`, Form 8919 + 1099-statement path when `contractor=true`). The §5.4 flag becomes user-reachable as soon as that UI change lands; no validator change required.
