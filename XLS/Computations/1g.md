# Form 1040 — Line 1g — Wages from Form 8919, line 6

**Tax year: 2025.**
**Last verified against the code on 2026-05-31.**

This document explains how the dollar amount that goes on **Line 1g** of Form 1040 is calculated. Line 1g reports the *wages* of a worker who was misclassified as an independent contractor by a hiring firm — wages on which the firm did NOT withhold the employee's share of Social Security and Medicare tax. The worker reports these wages and computes the unpaid employee-share Social Security + Medicare tax on **Form 8919** (*Uncollected Social Security and Medicare Tax on Wages*). Two amounts flow out of Form 8919: line 6 (the wages) goes to **Form 1040 line 1g**, and line 13 (the uncollected tax) goes to **Schedule 2 line 6**.

Form 8919 is a **per-spouse** form — on a Married Filing Jointly return both spouses can each have their own Form 8919, and Form 1040 line 1g sums their line-6 amounts. The application produces zero, one, or two Forms 8919 per return depending on which spouses have qualifying firms.

The document is organized in three parts:

1. **The IRS rule.** What the controlling IRS publications and Internal Revenue Code sections say must happen. This is the source of truth.
2. **The code implementation.** What the application's backend Java code currently does.
3. **The comparison.** A side-by-side check showing whether the code follows the IRS rule honestly, and where the two diverge.

A note on vocabulary used in this document:

- **Misclassified worker.** A person who performed services for a firm and believes the firm should have treated the pay as employee wages (subject to employee Social Security + Medicare withholding) rather than independent-contractor pay (which has no employer-side Social Security + Medicare withholding). The employee-vs-contractor test comes from Internal Revenue Code §3121(d) and §3508; common-law factors (degree of control, behavioral/financial control, type of relationship) are spelled out in Treasury Regulation §31.3121(d)-1 and Revenue Ruling 87-41.
- **Form 8919.** *Uncollected Social Security and Medicare Tax on Wages.* The IRS form a misclassified worker files to (a) report the wages on Form 1040 line 1g, and (b) self-assess and pay the *employee's* share of Social Security + Medicare tax on those wages (Schedule 2 line 6). The firm remains liable for the *employer's* share separately under Internal Revenue Code §3509, but that is the firm's problem, not the worker's.
- **Reason codes A / C / G / H.** Four codes the worker may enter on Form 8919 column (c). Each says how the worker arrived at the conclusion that the pay was misclassified employee wages. Section 1.3 below explains each.
- **Form SS-8.** *Determination of Worker Status for Purposes of Federal Employment Taxes and Income Tax Withholding.* A separate IRS form the worker (or the firm) may file to ask the IRS to formally determine whether the worker is an employee or an independent contractor. Reason code G on Form 8919 means a Form SS-8 has been filed and is pending. Reason code H means a Form SS-8 should NOT be filed for that firm (the worker received a W-2 + 1099 from the same firm for the same services and is just reclassifying part of the 1099 amount).
- **Social Security wage base.** The maximum amount of earnings subject to the 6.2% Social Security tax in a given year. For 2025 the wage base is **$176,100**. Once the worker's combined Social Security wages (W-2 box 3 + W-2 box 7 + Railroad Retirement Tax Act tier 1 compensation + Form 4137 line 10 unreported tips) reach the wage base, no additional Social Security tax is owed on Form 8919 wages — but the 1.45% Medicare tax has no wage-base cap, so Medicare tax continues.
- **Railroad Retirement Tax Act (RRTA) tier 1.** Railroad employees pay tier 1 tax instead of regular Social Security tax, at the same 6.2% rate up to the same wage base. RRTA tier 1 compensation appears in W-2 box 14 with a "RRTA" label. It counts toward the Social Security wage-base usage on Form 8919 line 8 the same way W-2 box 3 + box 7 do.
- **Form 4137.** *Social Security and Medicare Tax on Unreported Tip Income.* The worker files Form 4137 to pay Social Security + Medicare tax on tips the worker did not report to the employer. The Social-Security-taxable portion (Form 4137 line 10) counts toward the Form 8919 line 8 wage-base usage, because the same wage base is shared.
- **Form 8959.** *Additional Medicare Tax.* When the worker's combined Medicare wages exceed $200,000 (Single / Head of Household / Qualifying Surviving Spouse), $250,000 (Married Filing Jointly), or $125,000 (Married Filing Separately), an additional 0.9% Medicare tax applies on the excess. Form 8919 line 6 wages count toward the Form 8959 line 3 Medicare wage base; the application adds them in when Form 8959 is required.

## Part 1 — The IRS rule (the source of truth)

### 1.1 Authority

The IRS publications and Internal Revenue Code sections used to write this document are in `C:\us-tax\docs\books\` unless noted otherwise:

- **2025 Instructions for Form 1040 / 1040-SR** (`i1040gi_2025.pdf`) — the controlling rule for the Line 1g label. Defines Line 1g as "Wages from Form 8919, line 6".
- **Publication 17 — Your Federal Income Tax** (`p17.pdf`) — the "Wages, Salaries, and Other Earnings" chapter covers Form 8919 in lay terms; restates the reason-code list (A, C, G, H) and the misclassification test.
- **Publication 531 — Reporting Tip Income** (`p531.pdf`) — relevant for the Form 4137 line 10 reference inside Form 8919 line 8 (the Social-Security-taxable unreported-tip amount).
- **Form 8919 — Uncollected Social Security and Medicare Tax on Wages** (NOT local; available at <https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-prior/f8919--2025.pdf>) — the form itself, with the per-firm columns (a) through (f) and the per-spouse lines 6 through 13. Documentation gap: not bundled in `docs/books/`.
- **Instructions for Form 8919** (NOT local; available at <https://www.irs.gov/instructions/i8919>) — the procedural source for the reason codes and the line-by-line computation. Documentation gap: not bundled in `docs/books/`.
- **Internal Revenue Code §3121(a) and §3121(d)** — define wages (§3121(a)) and the employee-vs-contractor test (§3121(d)). The misclassified worker on Form 8919 is, by definition, someone who meets the §3121(d) employee test even though the firm treated them as something else.
- **Treasury Regulation §31.3121(d)-1** — the common-law employee test (behavioral control, financial control, type of relationship). Reasonable-basis safe harbor under §530 of the Revenue Act of 1978 is the firm-side counterpart to §3121(d) — but Form 8919 is filed by the WORKER, not the firm, so §530 is not directly relevant to the worker's filing.
- **Internal Revenue Code §3509** — special rates the IRS may assess the FIRM after determining misclassification. The §3509 reduced employer-side rates do NOT reduce the worker's Form 8919 self-assessment; the worker still computes the full 6.2% + 1.45% on the wages.
- **Internal Revenue Code §3101** — imposes the employee's share of Social Security tax (6.2%) and Medicare tax (1.45%). Form 8919 is the mechanism by which the worker pays these directly when the firm did not withhold.
- **Revenue Procedure 2024-40** — sets the 2025 Social Security wage base of $176,100. The application reads it from `ReferenceData.SOCIAL_SECURITY_WAGE_BASE` rather than hard-coding the value in line-1g logic.

Supplementary general-purpose references in `docs/books/`: `J.K. Lasser's Your Income Tax 2025` chapter on wages and Form 8919; `Taxes For Dummies 2024 Edition`. Both restate the same rules in lay terms; neither is the controlling source.

The local file `C:\us-tax\lines\1g.md` is the project's developer-facing specification for Line 1g. The companion knowledge file at `C:\us-tax\knowledge\line-1g-form-8919.md` and the dependency map at `C:\us-tax\dependencies\1g.md` track the same rules at finer-grained level (per-section field tables, two-spouse aggregation, downstream form integration, test coverage).

### 1.2 The basic equation

From the 2025 Instructions for Form 1040, line 1g:

> Enter the wages from Form 8919, line 6.

In plain English, the IRS rule for Line 1g is:

```text
Line 1g = the total wages from one or two Form 8919 instances on this
          return. Each Form 8919 is per-spouse and reports wages from
          firms that misclassified the worker as a contractor and did
          NOT withhold the employee share of Social Security and
          Medicare tax.

Form 1040 line 1g = Form 8919 (taxpayer) line 6
                  + Form 8919 (spouse) line 6        [on MFJ only]
```

The same wages also drive **Schedule 2 line 6** (the uncollected tax from each Form 8919 line 13) and **Form 8959 line 3** (the Medicare-wages base for Additional Medicare Tax, when Form 8959 is required).

### 1.3 The four reason codes (Form 8919 column c)

Each firm row on Form 8919 must carry one of these codes:

- **A** — The IRS issued a *determination letter* stating the worker is an employee of this firm. The worker must enter the determination date in column (d).
- **C** — The IRS issued *other correspondence* (not a determination letter) stating the worker is an employee of this firm. The worker must enter the correspondence date in column (d).
- **G** — The worker filed Form SS-8 with the IRS asking for a determination and has not yet received a reply. Form SS-8 itself is NOT attached to Form 1040; it is filed separately with the IRS.
- **H** — The worker received BOTH a Form W-2 and a Form 1099-MISC or 1099-NEC from the *same firm* for the *same services*, AND the amount on the 1099 should have been W-2 wages. Form SS-8 should NOT be filed for code H.

Codes A, C, G, H are exhaustive. Any other code is invalid and Form 8919 cannot be filed without one of the four.

### 1.4 What does NOT go on Form 8919

| Type of pay | Goes on | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Genuine independent-contractor pay (worker passes the §3121(d) test as a contractor) | Schedule C (gross receipts) + Schedule SE (self-employment tax) | Worker IS a contractor; the §3121(d) test confirms it. Form 8919 is for misclassification, not for legitimate contractor pay. |
| Unreported tips (worker received tips but did not report them to the employer) | Form 4137 → Form 1040 line 1c (tips) AND Schedule 2 line 6 (uncollected tax) | Separate IRS form for the tip-specific case; Form 4137 line 10 feeds Form 8919 line 8 but does not double-count on line 6. |
| W-2 box 12 codes M or N (uncollected Social Security and Medicare tax on group-term life insurance for former employees) | Schedule 2 line 13 directly (not Form 8919) | M and N are an employer-driven reporting code, not a worker-driven reclassification. |
| Wages already correctly reported as W-2 box 1 income | Form 1040 line 1a (wages from W-2 box 1) | Already wages; no reclassification needed. |
| Household-employer wages the worker received but the household-employer did not withhold | Form 1040 line 1b (household employee wages) | Different statutory path (Internal Revenue Code §3510 / Schedule H). The line 1b path handles household-employer Social Security + Medicare separately. |

### 1.5 The complete IRS equation (plain English)

For each spouse who has at least one qualifying firm row:

```text
Step A — Per-firm columns (a) through (f):

    For each firm row i (Form 8919 supports up to 5 firms per page;
    overflow goes to attached pages):
        column (a) firm name           — required
        column (b) firm EIN or SSN     — required; "unknown" acceptable
                                         if genuinely unknown
        column (c) reason code         — required; must be A, C, G, or H
        column (d) IRS date            — required only when code is A or C
        column (e) 1099 received       — yes / no; contextual for code H
        column (f) wagesNoFicaNotW2    — required; the dollar amount on
                                         which no employee Social Security
                                         + Medicare tax was withheld

Step B — Per-spouse aggregation (Form 8919 lines 6 through 13):

    line6 = sum of column (f) across all firm rows for this spouse
            (this is the amount that flows to Form 1040 line 1g)

    line7 = $176,100  (2025 Social Security wage base; constant for the
                       full tax year)

    line8 = W-2 box 3 (Social Security wages) attributed to this spouse
          + W-2 box 7 (Social Security tips) attributed to this spouse
          + Railroad Retirement Tax Act tier 1 compensation
            (W-2 box 14 RRTA) attributed to this spouse
          + Form 4137 line 10 (unreported tips subject to Social
                                Security tax) for this same spouse

    line9  = max(0, line7 − line8)
             (the remaining Social Security wage base this spouse
              still has available)

    line10 = min(line6, line9)
             (Form 8919 wages that fit under the remaining wage base)

    line11 = line10 × 6.2%
             (the employee Social Security tax owed on those wages)

    line12 = line6 × 1.45%
             (the employee Medicare tax on the full line6 — Medicare
              has NO wage-base cap)

    line13 = line11 + line12
             (total uncollected Social Security + Medicare tax for
              this spouse, going to Schedule 2 line 6)

Step C — Carry to Form 1040 and Schedule 2:

    Form 1040 line 1g     = taxpayer line6 + spouse line6   (MFJ)
                          = taxpayer line6                  (other)
    Schedule 2 line 6     = taxpayer line13 + spouse line13 (MFJ)
                          = taxpayer line13                 (other)

Step D — If Form 8959 (Additional Medicare Tax) applies:

    Form 8959 line 3 receives both spouses' line6 amounts
    (Form 8919 wages count toward Medicare wages for the 0.9%
     Additional Medicare Tax base — line 3 is one of the inputs to
     Form 8959 line 4 along with W-2 box 5 Medicare wages.)
```

### 1.6 Per-spouse multiplicity

Form 8919 is a per-spouse form, not a return-level form. Multiplicity:

- **Single, Head of Household, Qualifying Surviving Spouse, Married Filing Separately:** zero or one Form 8919 (taxpayer only).
- **Married Filing Jointly:** zero, one, or two Forms 8919 (taxpayer, spouse, or both). If both spouses qualify, each spouse completes a separate Form 8919; the two line 6 amounts sum into Form 1040 line 1g.

### 1.7 More than 5 firm rows

A single Form 8919 holds up to 5 firms. For a spouse with more than 5 firms:

- Attach additional Forms 8919 with rows 1–5 filled in for the extra firms.
- Compute lines 6–13 ONCE per spouse, on the main (first) Form 8919, using the COMBINED totals across all attached forms.
- The line 6 amount on the main form must equal the combined column (f) across all attached forms for that spouse.

### 1.8 No negative line 1g

Form 8919 line 6 is a sum of non-negative wage amounts. Line 1g cannot be negative. (This contrasts with Form 1040 line 1f, which CAN be negative under the foreign-finality or special-needs-off-W-2 rules of Internal Revenue Code §137.)

### 1.9 What does NOT go on Line 1g (cross-line distinctions)

See the table in Section 1.4. The three most common confusions:

1. **Pure 1099-NEC / 1099-MISC contractor pay** — that pay is reported on Schedule C as gross receipts, and self-employment tax is computed on Schedule SE. Form 8919 is ONLY for the case where the worker disputes the contractor classification AND can support that dispute with one of the four reason codes.
2. **W-2 box 12 codes M / N** — those go directly to Schedule 2 line 13 (uncollected tax on group-term life insurance for former employees). They are NOT reclassification wages; they are an employer-driven reporting code for a specific fringe-benefit scenario.
3. **Form 4137 unreported tips** — those go to Form 1040 line 1c (as tips), not line 1g. The Social-Security-taxable portion (Form 4137 line 10) feeds the Form 8919 line 8 wage-base calculation but never doubles into line 6.

### 1.10 Whole-dollar rounding

The Form 1040 instructions allow whole-dollar rounding. Form 8919 lines 6 and 13 are rounded once at the end before being carried to Form 1040 line 1g and Schedule 2 line 6 respectively. The intermediate per-firm column (f) values may be in cents; the application's behavior is to keep them in cents and round only at the per-spouse aggregation points.

## Part 2 — The code implementation

The backend Java code that calculates Line 1g (and the rest of Form 8919) lives in `TaxReturnComputeService.java`:

- **`computeUncollectedSSTax(...)`** — runs in the standard line-1z compute path. Reads the per-spouse `uncollected-ss-medicare-taxpayer` / `uncollected-ss-medicare-spouse` forms, the W-2 statements (filtered by spouse SSN), and the result of `computeTips()` (for Form 4137 line 10). Produces zero, one, or two `Form8919` output objects and the aggregate line 1g + Schedule 2 line 6 amounts.

The method runs AFTER `computeTips()` (which produces Form 4137 line 10) so that Form 8919 line 8 has the unreported-tips component available. This execution order is enforced by the orchestrator in `prepare(...)`.

### 2.3 Reference data (constants that may change year-to-year)

| Reference constant | 2025 value | Where it lives | Where it is used |
|---|---|---|---|
| Social Security wage base | $176,100 | `ReferenceData.SOCIAL_SECURITY_WAGE_BASE` | Form 8919 line 7 (per-spouse) — the maximum amount of wages subject to the 6.2% Social Security tax in 2025 |
| Social Security tax rate | 6.2% | inline `new BigDecimal("0.062")` in `computeUncollectedSSTax` | Form 8919 line 11 multiplier — set by Internal Revenue Code §3101(a); does not change year-to-year |
| Medicare tax rate | 1.45% | inline `new BigDecimal("0.0145")` in `computeUncollectedSSTax` | Form 8919 line 12 multiplier — set by Internal Revenue Code §3101(b)(1); does not change year-to-year |

The 2024 wage base was $168,600. The 2026 wage base will be published by the Social Security Administration in October 2025 (Revenue Procedure 2025-32 is expected to confirm it for tax purposes). The 6.2% / 1.45% rates are statutory and have not changed since the 1980s; they are inline literals in the code because there is no expected year-to-year drift.

### 2.4 The code equation (Line 1g portion)

In plain English, the code does this (matching the IRS rule from Section 1.5):

```text
Step 0 — Decide whether either spouse has Form 8919 inputs.

    tipComputation = result of computeTips(...) — already finished.

    taxpayerData = saved form-uncollected-ss-medicare-taxpayer
                   (firms[], reason codes, wages)
    spouseData   = saved form-uncollected-ss-medicare-spouse
                   (null on MFS, or when the spouse form was not saved)

    On Married Filing Separately, spouseData is forced to null at the
    method entry (the spouse files separately; their Form 8919 belongs
    on their own return).

    If taxpayerData has no firms AND spouseData has no firms:
        Return UncollectedComputation(line1g = null, schedule2Line6 = null,
                                       form8919Taxpayer = null,
                                       form8919Spouse = null).

Step 1 — Validate per-firm inputs.

    For each firm row on each spouse's form:
        - If reasonCode is not in {A, C, G, H}:
            Emit BLOCKING flag FORM8919_REASON_CODE_INVALID_TAXPAYER
            (or _SPOUSE).
        - If reasonCode is A or C AND irsDeterminationDate is missing:
            Emit BLOCKING flag FORM8919_IRS_DATE_REQUIRED_TAXPAYER
            (or _SPOUSE).
        - If reasonCode is G:
            Emit non-blocking advisory FORM8919_SS8_REQUIRED_TAXPAYER
            (or _SPOUSE) — reminder that Form SS-8 must be filed
            separately with the IRS (not attached to Form 1040).
        - If reasonCode is H AND received1099MiscOrNec is false:
            Emit non-blocking advisory FORM8919_CODE_H_1099_TAXPAYER
            (or _SPOUSE) — soft warning that code H normally pairs
            with receiving both a W-2 and a 1099 from the same firm.
        - If firmFederalIdNumber is missing or blank (and the firm row
          is otherwise present):
            Emit BLOCKING flag MISSING_FIRM_FEDERAL_ID_FORM_8919_TAXPAYER
            (or _SPOUSE) — IRS e-file rejects Form 8919 without a firm
            identifier; the literal word "unknown" is acceptable.

Step 2 — Per-spouse aggregation (Form 8919 lines 6 through 13).

    For each spouse with firms (taxpayer, then spouse on MFJ):

        line6 = sum of firms[].wagesNoFicaNotW2

        line7 = ReferenceData.SOCIAL_SECURITY_WAGE_BASE      ($176,100)

        Read all W-2 statements whose employeeSSN matches THIS spouse:
            ssWagesFromW2 = sum of socialSecurityWagesAmount (box 3)
            ssTipsFromW2  = sum of socialSecurityTipsAmount  (box 7)
            rrtaFromW2    = sum of box14RrtaCompensation
                            (Railroad Retirement Tax Act tier 1)

        unreportedSsTips = tipComputation.form4137{Taxpayer|Spouse}
                              .line10SocialSecurityTaxableTips
                           (0 when no Form 4137 for this spouse)

        line8 = ssWagesFromW2 + ssTipsFromW2 + rrtaFromW2 + unreportedSsTips

        line9  = max(0, line7 - line8)
        line10 = min(line6, line9)
        line11 = line10 × 0.062            (Social Security tax)
        line12 = line6  × 0.0145           (Medicare tax — no cap)
        line13 = line11 + line12

        Build a Form8919 output object:
            header        = spouse's name + SSN
            firms         = all firm rows
            line6 through line13 as above
        Round each output line to whole dollars at this point.

Step 3 — Return-level aggregation.

    Form 1040 line 1g    = addNonNull(taxpayer.line6, spouse.line6)
    Schedule 2 line 6    = addNonNull(taxpayer.line13, spouse.line13)
    form1040.income.uncollectedSocialSecurityMedicareWages = line1g
    schedule2.otherTaxes.uncollectedSocialSecurityMedicareTaxOnWages = line6

    Return UncollectedComputation(line1g, schedule2Line6,
                                   form8919Taxpayer, form8919Spouse).

Step 4 — Downstream (after this method returns).

    If Form 8959 (Additional Medicare Tax) is required, the orchestrator
    adds the per-spouse line 6 amounts to Form 8959 line 3
    (uncollectedWages contribution to the Medicare-wages base).

    The line 1b household-employee path skips employers whose row is
    flagged reportedOnForm8919 = true to prevent the same wages from
    counting on both line 1b and line 1g (the line 1b collision guard).
```

### 2.6 Married Filing Separately guard

On a Married Filing Separately return, the code:

- Reads only the taxpayer's `uncollected-ss-medicare-taxpayer` form. The spouse's `uncollected-ss-medicare-spouse` form is ignored at method entry (the spouse files separately; that form belongs on the spouse's own return).
- Filters W-2s for line 8 by the taxpayer's SSN only; spouse-owned W-2s do not contribute to the taxpayer's line 8.
- Reads only the taxpayer's `tipComputation.form4137Taxpayer.line10` for unreported tips; the spouse's Form 4137 is ignored.
- Returns at most one `Form8919` output object (the taxpayer's).

This matches the per-return scope under Internal Revenue Code §6013 (joint return election) — when a couple elects to file separately, each spouse's wages stay on their own return.

### 2.7 Execution order: `computeTips()` must run first

Form 8919 line 8 includes `form4137.line10` (unreported tips subject to Social Security tax). `line10` is computed by `computeTips(...)` from each spouse's `unreported-tip-income-{taxpayer,spouse}` form.

The orchestrator in `prepare(...)` enforces the order:

```text
1. computeTips(...)              → produces tipComputation with form4137{Taxpayer,Spouse}
2. computeUncollectedSSTax(tipComputation, ...) → reads form4137.line10 for Form 8919 line 8
```

Reversing this order would silently miscompute line 8 (it would always read line10 = 0 because tips have not been computed yet) and inflate line 9, which would in turn over-charge Social Security tax on line 11.

### 2.8 Cross-form references

The code makes three cross-form contributions:

1. **Form 1040 line 1g.** `form1040.income.uncollectedSocialSecurityMedicareWages = taxpayer.line6 + spouse.line6` (null on a single-spouse-no-firms return).
2. **Schedule 2 line 6.** `schedule2.otherTaxes.uncollectedSocialSecurityMedicareTaxOnWages = taxpayer.line13 + spouse.line13` (the uncollected tax stacks on Schedule 2 line 6, then into Schedule 2 total → Form 1040 line 17).
3. **Form 8959 line 3.** When Form 8959 (Additional Medicare Tax) applies, each spouse's `line6` adds to `form8959.line3UncollectedWages`. This is what makes Form 8919 wages count toward the Additional Medicare Tax base. The orchestrator handles this contribution after both `computeUncollectedSSTax(...)` and the Form 8959 trigger are evaluated.

The code also enforces the **line 1b collision guard**: the line 1b household-employee path checks each employer row for `reportedOnForm8919 = true` and skips it when set, preventing the same wages from counting on both line 1b and line 1g.

## Part 3 — Comparison between the IRS rule and the code

Reading the two parts side-by-side, the code follows the IRS rule on every point that affects the dollar amount on Line 1g.

| IRS requirement | Code behavior | Faithful? |
|---|---|---|
| `line6 = sum of column (f) wages` per spouse | `line6 = sum of firms[].wagesNoFicaNotW2` per spouse | Yes |
| `line7 = $176,100` (2025 Social Security wage base) | `line7 = ReferenceData.SOCIAL_SECURITY_WAGE_BASE` | Yes |
| `line8 = W-2 box 3 + W-2 box 7 + RRTA tier 1 + Form 4137 line 10` | SSN-filtered W-2 reads + `tipComputation.form4137{Taxpayer,Spouse}.line10` | Yes |
| `line9 = max(0, line7 - line8)` | `maxZero(line7.subtract(line8))` | Yes |
| `line10 = min(line6, line9)` | `line6.min(line9)` | Yes |
| `line11 = line10 × 6.2%` | `line10.multiply(new BigDecimal("0.062"))` | Yes |
| `line12 = line6 × 1.45%` (no wage-base cap) | `line6.multiply(new BigDecimal("0.0145"))` (no cap) | Yes |
| `line13 = line11 + line12` | `line11.add(line12)` | Yes |
| Form 1040 line 1g = sum of both spouses' line 6 | `addNonNull(taxpayerLine6, spouseLine6)` | Yes |
| Schedule 2 line 6 = sum of both spouses' line 13 | `addNonNull(taxpayerLine13, spouseLine13)` | Yes |
| Form 8959 line 3 += each spouse's Form 8919 line 6 (when Form 8959 applies) | `form8959.line3UncollectedWages += line6` per spouse, done after `computeUncollectedSSTax(...)` returns | Yes |
| Reason code must be A, C, G, or H | Per-firm validator emits `FORM8919_REASON_CODE_INVALID_*` blocking flag otherwise | Yes |
| IRS date required when code A or C | Per-firm validator emits `FORM8919_IRS_DATE_REQUIRED_*` blocking flag otherwise | Yes |
| Married Filing Separately: spouse's Form 8919 belongs on spouse's own return, not taxpayer's | Spouse form is forced to null at method entry on MFS; spouse W-2s excluded from taxpayer line 8 | Yes |
| Whole-dollar rounding on lines 6 and 13 before carrying to Form 1040 line 1g / Schedule 2 line 6 | `roundMoney(line6)` / `roundMoney(line13)` at each per-spouse output point | Yes |
| Line 1b household-employee path must not double-count wages already on Form 8919 | Line 1b skips employers flagged `reportedOnForm8919 = true` (collision guard) | Yes |
| Form SS-8 advisory for reason code G | Non-blocking `FORM8919_SS8_REQUIRED_*` flag emitted; Form SS-8 itself NOT generated (separate filing) | Yes |

### 3.1 Items the code does NOT support (intentional out-of-scope decisions)

The following are documented as out of scope and not bugs:

1. **Form SS-8 generation.** Reason code G means the worker has Form SS-8 pending with the IRS. Form SS-8 itself is filed separately (not attached to Form 1040). The application emits the `FORM8919_SS8_REQUIRED_*` non-blocking advisory but does not generate the SS-8 form. The user files SS-8 directly with the IRS.
2. **Multi-page Form 8919 PDF (more than 5 firms).** The 2025 IRS instructions allow attaching extra Forms 8919 with rows 1–5 only for the overflow. The application's frontend `buildMultiPagePdf()` handles the chunking at render time; the backend processes all firms regardless of how many. There is no separate output object for the "main" form versus "overflow" forms — the application produces one `Form8919` per spouse with the full firm list and lets the frontend page-split.
3. **Independent §3121(d) employee-vs-contractor verification.** The application does not run the common-law tests (behavioral control, financial control, type of relationship) to confirm the worker was genuinely an employee. The worker's reason code (A, C, G, or H) is the supporting evidence; codes A and C require an IRS document (date + presumably letter on file); codes G and H rest on the worker's representation.
4. **§3509 employer-side rate consideration.** Section §3509 lets the IRS use reduced employer-side rates when assessing the firm post-misclassification. This applies to the firm, not the worker. The worker's Form 8919 self-assessment always uses the full 6.2% + 1.45% on the wages. The application implements only the worker's path.

### 3.2 Items where the code is stricter than the IRS rule (defensive measures)

1. **Per-W-2 SSN filter on line 8.** When multiple W-2s are present, only W-2s whose `employeeSSN` matches each spouse's SSN feed that spouse's Form 8919 line 8. This prevents a stale spouse W-2 from inflating the taxpayer's line 8 (and thus shrinking line 9 / understating Social Security tax). On Married Filing Separately, the spouse SSN is null and only the taxpayer's W-2s are read.
2. **`MISSING_FIRM_FEDERAL_ID_FORM_8919_*` blocking flag.** The IRS form allows the literal word "unknown" in the firm identifier column when the worker genuinely does not know the firm's EIN — but a blank field would be rejected at e-file time. The application enforces this at compute time so the return is e-file-ready when it leaves compute. The UI shows an advisory hint next to the field but never refuses save (save-vs-compute principle).
3. **Line 1b collision guard.** When a worker has both a household-employer row (line 1b path) and a Form 8919 firm row for the same wages, the line 1b path checks the `reportedOnForm8919 = true` flag and skips the row to prevent double-counting. This protects against a structural error the IRS would catch at document-matching.
4. **Per-firm validation at compute time.** Reason code in {A, C, G, H} and IRS date for codes A/C are both enforced as BLOCKING flags at compute time. The IRS form itself does not technically refuse to print without these (it would just be incomplete), but the application refuses to finalize the return to prevent the user filing a Form 8919 the IRS would correspond about.

### 3.3 Items captured in the input form but not used in the Line 1g math

| Field | What it does today | Why it is captured anyway |
|---|---|---|
| `firms[].received1099MiscOrNec` | Stored on the Form 8919 output for audit-trail purposes; drives the `FORM8919_CODE_H_1099_*` advisory | Required for Form 8919 column (e) display; informational for code H |
| `firms[].irsDeterminationDate` (when reason code is G or H) | Stored but not required | Column (d) is blank for codes G/H on the IRS form; the input field is present so the user can enter a date if they have one for personal records |
| `firms[].firmFederalIdNumber` literal "unknown" | Stored as-is | Allowed by IRS instructions when the firm's EIN is genuinely unknown; satisfies the `MISSING_FIRM_FEDERAL_ID_FORM_8919_*` validator |

These are honest, intentional non-uses for line 1g — not bugs.

### 3.4 Gap closures and remaining constraints

**For Line 1g itself, the implementation is complete.** No implementation gaps have been documented during the line audit. The code path is well-bounded and the four IRS reason codes are fully enumerated.

**2026-05-31 §17 promotion of `FORM8919_CODE_H_1099_*`.** The reason-code-H + no-1099 validator was promoted from non-blocking soft warning to **blocking + rules.md §17 non-overrideable**. Per the 2025 Instructions for Form 8919, code H applies ONLY when the worker received both a W-2 and a 1099-MISC/1099-NEC from the same firm for the same services — the code is structurally about reclassifying part of the 1099 amount as W-2 wages. Without a 1099 there is nothing to reclassify; filing as-is invites near-certain IRS document-matching correspondence (typically a CP-2057 letter). The promotion was made because no legitimate scenario produces code H + no 1099 — the worker always has a correct alternative (switch to code G if Form SS-8 is pending, A if there's an IRS determination letter, C if there's other IRS correspondence, or delete the firm row if none applies). See `NonOverrideableFlags.java` for the entry and rationale; e2e test 11 in `line1g-uncollected-ss-medicare.spec.ts` verifies the 409-with-flag pattern with and without `overrideFlags=true`.

**Documentation gaps:** `f8919--2025.pdf` (Form 8919 itself) and `i8919--2025.pdf` (Instructions for Form 8919) are NOT yet bundled in `C:\us-tax\docs\books\`. The line 1g spec relies on `i1040gi_2025.pdf` (Form 1040 instructions; sets the label), `p17.pdf` (lay-friendly summary), and the line spec at `C:\us-tax\lines\1g.md` for the form-line-level rule mapping. Acquiring `f8919` and `i8919` locally would close the documentation chain — same shape as the `f8839` / `i8839` documentation gap for line 1f and the `f2441` / `i2441` documentation gap for line 1e (now closed).

**Remaining constraints** (documented as out-of-scope in section 3.1, not as bugs):

- **No independent §3121(d) employee-vs-contractor verification** — the worker's reason code is the source of truth.
- **Form SS-8 not generated** — filed separately by the worker.
- **§3509 employer-side rate not modeled** — applies to the firm, not the worker.
- **Multi-page Form 8919 PDF** — handled at render time by the frontend, not the backend.

**Cross-line / cross-form consistency**:

- Line 1g feeds line 1z; line 1z feeds Form 1040 line 9 (total income).
- Schedule 2 line 6 feeds Schedule 2 total; Schedule 2 total feeds Form 1040 line 17 (other taxes).
- Form 8959 line 3 feeds the Additional Medicare Tax base; Form 8959's final tax → Schedule 2 line 11 → Form 1040 line 17.

## Part 4 — Summary

**Form 1040 Line 1g = the wages reported on Form 8919, line 6 — wages from firms that misclassified the worker as an independent contractor and did not withhold the employee's share of Social Security and Medicare tax. Each spouse has their own Form 8919 (per-spouse multiplicity); Form 1040 line 1g sums their line 6 amounts. Each firm row carries one of four IRS reason codes (A = IRS determination letter; C = other IRS correspondence; G = Form SS-8 pending; H = received both W-2 and 1099 from same firm for same services). The companion Form 8919 lines 7 through 13 compute the uncollected Social Security and Medicare tax: line 7 is the 2025 Social Security wage base of $176,100; line 8 sums W-2 boxes 3 + 7, Railroad Retirement Tax Act tier 1 compensation, and Form 4137 line 10 (unreported tips subject to Social Security tax); line 9 is the remaining wage base; line 10 is the Form 8919 wages that fit under it; line 11 is line 10 × 6.2%; line 12 is line 6 × 1.45% (Medicare has no wage-base cap); line 13 is the total uncollected tax that flows to Schedule 2 line 6. When Form 8959 (Additional Medicare Tax) applies, the same line 6 wages also count toward Form 8959 line 3. The application produces zero, one, or two `Form8919` output objects per return.**

The application enforces all five IRS-required validations as blocking flags at compute time: reason code in {A, C, G, H}, IRS date for codes A/C, firm federal identifier present (or the literal word "unknown"), and two cross-line guards (Married Filing Separately spouse exclusion, line 1b household-employee collision guard). Three advisories provide soft reminders for reason code G (file Form SS-8 separately), reason code H without a 1099 (uncommon pairing), and the Married Filing Separately scope. Line 1g is **never negative** — it is a sum of non-negative wage amounts.
