# Form 1040 — Line 1z — Total wages (sum of lines 1a through 1h)

**Tax year: 2025**

This document explains how the dollar amount that goes on **Line 1z** of Form 1040 is calculated. Line 1z is the *wage subtotal* on Form 1040 / 1040-SR — the sum of every wage-style sub-line above it (lines 1a through 1h). It does **not** include line 1i (Nontaxable combat pay election), which is an election marker used downstream by two credit computations (Earned Income Credit on line 27a and Schedule 8812's Additional Child Tax Credit), not a taxable wage figure.

The document is organized in three parts:

1. **The IRS rule.** What the controlling IRS publications say must happen. This is the source of truth.
2. **The code implementation.** What the application's backend Java code currently does.
3. **The comparison.** A side-by-side check showing whether the code follows the IRS rule honestly, and where the two diverge.

## Part 1 — The IRS rule (the source of truth)

### 1.1 Authority

The IRS publications used to write this document are in `C:\us-tax\docs\books\`:

- **2025 Instructions for Form 1040 / 1040-SR** (`i1040gi_2025.pdf`) — the controlling rule for the Line 1z label, the lines included, and the downstream feed to line 9.
- **Publication 17 — Your Federal Income Tax** (`p17.pdf`) — Chapter 5 (Wages, Salaries, and Other Earnings) covers what counts as wages and what does not.

- **Publication 525 — Taxable and Nontaxable Income** — defines the taxability of various sub-line components.

### 1.2 The basic rule

From the 2025 Instructions for Form 1040, Line 1z:

> **Line 1z — Add lines 1a through 1h.**

That is the entire IRS rule. Line 1z is a pure arithmetic subtotal of the eight sub-lines above it.

In plain English: Line 1z is the **total wages** the taxpayer (and the spouse on a joint return) must report on Form 1040. It is later used to compute Total income (Form 1040 line 9), which feeds Adjusted gross income (line 11) and Taxable income (line 15).

### 1.4 What is excluded from Line 1z

#### Exclusion A — Line 1i (Nontaxable combat pay election)

Line 1i sits below the wage sub-lines but is **explicitly outside Line 1z**. The IRS labels Line 1i as the "Nontaxable combat pay election" — combat pay that is NOT subject to income tax but that the taxpayer may *elect* to include in earned income solely for the Earned Income Credit and the Additional Child Tax Credit. Including it in Line 1z would make the pay appear taxable, which is wrong.

Mechanically, Line 1i is consumed in two downstream places:

1. **Earned Income Credit on Form 1040 line 27a** — Line 1i is added to the EIC earned-income base when the election is in effect, per Internal Revenue Code §32(c)(2)(B)(vi).
2. **Schedule 8812 line 18a (Earned income for Additional Child Tax Credit)** — Line 1i is added to the ACTC earned-income base when the election is in effect, per Internal Revenue Code §24(d)(1)(B).

Neither of those downstream uses changes Line 1z itself.

See `C:\us-tax\XLS\Computations\1i.md` for the full Line 1i specification.

#### Exclusion B — Lines 2 through 8 (later income lines)

The Form 1040 income lines BELOW Line 1z — interest (lines 2a / 2b), dividends (lines 3a / 3b), Individual Retirement Arrangement distributions (lines 4a / 4b), pension and annuity (lines 5a / 5b), Social Security benefits (lines 6a / 6b / 6c), capital gain or loss (lines 7a / 7b), and Schedule 1 additional income (line 8) — are NOT wages. They are added separately on Line 9.

### 1.5 Negative amounts

Line 1z is a straight algebraic sum of its sub-lines. No floor at zero applies at Line 1z.

The one sub-line that can be legitimately negative under current IRS rules is **Line 1f** (employer-provided adoption benefits). When the taxpayer adopts a child whom a state has determined to be a child with special needs, Internal Revenue Code §137(a)(3) lets the employer-provided adoption-benefit exclusion exceed the actual expenses, which can drive the Form 8839 line 29 result below zero. The IRS instructions for Form 8839 explicitly accommodate this.

When Line 1f is negative, Line 1z is reduced by that amount and may itself be negative. That negative amount flows on to Line 9 (Total income) and then to Line 11 (Adjusted gross income). The floor at zero appears only on **Line 15** (Taxable income), per the standard Form 1040 instruction *"if zero or less, enter -0-"*.

### 1.6 Null handling on a tax return with no wages

If every sub-line 1a through 1h is missing (the filer has no wages at all — for example, a return with only Social Security benefits), Line 1z should remain blank rather than printing as zero. Printing zero on a blank line is acceptable but not required, and the application's convention is to leave the slot empty when no upstream sub-line has been computed.

There is one application-specific carve-out: if the taxpayer answered "Yes" to the employment-income question (so the application expects a Form W-2) but no W-2 has been entered yet, the application stores Line 1z as 0 (rather than null) so the downstream invariants on Form 1040 line 9 hold. This is a UI consistency choice, not an IRS requirement.

### 1.7 Whole-dollar rounding

The IRS allows whole-dollar rounding on Form 1040 as long as it is applied consistently across the return. The recommended practice is to keep cents during the per-sub-line computations (1a through 1h) and to round to whole dollars only when printing the final return. The Line 1z sum then uses the already-rounded sub-line values.

### 1.8 The complete IRS equation (plain English)

Putting the rule together:

```text
For each of the eight wage sub-lines (1a, 1b, 1c, 1d, 1e, 1f, 1g, 1h):
    take the value already computed for that sub-line
    (treat a missing/blank sub-line as zero for the sum)

Line 1z = sum of those eight values

DO NOT include Line 1i (Nontaxable combat pay election) in the sum.
DO allow the sum to be negative if Line 1f is negative.
```

### 1.9 Reference data (constants that may change year to year)

**Line 1z has no tax-year-specific numeric constant.** No rate, no threshold, no limit, no inflation-adjusted figure appears inside the Line 1z computation. Line 1z is a pure summation.

All numeric reference data lives in the upstream sub-line computations (`1a` through `1h`). For example:

| Reference data point | Lives in | Tax-year sensitivity |
|---|---|---|
| W-2 box 11 Nonqualified plans carve-out for Line 1a | `1a.md` §1.4 | No rate; rule unchanged across years |
| Household employee W-2 reporting threshold (relevant only to Line 1b eligibility, not to the Line 1z sum) | `1b.md` | $2,800 for tax year 2025; updated annually by Revenue Procedure |
| Tip-reporting threshold of $20 per month per employer (relevant only to Line 1c) | `1c.md` | Set by Internal Revenue Code §6053(a); unchanged |
| Excludable employer-provided adoption benefits cap (relevant only to Line 1f via Form 8839) | `1f.md` | $17,280 for tax year 2025; updated annually under Internal Revenue Code §137(b) |
| Annual Social Security taxable wage base (relevant only to Line 1g via Form 8919) | `1g.md` | $176,100 for tax year 2025; updated annually by the Social Security Administration |

None of these constants enter the Line 1z formula directly. Changes to them affect the sub-line values, which Line 1z then sums.

### 2.4 The code equation (step by step)

```text
1. Read each of the eight upstream sub-line values
   from the local variables computed earlier in buildIncome:
       line1a, line1b, line1c, line1d, line1e, line1f, line1g, line1h
   Each variable is a java.math.BigDecimal or null.

2. Sum them using a null-tolerant helper called addNonNull:
       addNonNull(a, b) returns:
           a + b      when both a and b are non-null
           a          when only b is null
           b          when only a is null
           null       when both are null
   The eight values are folded left-to-right by addNonNull
   into a single BigDecimal called totalWagesRaw.

3. Round to whole dollars:
       line1z = roundMoney(totalWagesRaw)
   where roundMoney rounds to the nearest dollar
   (with half-up rounding) and returns null if its input is null.

4. Store the result on the Income output model:
       if line1z is not null:
           income.setTotalWages(line1z)
       else if the application's "this filer should have wages but
              none arrived" flag is true (see §1.6):
           income.setTotalWages(BigDecimal.ZERO)
       otherwise:
           leave income.totalWages unset (null)

5. Line 1i (Nontaxable combat pay election) is stored on a separate
   field (income.nontaxableCombatPayElection) and is NOT folded
   into totalWagesRaw at any step.
```

The exact source is at `TaxReturnComputeService.java:6215-6217` (the sum) and `:6350-6354` (the storage), preserved verbatim:

```java
    line1d), line1e), line1f), line1g), line1h);

if (line1z != null) {
    income.setTotalWages(line1z);

```

### 2.5 The internal worksheet recomputation site (not user-visible)

A SECOND piece of code at `TaxReturnComputeService.java:11027-11032` recomputes Line 1z **locally** for use inside the Publication 915 (Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits) Worksheet 3 lump-sum election computation. That site folds the eight sub-line components into a worksheet-only local variable; it does NOT write back to `income.totalWages`. ### 3.1 Side-by-side check

### 3.4 Missing fields

### 3.5 Summary

For tax year 2025, the **Line 1z compute is faithful to the IRS rule**. The amount written to Form 1040 Line 1z for any combination of inputs (eight sub-line values) matches what the 2025 IRS Instructions for Form 1040 would produce. ## Glossary

- **Form 1040** — the standard United States individual federal income tax return.
- **Form 1040-SR** — a variant of Form 1040 designed for taxpayers age 65 or older; the line numbers and computations are identical to Form 1040.
- **Form W-2** (Wage and Tax Statement) — the year-end pay summary an employer sends to each employee and to the Social Security Administration.
- **Form 2441** (Child and Dependent Care Expenses) — the form that figures the credit for child and dependent care expenses and the taxable portion of employer-provided dependent care benefits.
- **Form 4137** (Social Security and Medicare Tax on Unreported Tip Income) — the form that computes the Social Security and Medicare tax on cash tips not reported to the employer.
- **Form 4852** (Substitute for Form W-2, Wage and Tax Statement) — the form a taxpayer files when the actual Form W-2 is missing or wrong.
- **Form 8839** (Qualified Adoption Expenses) — the form that figures the employer-provided-adoption-benefits exclusion and the adoption credit.
- **Form 8919** (Uncollected Social Security and Medicare Tax on Wages) — the form an employee files when work was misclassified as independent contracting.
- **Schedule 1** (Additional Income and Adjustments to Income) — the supplementary schedule for income types and adjustments that do not have a dedicated Form 1040 line.
- **Schedule 8812** (Credits for Qualifying Children and Other Dependents) — the schedule that computes the Child Tax Credit, the Credit for Other Dependents, and the refundable Additional Child Tax Credit.
- **Earned Income Credit** — a refundable federal income tax credit for low- and moderate-income working taxpayers, computed on Form 1040 line 27a using earned income.
- **Additional Child Tax Credit** — the refundable portion of the Child Tax Credit, computed on Schedule 8812 Part II-A using earned income.
- **Adjusted gross income** — Form 1040 line 11; total income (line 9) minus the adjustments on Schedule 1 Part II.
- **Taxable income** — Form 1040 line 15; adjusted gross income (line 11) minus the standard or itemized deduction (line 12) and any qualified business income deduction (line 13).
- **Internal Revenue Code** — Title 26 of the United States Code; the statute defining federal tax law.
- **Publication 17 — Your Federal Income Tax** — the IRS's master guide for individuals filing Form 1040.
- **Publication 525 — Taxable and Nontaxable Income** — the IRS publication that defines what kinds of income are taxable and what kinds are not.
- **Publication 915 — Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits** — the IRS publication containing the lump-sum-election worksheet referenced in §2.5.
- **Notice 2014-7** — IRS guidance allowing certain Medicaid waiver payments to be excluded from gross income (relevant to Line 1d).
