# Form 1040 (2025) — Line 26: 2025 estimated tax payments and amount applied from 2024 return

> Plain-English specification of the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) canonical computation for Form 1040 line 26, followed by the as-implemented computation in this codebase, with a gap analysis at the end.
>
> Tax year: **2025**.
>
> Sources consulted: 2025 Form 1040; the 2025 Instructions for Form 1040 (`C:\us-tax\docs\books\i1040gi_2025.txt`); the 2025 Form 1040-ES (Estimated Tax for Individuals); Publication 17 (`C:\us-tax\docs\books\p17.pdf`); Publication 505 (Tax Withholding and Estimated Tax); plus the project's own `lines\26.md`, `dependencies\26.md`, and `knowledge\line-26-estimated-tax-payments.md`.

---

## Quick orientation

The Form 1040 label for line 26 is:

> **26. 2025 estimated tax payments and amount applied from 2024 return**

Line 26 is part of the payments section of Form 1040. It is summed with line 25d (total federal income tax withheld) and line 32 (total other payments and refundable credits) to produce line 33 (total payments), which the IRS compares against line 24 (total tax) to determine whether the taxpayer owes more (line 37) or gets a refund (line 34).

Line 26 captures **federal income tax the taxpayer paid voluntarily in advance** during the 2025 tax year (or that was carried forward from the prior year's overpayment), as distinct from:
- Withholding the payer pulled from the taxpayer's pay (line 25a / 25b / 25c).
- Refundable credits the IRS pays back (line 27a, 28, 29, 30, 31 → line 32).
- Balance due paid at filing time (a check sent in with Form 1040 — not creditable on line 26).
- Payments made with Form 4868 (extension to file — captured later through Schedule 3 line 10 → line 31, not on line 26).

Three things to keep in mind throughout:

1. Line 26 has **no rate**, **no threshold**, and **no formula** beyond "add up every payment the taxpayer made for 2025 estimated tax, plus any carryforward credit from the 2024 return."
2. On a joint return, **both spouses' estimated payments combine on line 26**. The implementation reads both the taxpayer and spouse personal forms on Married filing jointly and aggregates the amounts.
3. The IRS allows payments on any date, in any amount, by any of several methods (Form 1040-ES voucher, Electronic Federal Tax Payment System, IRS Direct Pay, IRS-authorized card processor). The line-26 aggregation step does not care about the payment method or date — it cares only about the total.

## 1. The IRS-canonical computation (what the form really says to do)

### 1a. Equation for line 26

```
Form 1040 line 26 = sum of every 2025 estimated federal income tax payment the taxpayer actually made
                   + amount of 2024 overpayment from the original 2024 return that was applied to 2025 estimated tax
                   + amount of 2024 overpayment from an amended 2024 return (Form 1040-X) that was applied to 2025 estimated tax
```

### 1b. Word version

Find every federal estimated-tax payment the taxpayer made during the 2025 tax year (which runs from January 1, 2025 to roughly January 15, 2026 for the final quarterly installment). These payments may have been made via:

- A Form 1040-ES paper voucher mailed to the IRS with a check.
- The Electronic Federal Tax Payment System (EFTPS).
- IRS Direct Pay (electronic checking/savings account debit through the IRS website).
- A credit or debit card via an IRS-authorized processor.

Add up the dollar amounts of every such payment.

Then, if on the original 2024 return the taxpayer elected to apply some or all of the 2024 overpayment to 2025 estimated tax (the "apply to next year's estimated tax" box on the 2024 Form 1040 line 36), add that amount.

Finally, if the taxpayer later filed Form 1040-X to amend the 2024 return AND on that amended return elected to apply additional overpayment to 2025 estimated tax, add that amount too.

The total is Form 1040 line 26.

### 1d. Reference data (2025 constants that drive line 26)

**None.** Line 26 has no tax-year-dependent constant — no rate, no threshold, no inflation-indexed amount. The taxpayer simply records each payment they made, and the IRS sums them on the return.

For context (constants that affect WHEN the payments were due, NOT the line-26 sum itself):

| Constant | Value (2025) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 due date | April 15, 2025 | 2025 Form 1040-ES, IRS calendar |
| Q2 due date | **June 16, 2025** (June 15 is a Sunday) | 2025 Form 1040-ES |
| Q3 due date | September 15, 2025 | 2025 Form 1040-ES |
| Q4 due date | January 15, 2026 | 2025 Form 1040-ES |
| January payment exception | If the taxpayer files the 2025 return and pays the balance by **February 2, 2026** (January 31 is Saturday → February 1 is Sunday → February 2 is Monday), the Q4 installment is not required | 2025 Form 1040-ES |

The due dates matter for Form 2210 (Underpayment of Estimated Tax) penalty analysis. They do NOT change the line-26 arithmetic — line 26 is the total of all creditable 2025 estimated payments regardless of when they were paid.

### 1f. Filing-status interaction

- **Single, Head of household, Qualifying surviving spouse:** Sum only the filer's own payments (there is no spouse on the return).
- **Married filing jointly:** Sum BOTH the taxpayer's and the spouse's estimated payments. Per Publication 505, if both spouses made separate estimated payments during 2025 but file a joint 2025 return, all such payments combine on line 26 of the joint return.
- **Married filing separately:** Sum only the filer's own payments. If the spouses paid joint estimated tax but file separate 2025 returns, they may divide the payments any way they agree; if they cannot agree, the payments must be divided in proportion to each spouse's individual tax as shown on the separate 2025 returns (Publication 505 allocation rule). The implementation does NOT auto-allocate — it reads only the taxpayer form on Married filing separately.

### 1g. Divorced-taxpayer rule (2025 Form 1040 supplemental annotation)

If the taxpayer made joint estimated-tax payments with a former spouse during 2025, the 2025 Form 1040 line 26 provides a supplemental space to enter the former spouse's Social Security Number. This is an **annotation field** — it does NOT change the line-26 sum. The actual allocation between the two former spouses is handled per the Publication 505 rules (taxpayer agreement, or proportional-to-tax allocation).

The implementation captures the former-spouse Social Security Number via the `divorceFormerSpouseSSN` field on `estimated-tax-payments-taxpayer` but does NOT include it in any arithmetic. It is preserved for the PDF render and for auditability.

## 2. The computation as actually implemented in the code

> File: `C:\us-tax\us-tax-be\src\main\java\com\ustax\microservices\TaxReturnComputeService.java`

The implementation is concentrated inside one method (`computeLine26EstimatedTax`) and one composer call site (in `computeLine31ThroughLine38`).

### 2a. Helper — `computeLine26EstimatedTax(taxpayerForm, spouseForm, isMfs)` (line 26973)

```
input  taxpayerForm = the estimated-tax-payments-taxpayer personal form payload, or null when never saved
input  spouseForm   = the estimated-tax-payments-spouse personal form payload, or null when never saved
input  isMfs        = true on Married filing separately, false otherwise
output total        = the rounded sum of positive payment amounts, or null when no payments

read taxpayerMade = taxpayerForm is not null ? taxpayerForm.madeEstimatedTaxPayments : null
read spouseMade   = NOT isMfs AND spouseForm is not null AND spouseForm.madeEstimatedTaxPayments == TRUE

# Screening gate: at least one form must indicate payments were made.
if taxpayerMade is not TRUE AND spouseMade is false:
    return null

# Six amount fields aggregated per form (in this order):
fields = [
    installment1Amount,
    installment2Amount,
    installment3Amount,
    installment4Amount,
    priorYearOverpaymentCredited,
    amendedReturnOverpaymentCredited
]

total = null

if taxpayerMade is TRUE:
    for each field in fields:
        v = parseAmount(taxpayerForm[field])
        if v is not null AND v > 0:
            total = addNonNull(total, v)

# On non-MFS (Single / Head of household / Qualifying surviving spouse / Married filing jointly),
# ALSO read the spouse form. On Married filing separately, the spouse form is NEVER read
# (each spouse files their own separate return).
if NOT isMfs AND spouseMade:
    for each field in fields:
        v = parseAmount(spouseForm[field])
        if v is not null AND v > 0:
            total = addNonNull(total, v)

return (total is not null AND total > 0) ? roundMoney(total) : null
```

Four things to notice in the helper:

1. **Screening gate is OR-ed across both forms.** If the taxpayer left the form blank (or never opened it) but the spouse made payments, the gate still opens and the spouse contribution is summed.
2. **Per-form gate enforcement.** Within the aggregation loop, each form's amount fields are only read if THAT form's `madeEstimatedTaxPayments` is true. A taxpayer who answered "no" but accidentally typed an installment amount has that amount silently ignored (correct UX semantic).
3. **Positive-amount filter.** Each amount must be `parseAmount(...) > 0` to contribute. Negative amounts (corrupt data) and zero amounts are silently skipped. This matches the IRS rule that all line-26 contributions are non-negative.
4. **`divorceFormerSpouseSSN` is not in the fields list.** It is preserved on the entity for PDF rendering but is structurally excluded from the sum.

### 2b. Composer — line 26 section of `computeLine31ThroughLine38` (line 26821)

```
filingStatusStr = normalizeFilingStatus(filingStatusData.filingStatus)
isMfs = "Married filing separately" equals-ignoring-case filingStatusStr
isMfj = "Married filing jointly"   equals-ignoring-case filingStatusStr

line26 = computeLine26EstimatedTax(
    estimatedTaxPaymentsTaxpayer,
    estimatedTaxPaymentsSpouse,
    isMfs)

payments.setEstimatedTaxPayments(line26)
```

The composer is a single delegate call. No additional rounding, no additional filtering, no additional source of payments. All complexity is concentrated in the helper.

### 2c. Downstream consumer — line 33 total payments (line 26854 approximate)

```
line33 = safeAmount(totalWithholding)        # line 25d
       + safeAmount(estimatedTaxPayments)    # line 26
       + safeAmount(line32)                  # total other payments and refundable credits
payments.setTotalPayments(line33)
```

Line 33 is the simple sum of three preceding payments sub-totals. Line 26's contribution is exactly its stored value (or zero when null).

### 2d. Compute order (relevant call sites in `prepare()`)

```
~217    personalForms.get("estimated-tax-payments-taxpayer")  → estimatedTaxPaymentsTaxpayer
~218    personalForms.get("estimated-tax-payments-spouse")    → estimatedTaxPaymentsSpouse
~217    personalForms.get("filing-status")                    → filingStatusData
~3400   computeLine31ThroughLine38(...)                       → writes Payments.estimatedTaxPayments
```

### 2e. Frontend display

Line 26 is displayed on the Form 1040 PDF preview at the page-2 Acroform field for line 26 via the semantic name `line26_estimated_tax_payments`. The frontend reads `form.payments?.estimatedTaxPayments` from the compute response and writes the formatted amount into that field. The frontend ALSO recomputes line 33 client-side using `sumAmounts([line25dTotalWithholding(), payments.estimatedTaxPayments, line32])`; the values are equivalent because the frontend reads the same three sub-line values.

### 2f. Downstream Form 2210 consumer

The Form 2210 (Underpayment of Estimated Tax) computation reads `Payments.totalPayments` (which includes line 26) to compare against the required annual payment. If actual payments fall short of the required annual payment, Form 2210 computes a per-quarter underpayment penalty. The line-26 amount itself does NOT affect Form 2210's required-annual-payment calculation (which is based on total tax for the year, with safe-harbor rules for prior-year tax) — it only affects whether the taxpayer is in shortfall.

## 4. Identified gaps and shortcomings

Listed in order of taxpayer-impact severity. None are blocking for the wage-earner population in scope; all are documented and deferred per `lines\26.md` and the `knowledge\line-26-estimated-tax-payments.md` §12 gap inventory.

### Gap A — Payment dates not captured

**Severity:** Low for line 26 itself; Medium for Form 2210 Schedule AI.

**What the IRS rule says.** Line 26 is the total of all creditable 2025 estimated payments — payment dates do not affect this total. However, Form 2210 (Underpayment of Estimated Tax) Schedule AI (Annualized Income Installment Method) can reduce the underpayment penalty for taxpayers whose income was uneven across quarters (e.g., a year-end bonus or a Q4 stock sale). Schedule AI requires the actual date of each payment to properly allocate it to its quarter.

**What the code does.** The 4 installment fields (`installment1Amount` through `installment4Amount`) capture amounts per quarterly slot but NOT actual payment dates. Line 26 is correct because the sum is order-independent. Form 2210 uses the project's regular-method computation (4 equal installments, withholding split equally), which is conservative — Schedule AI would sometimes reduce the penalty further but is not implemented.

**Fix:** out of scope until Form 2210 Schedule AI is in scope. Deferred per `lines\26.md §8` and `knowledge\line-26-estimated-tax-payments.md` G6.

### Gap B — Farmers and fishermen single-installment safe harbor

**Severity:** Out of scope.

**What the IRS rule says.** Farmers and fishermen (taxpayers whose gross income from farming or fishing is at least two-thirds of total gross income) may pay all their estimated tax by January 15, 2026 in one installment instead of the four quarterly installments. They can also file by March 1, 2026 to avoid an estimated-tax penalty entirely. Form 2210-F handles their penalty computation.

**What the code does.** The project's intake schema has 4 quarterly slots; there is no single-installment shortcut. The line-26 total still works correctly for a farmer who entered the full amount in `installment4Amount` only (the sum is the same). The deficiency is that Form 2210-F is not implemented for the corresponding penalty computation.

**Fix:** out of scope — farming and fishing income paths are documented as out of scope for the project's wage-earner-first scope.

### Gap C — Married-filing-separately auto-allocation of joint estimated payments

**Severity:** Low.

**What the IRS rule says.** If divorced or separated spouses paid joint estimated tax during 2025 but file separate 2025 returns, they may divide the payments any way they agree. If they cannot agree, Publication 505 specifies a proportional-to-tax allocation: each spouse's share equals the joint payment × (that spouse's individual tax / combined individual tax on the two separate returns).

**What the code does.** The implementation does NOT auto-allocate. On Married filing separately, the helper reads only `estimated-tax-payments-taxpayer` and ignores the spouse form. The user is responsible for entering the agreed-upon allocation directly into the taxpayer form fields. If the spouses cannot agree and need the proportional allocation, the user must compute it manually.

**Fix:** out of scope until a multi-party allocation UI exists. Deferred per the documented out-of-scope policy.

### Gap D — Community-property Married-filing-separately 50/50 split

**Severity:** Out of scope.

**What the IRS rule says.** In community-property states (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin), each spouse's share of community estimated-tax payments is generally 50%. Publication 555 documents the community-property rules.

**What the code does.** The project does not currently distinguish community-property states from others and does not apply the 50/50 split.

**Fix:** out of scope. Same documented out-of-scope policy as Gap C.

### Gap E — Form 1040-ES voucher PDF export

**Severity:** None (out of scope for line-26 correctness).

The project does not currently generate Form 1040-ES quarterly vouchers for next-year payments. This is a separate PDF-export feature and does not affect the line-26 computation. Noted here only because `knowledge\line-26-estimated-tax-payments.md` §12 lists it as a deferred item.

## 5. Validation rules the implementation already enforces (positive findings)

- `line 26 ≥ 0` — every amount field is filtered by `compareTo(BigDecimal.ZERO) > 0`; negative values are silently discarded.
- `line 26 = sum of (all positive Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4 installments + prior-year overpayments)` for the captured sources — exactly matches the IRS rule.
- `line 26 excludes the spouse's payments on Married filing separately` — enforced by the helper's `!isMfs && spouseMade` clause.
- `line 26 combines both spouses' payments on Married filing jointly` — both forms read; both gates checked independently; both forms' positive amounts summed.
- `line 26 = null when the gate is closed and no positive amounts exist` — returned-null semantics match the project's null-when-zero convention.
- `line 26 sub-total rounded to whole dollars before storage` — `roundMoney(total)` at the helper return.
- `divorceFormerSpouseSSN` is stored on the entity but NOT summed into line 26 — annotation-only behavior matches the IRS rule that the SSN is an information field.
- `line 33 = line 25d + line 26 + line 32` — enforced by `computeLine31ThroughLine38(...)`.
- The taxpayer form may be null (never saved) while the spouse form has payments — the helper handles this safely (G4 fix 2026-04-19).

## 9. Gap closure log — 2026-06-10 third pass

A principled third-pass re-audit of the line-26 helper (`TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine26EstimatedTax` at line 26973) against the IRS rule and the project's single-return-per-household architecture surfaced one previously-undocumented IRS-correctness bug — classified as **Gap F**. Closed in this session.

### 9a. Gap F — Non-MFJ stale-spouse-form aggregation

**Severity:** Medium. **Status:** ✅ FIXED.

**What the IRS rule says.** Per Publication 17 chapter 6 and Publication 559 (Survivors, Executors, and Administrators), only a Married-filing-jointly return aggregates both spouses' estimated payments on line 26. Single, Head of household, and Qualifying surviving spouse filers have no living spouse on the return, so their line 26 reflects only their own payments. Married filing separately also reflects only the filer's own payments (with the Publication 505 allocation rule for jointly-paid amounts).

**What the code did before the fix.** The helper's spouse-form aggregation guard was `!isMfs`, which let through Single / Head of household / Qualifying surviving spouse / Married filing jointly. The intent in the accompanying comment ("non-MFS — including MFJ") aligned with the code but contradicted the IRS rule. The bug surfaces in this realistic scenario:

1. User is initially Married filing jointly and saves the `estimated-tax-payments-spouse` personal form with spouse estimated payments.
2. User changes filing status to Single (for example: divorced or widowed mid-year; or the user-typed-MFJ-by-mistake and corrected to Single).
3. The spouse personal form persists in storage under the same household Firebase user identity per `project_single_return` ("the application produces ONE tax return per household; no separate returns for spouse or dependents").
4. On compute, `personalForms.get("estimated-tax-payments-spouse")` returns the stale spouse form.
5. The helper's `!isMfs` guard evaluates true for Single → reads the spouse form → sums spouse payments.
6. Line 26 is overstated. The taxpayer ends up claiming a credit they were not entitled to (the divorced or widowed taxpayer cannot count their former or deceased spouse's separate-life estimated payments on a Single return).

### 9e. Lesson captured from the third pass

The Gap F bug had three properties that let it survive prior audits:

1. **The code comment matched the code.** The guard `!isMfs` and the comment "non-MFS (including MFJ)" agreed with each other. A first-pass code reader would not notice the discrepancy without cross-checking the IRS rule.
2. **The IRS rule looked compatible at first read.** Publication 17 and the project's own `lines\26.md §6` describe MFJ aggregation; a casual read suggests "MFJ aggregates spouse, MFS does not" — which `!isMfs` correctly captures for the MFJ vs MFS distinction. The Single / Head of household / Qualifying surviving spouse case is invisible unless you explicitly think "what about non-MFJ-non-MFS?"
3. **The test inventory had a symmetric blind spot.** Tests covered MFJ-positive, MFJ-with-taxpayer-form-null, and MFS-negative. Single + stale spouse form was structurally outside the MFJ/MFS dichotomy — no test exercised it.

**Diagnostic shortcut for future audits:** when a filing-status guard uses a NEGATION of a status (`!isMfs`), explicitly enumerate every filing status that satisfies the negation (Single, Head of household, Qualifying surviving spouse, Married filing jointly — four statuses) and check whether the IRS rule applies to ALL of them. If the IRS rule applies to only one (typically Married filing jointly), the negation is the wrong guard. Prefer affirmative single-status guards (`isMfj`) over negated multi-status guards (`!isMfs`).

This is a generalization of the pattern that the 25b session named "compute-wiring drift" (forms registered in 4-5 places but not loaded in `prepare()`) and the 25c session named "composer-truth-table coverage" (every Boolean state of every composer conditional should have a test).
