## Quick orientation

The Form 1040 label for line 27c, as printed on the 2025 page 2:

> **"c   If you do not want to claim the EIC, check here ......."**

In one sentence: line 27c is a single **checkbox** on Form 1040 page 2 sitting directly below line 27b. It carries **no dollar amount**. Its value tells the Internal Revenue Service whether the taxpayer is *not* claiming the Earned Income Credit on this return — either because the taxpayer voluntarily declines the credit, or because the taxpayer is disqualified from it under some statutory rule.

The visible printed label uses the word "want" — implying voluntary opt-out — but the 2025 Instructions for Form 1040 extend the rule. In every disqualification scenario discussed in the instructions (foreign earned income exclusion, nonresident alien status, age outside the childless 25-to-64 window, investment income over the 2025 ceiling of $11,950, the Married Filing Separately gate, etc.), the instructions explicitly tell the taxpayer to "check the box on line 27c." So in practical terms the box should be checked in two distinct situations:

1. The taxpayer **chose** not to claim the Earned Income Credit (voluntary opt-out).
2. The taxpayer **could not** claim the Earned Income Credit (forced disqualification).

Both situations produce the same end state: line 27a is blank (no Earned Income Credit dollar value), and line 27c is checked. This is what allows the Internal Revenue Service to distinguish "the taxpayer is claiming Earned Income Credit but the value is zero" (where some hidden bug in our computation might be at fault) from "the taxpayer is not claiming Earned Income Credit at all" (where the absence of a value is intentional).

## 1. The Internal Revenue Service canonical rule for line 27c

### 1a. What the Internal Revenue Service says, in plain English

Line 27c should be checked when the Earned Income Credit dollar amount on line 27a is blank for any reason. The instructions for Form 1040 (2025) tell the taxpayer to check this box in two distinct flows:

- **Voluntary opt-out flow.** The taxpayer may meet all the eligibility tests for the Earned Income Credit but decides not to claim it. Common reasons include simplification (the credit is small and the taxpayer wants to skip the qualifying-child reporting), strategic reasons (claiming the credit might delay the refund under the Protecting Americans from Tax Hikes Act of 2015 hold rule for Earned Income Credit returns until mid-February), or general preference. The taxpayer signals the decline by checking line 27c and leaving line 27a blank.
- **Forced-disqualification flow.** The taxpayer fails one of the Earned Income Credit eligibility tests (foreign earned income exclusion via Form 2555; nonresident alien for any part of the year; failure of the prior-year recertification gate under Internal Revenue Code section 32(k); investment income above the 2025 ceiling of $11,950; Married Filing Separately without the separated-spouse exception under Internal Revenue Code section 32(d)(2); childless filer under age 25 or over age 64; insufficient earned income; etc.). In every one of these scenarios the 2025 Instructions for Form 1040 say, near the end of the discussion of the disqualifying condition, "You can't take the credit. Check the box on line 27c."

### 1b. The single rule that captures both flows

Both flows collapse into the same observable state: the line 27a dollar amount is not present on the return. So the IRS canonical rule for line 27c reduces to a single conditional:

```text
line27c = (line27a is blank)
```

Equivalently, expressed against the project's internal model:

```text
line27c = (computedEarnedIncomeCredit is null OR zero)
```

In code-style notation:

```text
line27c = (form1040.payments.earnedIncomeCredit == null)
```

The reason the IRS rule is expressible as a single check is that **all** the upstream paths — voluntary decline; foreign earned income exclusion; nonresident alien; failed prior-year recertification; investment income ceiling; Married Filing Separately gate; childless age window failure; insufficient earned income; both Earned Income Credit table lookups producing zero credit — converge on the same downstream state of "the line 27a value is absent." The project's compute helper at `computeLine27aEIC` returns `null` for every one of these paths (see `XLS/Computations/27a.md §9c` for the enumeration).

### 1c. What checking line 27c does

Checking line 27c does not change any other value on the return. It does not subtract a dollar amount. It does not flow into line 32 (other refundable credits subtotal). It does not feed line 33 (total payments). Its sole purpose is to record the absence of an Earned Income Credit claim for downstream Internal Revenue Service processing.

The implication for line 27a: when line 27c is checked, line 27a is required to be blank or zero. When line 27c is unchecked, line 27a is required to carry a positive Earned Income Credit value computed via the standard Earned Income Credit worksheet (see `XLS/Computations/27a.md §1`).

### 1d. Equation for line 27c itself

Because line 27c is a checkbox, its "value" is Boolean (true / checked, or false / unchecked):

```text
line27c = (line27a is blank or zero)
```

On a joint return, the rule is applied to the household-level Earned Income Credit value, not separately to each spouse. Either spouse's disqualifier flows through the joint Earned Income Credit computation; the resulting line 27a value (or the absence of one) is what drives line 27c.

## 5. The Internal Revenue Service canonical computation, step by step

For a reader who wants to manually compute line 27c for a 2025 tax return:

1. **First compute line 27a** following all the steps in `XLS/Computations/27a.md §5`. This includes:
   - Reading the taxpayer's Earned Income Credit personal form to check `claimsEIC`.
   - Verifying no hard disqualifier fires (Form 2555 foreign earned income exclusion, nonresident alien status, prior-year denial without Form 8862, taxpayer Individual Taxpayer Identification Number per Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(E)).
   - Verifying the Married Filing Separately gate or its separated-spouse exception.
   - Computing earned income from Wage and Tax Statement (Form W-2) box 1 wages plus the combat-pay election and other earned-income fields.
   - Verifying investment income is at or below the 2025 ceiling of $11,950.
   - Counting qualifying children per Internal Revenue Code section 152(c).
   - Looking up the Earned Income Credit value twice (once against earned income; once against adjusted gross income) in the 2025 Earned Income Credit table.
   - Taking the smaller of the two and rounding down to the nearest dollar.
2. **Examine the result.** Either line 27a has a positive dollar value, or it is blank.
3. **Set line 27c:**
   - If line 27a has a positive dollar value → set line 27c to `false` (unchecked).
   - If line 27a is blank (any reason) → set line 27c to `true` (checked).

In pseudocode:

```text
computedLine27a = computeLine27aEIC(...)
line27c = (computedLine27a is null OR computedLine27a == 0)
```

### Step-by-step example

A single filer with $25,000 of Wage and Tax Statement (Form W-2) box 1 wages, no qualifying children, age 30, no Form 2555, no Individual Taxpayer Identification Number, no nonresident alien status, no investment income, and `claimsEIC = true` runs the Earned Income Credit table. The childless-Earned-Income-Credit phaseout end for Single filers in 2025 is $19,104; $25,000 exceeds that, so the table returns $0. The line 27a helper observes that both creditFromEarnedIncome and creditFromAGI are $0 and returns `null`. The line 27c renderer observes the null and writes `true`. On the printed Form 1040, line 27a is blank and the line 27c checkbox is marked.

The same filer with `claimsEIC = false` (voluntary opt-out) produces the same observable result: line 27a is blank, line 27c is checked. The Internal Revenue Service cannot distinguish the two scenarios from the line 27c value alone — and that is by design. The Internal Revenue Service does not need to distinguish them, because the absence of an Earned Income Credit claim is what matters for downstream processing.

## 6. The computation as implemented in this codebase

Line 27c is **implemented at the render layer only**, with no compute-time storage on the output model.

### 6a. The compute path

The backend helper at `TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine27aEIC` (line 27342 onward in `src/main/java/com/ustax/microservices/TaxReturnComputeService.java`) returns a `BigDecimal` Earned Income Credit value or `null`. When any disqualifier fires, the helper returns `null` at the corresponding guard. The disqualifier paths that produce `null`, as enumerated in `XLS/Computations/27a.md §9c`, are:

| Disqualifier | Internal Revenue Code reference | Returns `null` at |
|---|---|---|
| `claimsEIC = false` (voluntary opt-out) | Not statutory; project intake gate | Top of helper |
| `hasForm2555 = true` | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(D) | Hard-disqualifier block |
| `isNonresidentAlien = true` | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(C) | Hard-disqualifier block |
| Taxpayer Social Security Number is Individual Taxpayer Identification Number-format (leading 9) | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(E) and section 32(m) | Gap L closure block |
| Spouse Social Security Number is Individual Taxpayer Identification Number-format on Married Filing Jointly | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(E) and section 32(m) | Gap L closure block |
| `eicPreviouslyDenied = true` without Form 8862 recertification | Internal Revenue Code section 32(k) | Form 8862 gate |
| Married Filing Separately without separated-spouse exception | Internal Revenue Code section 32(d)(1) and section 32(d)(2) | Filing-status gate |
| Earned income ≤ 0 after inmate-wages exclusion | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(2)(B)(iv) | After earned-income computation |
| Investment income > $11,950 ceiling | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(F) | Investment-income gate |
| `mainHomeInUsMoreThanHalfYear = false` (childless path only) | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(A)(ii)(I) | Gap I closure block |
| `taxpayerCanBeClaimedAsDependent = true` (childless path only) | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(A)(ii)(III) | Gap H closure block |
| `spouseCanBeClaimedAsDependent = true` on Married Filing Jointly (childless path only) | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(A)(ii)(III) | Gap H closure block |
| Childless filer with age < 25 or > 64 | Internal Revenue Code section 32(c)(1)(A)(ii)(II) | Childless age block |
| Both Earned Income Credit table lookups produce credit ≤ 0 | The 2025 Earned Income Credit table | End of helper |

Every one of these paths sets the `Payments.earnedIncomeCredit` field to `null` on the output model.

### 6b. The render path

The Angular client-side renderer at `us-tax-ui/src/app/forms/form-tax-return-1040.component.ts:830` reads the `payments.earnedIncomeCredit` field from the computed tax return and writes the line 27c Acroform value:

```typescript
// XLS/Computations/27a.md §9 Gap C closure — line 27c checkbox ("If you
// don't want to claim the EIC, check this box") must be checked whenever
// line 27a is null, i.e., whenever the taxpayer is disqualified for any
// reason (Form 2555, nonresident alien, investment-income ceiling, MFS
// without §32(d)(2) exception, childless-EIC age out-of-range, etc.)
// OR the taxpayer voluntarily declined (`claimsEIC = false` → backend
// returns null). All those paths collapse to `earnedIncomeCredit == null`
// upstream, so a single null check covers the whole gate matrix.
values['line27c_no_schedule_eic_claim'] = form.payments?.earnedIncomeCredit == null;
```

The expression `form.payments?.earnedIncomeCredit == null` evaluates to `true` precisely when the helper has returned a null Earned Income Credit value — which is exactly the Internal Revenue Service canonical condition for checking line 27c. The use of JavaScript's `==` (rather than `===`) means both `null` and `undefined` collapse to `true`, which is the desired behaviour when the payments object is incomplete or missing.

### 6c. The implemented "formula" expressed in §1b notation

```text
line27c = (form.payments?.earnedIncomeCredit == null)
```

This is identical to the IRS canonical equation from §1b. No translation layer or intermediate logic; the implementation mirrors the rule directly.

### 6d. What is NOT stored on the output model

The `Payments.java` output model does not have a `line27c` field. The Boolean value is computed at render time and written directly to the PDF Acroform field; it is not persisted across render passes. This is an acceptable design because line 27c has no downstream consumer — no other computation reads it, and the PDF output is the only place where it appears.

## 8. Validations and gating rules

Line 27c carries no numeric value, so no floor / ceiling / range validation applies.

The only structural validation is the cross-consistency invariant with line 27a:

```text
invariant: (line27a is positive) implies (line27c is false)
invariant: (line27a is blank)    implies (line27c is true)
```

The implementation enforces this invariant by construction. The render layer writes `line27c_no_schedule_eic_claim = (form.payments?.earnedIncomeCredit == null)`, so it is impossible for line 27c to be `false` when line 27a is null, or for line 27c to be `true` when line 27a is positive. The invariant cannot be violated by any input combination.

This codebase emits **no validation flags** related to line 27c. There is no `TaxReturnFlag` for line 27c. The cross-consistency invariant does not require user-facing flagging because the renderer cannot violate it.

## 10. Gap analysis

### Gap C — Line 27c auto-check (CLOSED)

**Status: CLOSED in `XLS/Computations/27a.md §9c` on the same closure session that fixed the line 27a PDF field name.**

**Description (now historical):** before the §9c closure, the frontend renderer had zero references to `line27c_no_schedule_eic_claim`. The Acroform checkbox rendered unchecked on every return, regardless of the state of the Earned Income Credit. This violated the Internal Revenue Service canonical rule: for a disqualified or opt-out taxpayer the box should have been checked, but the rendered Form 1040 left it blank.

**Closure as applied:** added one line of code at `form-tax-return-1040.component.ts:830`:

```typescript
values['line27c_no_schedule_eic_claim'] = form.payments?.earnedIncomeCredit == null;
```

The closure rationale (also recorded in `XLS/Computations/27a.md §9c`):

> Concluded that a single null check (`payments?.earnedIncomeCredit == null`) is the complete signal for line 27c.

The implementation is now correct end to end. No further work is required for line 27c itself.

### Gap U — Missing end-to-end test for the render path

**Status: ✅ CLOSED 2026-06-11.**

**Description (historical):** no test asserted the rendered Acroform value for line 27c. The compute layer was correct (the helper returns null on every disqualifier path), the renderer was correct (`form.payments?.earnedIncomeCredit == null` evaluates to the right Boolean), and the line 27c PDF field name was correct (`line27c_no_schedule_eic_claim`) — but a regression that changed the renderer line from `== null` to `!= null` (or any other corruption) would have silently broken the printed Form 1040 for every taxpayer without any test failing.

**Closure applied 2026-06-11:** added a focused unit test at `us-tax-ui/src/app/forms/form-tax-return-1040.line27c.spec.ts` that pins the IRS canonical rule directly against the renderer's `buildFieldValues()` output. The test instantiates the actual `FormTaxReturn1040Component` via Angular TestBed with a controlled `TaxReturnService` stub, sets the `computation()` signal to four representative scenarios, and asserts the resulting `fieldValues()['line27c_no_schedule_eic_claim']` value:

| Scenario | Mocked `payments.earnedIncomeCredit` | Expected `line27c_no_schedule_eic_claim` | Why it matters |

### 13f. Lesson captured

**Documentation drift can outlast a code closure.** Gap C was closed on the same day as the line 27 PDF-name fix in `XLS/Computations/27a.md §9c` — and yet the XLSX manifest continued to describe line 27c as "NOT YET IMPLEMENTED" for some time afterward. The diagnostic shortcut: **after closing a behavioral gap, also audit any data manifests, knowledge files, and combined specs that referenced the gap by its old open status.** The compute closure does not auto-propagate to derived documentation artifacts.

**Unit tests are appropriate regression guards for simple render rules.** For Gap U the question was whether to add a Playwright e2e test or a focused unit test. The unit test was cheaper, more direct (no PDF rendering needed), and tests the actual code (no test-only mirror function). The unit test is appropriate when the rule is short (one line), purely a translation of an upstream state into a Boolean field, and the upstream state is itself well-tested by other suites.
