## Quick orientation

The Form 1040 label for line 31 (per the actual 2025 IRS Form 1040 page 2):

> **"31   Amount from Schedule 3, line 15 ............... 31"**

In one sentence: line 31 is a **pure transcription** of Schedule 3 line 15 — the grand total of Schedule 3 Part II ("Other Payments and Refundable Credits"). Form 1040 line 31 is not computed independently on Form 1040; the value simply flows from the Schedule 3 Part II total to Form 1040 page 2.

Five facts to keep in mind throughout:

1. **Line 31 is a pure pass-through.** Form 1040 line 31 = Schedule 3 line 15. There is no Form-1040-level arithmetic for this line. Every dollar that ends up on line 31 came from one of the eight or nine source lines that feed Schedule 3 Part II.

2. **Line 31 carries only Schedule 3 Part II (refundable side).** Schedule 3 Part I (non-refundable credits) flows separately to Form 1040 line 20, NOT to line 31. Mixing the two is a frequent confusion: line 20 is the sum of non-refundable Schedule 3 lines; line 31 is the sum of refundable Schedule 3 lines.

3. **The 2025 Premium Tax Credit went through a structural change.** In tax year 2024 the Net Premium Tax Credit was on Form 1040 line 30. For tax year 2025 the Internal Revenue Service moved the Net Premium Tax Credit to Schedule 3 line 9, and Form 1040 line 30 was reassigned to the Refundable Adoption Credit. So in 2025 the Net Premium Tax Credit reaches Form 1040 only through line 31 (via Schedule 3 line 9 → line 15 → Form 1040 line 31). The full Premium Tax Credit computation lives on Form 8962 and is the topic of a separate audit; only the wiring into Schedule 3 line 9 matters here.

4. **Line 31 carries only the refundable side of the Premium Tax Credit.** When the user's Advance Premium Tax Credit (paid directly to the insurance marketplace) exceeded their final Premium Tax Credit at Form 8962 line 25, the excess is repaid via Schedule 2 line 1a — NOT subtracted from line 31. Schedule 3 line 9 is non-zero only when Form 8962 line 26 (the net refund) is positive.

5. **Line 31 flows into line 32 (other refundable credits subtotal) and from there into line 33 (total payments).** It does not directly reduce tax. It reduces the amount the taxpayer owes by increasing the total-payments side of the return.

The equation:

```text
Form 1040 line 31 = Schedule 3 line 15
                  = Schedule 3 line 9 + Schedule 3 line 10 + Schedule 3 line 11
                    + Schedule 3 line 12 + Schedule 3 line 14

Schedule 3 line 14 = Schedule 3 line 13a + Schedule 3 line 13b + Schedule 3 line 13c
                     + Schedule 3 line 13d + Schedule 3 line 13z

Schedule 3 line 15 = Schedule 3 line 9 + line 10 + line 11 + line 12 + line 14
```

Each of the eight individual source lines is described in the rest of this document.

## 1. The Internal Revenue Service canonical computation

### 1a. Schedule 3 line 9 — Net Premium Tax Credit (from Form 8962 line 26)

In plain English: when the user purchased health insurance through the Marketplace under the Affordable Care Act and the actual annual Premium Tax Credit they earned exceeded the Advance Premium Tax Credit already paid to the insurer on their behalf, the difference is a refundable credit on Schedule 3 line 9.

Per the 2025 Form 8962 instructions:

```text
Form 8962 line 24 = annual Premium Tax Credit (computed from Modified Adjusted Gross Income,
                    household size, and benchmark premium for the second-lowest-cost silver plan)
Form 8962 line 25 = Advance Premium Tax Credit (the amount the Marketplace paid to the insurer
                    in 2025 on the user's behalf — from Form 1095-A)

If Form 8962 line 24 > Form 8962 line 25:
   Form 8962 line 26 = Form 8962 line 24 - Form 8962 line 25
   Schedule 3 line 9 = Form 8962 line 26

If Form 8962 line 24 < Form 8962 line 25:
   The excess Advance Premium Tax Credit must be repaid. The repayment is computed in
   Form 8962 Part III (subject to the income-tier repayment cap) and goes to Schedule 2
   line 1a — NOT to Schedule 3 line 9. Schedule 3 line 9 stays at 0.

If Form 8962 line 24 = Form 8962 line 25:
   Schedule 3 line 9 = 0 (no net credit and no repayment).
```

Important 2025 rule per the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (which extended enhanced Premium Tax Credit rules through 2025): the historical 400-percent-of-federal-poverty-line cliff does NOT apply for 2025. Households above 400 percent of the federal poverty line may still qualify for a Premium Tax Credit if the benchmark premium exceeds 8.5 percent of household income.

Important Married Filing Separately rule: Married Filing Separately filers generally cannot claim the Premium Tax Credit, except for the statutory exceptions for domestic abuse and spousal abandonment (handled inside Form 8962).

### 1b. Schedule 3 line 10 — Amount paid with request for extension to file

In plain English: when the user requested an automatic extension of time to file (typically by April 15 of the year following the tax year) and paid an estimated tax amount along with the extension request, that payment is treated as a refundable credit on Schedule 3 line 10. The payment effectively counts as additional tax paid throughout the year.

Qualifying extension payment sources:

```text
Form 4868 payment   = "Application for Automatic Extension of Time To File U.S. Individual
                      Income Tax Return" — the most common case.
Form 2350 payment   = "Application for Extension of Time To File U.S. Income Tax Return" —
                      narrow case for U.S. citizens or residents abroad requesting more time
                      to qualify for the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Form 2555).
Electronic extension
payment via e-file  = a tax-software-initiated payment that also serves as the extension request,
                      without a paper Form 4868. The amount and date are the same as a Form 4868
                      payment for line 10 purposes.
```

Important guardrails:
- This is a *payment* line, not an extension-approval line. Granting the extension does not put anything on line 10; only the dollar amount the taxpayer paid does.
- Payment-processing fees (credit-card surcharges, etc.) are NOT part of the creditable amount.
- An extension of time to file is NOT an extension of time to pay tax. Interest and late-payment penalties accrue on any unpaid balance after the original due date.

### 1c. Schedule 3 line 11 — Excess Social Security and Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act tax withheld

In plain English: an employee who works for two or more employers during the year may have more Social Security tax (or Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act tax) withheld in total than the statutory annual maximum. Each individual employer correctly withholds up to the cap based on the wages they paid; the cumulative over-withholding across employers is refundable on Schedule 3 line 11.

The 2025 constants:

```text
Social Security wage base = $176,100
Social Security tax rate  = 6.2%
Maximum employee Social Security tax for 2025 = $176,100 × 6.2% = $10,918.20

Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act wage base = $176,100 (same as Social Security for 2025)
Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act employee rate = 6.2% (same as Social Security)
Maximum employee Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act tax for 2025 = $10,918.20
```

The per-person Schedule 3 line 11 computation:

```text
sumW2Box4   = sum of W-2 box 4 (Social Security tax withheld) across all W-2s for one person
sumRrtaTier1 = sum of W-2 box 14 (Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act tax) across all W-2s
              for one person who is a railroad employee

If the person had 2 or more employers AND sumW2Box4 > $10,918.20:
   excessSs = sumW2Box4 - $10,918.20
Else:
   excessSs = 0 (a single employer that over-withheld must refund the excess directly to
               the employee — the taxpayer cannot claim it on Schedule 3 line 11)

If the person had 2 or more employers AND sumRrtaTier1 > $10,918.20:
   excessRrta = sumRrtaTier1 - $10,918.20
Else:
   excessRrta = 0

Schedule 3 line 11 per person = excessSs + excessRrta
```

On a joint return, the cap is computed *separately* per spouse. The spouses' over-withholding is *not* combined before applying the cap. Each spouse's excess is summed at the return level.

Important guardrail: line 11 is defined by **excess withholding above the annual cap with 2+ employers**, not by a number-of-Employer-Identification-Numbers test. A taxpayer who switches jobs mid-year and gets a corrected Form W-2c plus an original W-2 from the SAME employer is NOT a "2-employers" case — that is still one employer and the over-withholding (if any) must be resolved with the employer directly.

### 1d. Schedule 3 line 12 — Credit for federal tax on fuels (Form 4136)

In plain English: certain fuels are subject to federal excise tax. A taxpayer who used those fuels for a tax-exempt purpose (off-highway business use, farming, aviation, etc.) can compute a credit on Form 4136 and claim the credit as a refundable amount on Schedule 3 line 12.

```text
Schedule 3 line 12 = Form 4136 total credit
```

The Form 4136 computation is itself an aggregation of multiple per-fuel-type sub-credits (gasoline, diesel, kerosene, alternative fuels, etc.) — the line-12 source value is the total from Form 4136.

### 1e. Schedule 3 line 13a — Credit for undistributed long-term capital gains (Form 2439)

In plain English: when a regulated investment company (a mutual fund) earns long-term capital gains but doesn't distribute them to shareholders, the fund pays tax on the undistributed gain and reports it on Form 2439. The shareholder can claim the tax paid by the fund as a refundable credit on Schedule 3 line 13a.

```text
Schedule 3 line 13a = sum across all Form 2439 statements of box 2 (tax paid by the fund
                      on the undistributed long-term capital gain)
```

The undistributed gain itself (box 1a) is reported separately on Schedule D as an additional long-term capital gain; only the tax-paid amount in box 2 reaches Schedule 3 line 13a.

### 1f. Schedule 3 line 13b — Credit for repayment under Internal Revenue Code section 1341

In plain English: when a taxpayer reported income in a prior year under a "claim of right" (e.g., they believed they were entitled to the income) and then had to repay more than $3,000 of that income in the current year (because it turned out they were not entitled), Internal Revenue Code section 1341 lets them choose between:

- (a) Deducting the repayment on the current year's return, OR
- (b) Computing the tax they would have paid in the prior year *without* that income, and claiming the difference as a *credit* on the current year's Schedule 3 line 13b.

Schedule 3 line 13b reports the dollar amount of the section-1341 credit (option b). The user computes this credit outside Form 1040 (using prior-year returns and IRS Publication 525 worksheets) and enters the result.

```text
Schedule 3 line 13b = section 1341 credit (user-computed; >$3,000 repayment threshold)
```

### 1g. Schedule 3 line 13c — Net elective payment election amount from Form 3800 Part III line 6 column (j)

In plain English: certain energy-related general business credits can be "monetized" by an elective payment election — the credit is treated as a tax payment, refundable in cash if it exceeds the tax liability. Schedule 3 line 13c carries the elective-payment dollar amount from Form 3800 Part III line 6 column (j).

This is a narrow source applicable mostly to entities (partnerships, S corporations, exempt organizations) and to specific energy credits under the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022. It is out of scope for the current application.

```text
Schedule 3 line 13c = Form 3800 Part III line 6 column (j) (elective payment amount)
                    — out of scope in this codebase (see Gap G-NEW-1 in section 4)
```

### 1h. Schedule 3 line 13d — Deferred amount of net section 965 tax liability

In plain English: Internal Revenue Code section 965 (enacted by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017) imposed a one-time transition tax on certain accumulated foreign earnings of U.S. shareholders of specified foreign corporations. Taxpayers could elect to pay the tax over 8 years. Schedule 3 line 13d reports the dollar amount of any installment deferred under that election that the user is recognizing this year.

For tax year 2025, this is a residual line for shareholders whose 2017 election still has remaining installments. The user enters the deferred amount directly.

```text
Schedule 3 line 13d = current-year installment of the section 965(i) deferred tax liability
                    (user-entered)
```

### 1i. Schedule 3 line 13z — Other refundable credits (write-in)

In plain English: line 13z is the catch-all write-in line for refundable credits that do not have their own line on Schedule 3 Part II. Each write-in is a description plus a dollar amount, and the user can enter multiple write-ins which sum together for line 13z.

Known 2025 write-in sources include:

- **Form 8689** — "Allocation of Individual Income Tax to the U.S. Virgin Islands." A U.S. citizen who paid tax to the U.S. Virgin Islands government uses Form 8689 to allocate the income; the resulting credit goes to Schedule 3 line 13z. The 2025 Form 1040 Instructions explicitly mention Form 8689 as a line 13z source.
- Less-common items per the Form 1040 instructions: certain credits for prior years not separately listed, refunds claimed from the U.S. Virgin Islands government, etc.

```text
Schedule 3 line 13z = sum of write-in amounts entered by the user
                    (each with a description for audit trail)
```

### 1j. Schedule 3 line 14 — Sum of lines 13a through 13z

In plain English: this is a pure subtotal of the five "other refundable credits" sub-lines.

```text
Schedule 3 line 14 = Schedule 3 line 13a (Form 2439)
                   + Schedule 3 line 13b (section 1341)
                   + Schedule 3 line 13c (Form 3800 — currently 0 in this codebase)
                   + Schedule 3 line 13d (section 965(i))
                   + Schedule 3 line 13z (write-ins)
```

### 1k. Schedule 3 line 15 — Sum of lines 9, 10, 11, 12, and 14

In plain English: this is the grand total of Schedule 3 Part II, the value carried to Form 1040 line 31.

```text
Schedule 3 line 15 = Schedule 3 line 9   (Net Premium Tax Credit)
                   + Schedule 3 line 10  (extension payment)
                   + Schedule 3 line 11  (excess Social Security / Railroad Retirement Tax)
                   + Schedule 3 line 12  (Form 4136 fuel credit)
                   + Schedule 3 line 14  (other refundable credits subtotal)

Form 1040 line 31  = Schedule 3 line 15
```

Important guardrail: line 15 includes line 14 once as the already-aggregated 13a-13z subtotal. The individual 13a/13b/13c/13d/13z values must NOT be re-added separately after line 14 has been formed (otherwise the credit doubles).

### 1l. Form 1040 downstream wiring

```text
Form 1040 line 31 = Schedule 3 line 15
Form 1040 line 32 = line 27a (Earned Income Credit)
                  + line 28 (Additional Child Tax Credit)
                  + line 29 (refundable American Opportunity Credit)
                  + line 30 (Refundable Adoption Credit)
                  + line 31 (Schedule 3 line 15)
Form 1040 line 33 = line 25d (total withholding)
                  + line 26 (estimated tax payments)
                  + line 32 (other refundable credits subtotal)
```

Important guardrail: line 31 is included once through line 32. It is never added again separately into line 33.

## 2. The as-implemented computation in this codebase

The computation is split across six helper methods that each populate a single Schedule 3 Part II source, followed by `finalizeSchedule3Totals` which aggregates lines 14 and 15, followed by the line 31 wiring step.

### 2a. Compute order in `prepare()`

Code at `TaxReturnComputeService.java:2026-2054`:

```java
// Each of these populates exactly one Schedule 3 Part II source field.
applyPremiumTaxCreditToSchedule3(schedule3, form8962);    // → line 9
applyForm4868ToSchedule3(schedule3, form4868);            // → line 10
computeExcessSocialSecurityTax(schedule3, w2Entries, you, spouse);  // → line 11
applyForm2439CreditToSchedule3(schedule3, form2439Entries);          // → line 13a
applyOtherPaymentsFormToSchedule3(schedule3, otherPaymentsForm);     // → lines 12, 13b, 13d, 13z

// Then aggregate.
finalizeSchedule3Totals(schedule3);                       // → lines 14 + 15
```

### 2b. Source field population — what each helper does

| Helper method | Sets `Schedule3OtherPaymentsCredits` field | Maps to | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| `applyPremiumTaxCreditToSchedule3` | `netPremiumTaxCredit` | Schedule 3 line 9 | Form 8962 line 26 (when positive) |
| `applyForm4868ToSchedule3` | `amountPaidWithExtension` | Schedule 3 line 10 | Form 4868 extension payment |
| `computeExcessSocialSecurityTax` | `excessSocialSecurityRrtaTaxWithheld` | Schedule 3 line 11 | sum across W-2 box 4 per SSN |
| `applyForm2439CreditToSchedule3` | `form2439` | Schedule 3 line 13a | sum of Form 2439 box 2 across statements |
| `applyOtherPaymentsFormToSchedule3` | `creditForFederalTaxOnFuels` | Schedule 3 line 12 | manual entry (Form 4136 result) |
| `applyOtherPaymentsFormToSchedule3` | `section1341Credit` | Schedule 3 line 13b | manual entry |
| `applyOtherPaymentsFormToSchedule3` | `deferredNet965TaxLiability` | Schedule 3 line 13d | manual entry |
| `applyOtherPaymentsFormToSchedule3` | `otherRefundableCredits` | Schedule 3 line 13z | sum of `otherRefundableCreditItems[].amount` |
| (none — out of scope) | `netElectivePaymentElectionAmount` | Schedule 3 line 13c | Form 3800 (not implemented) |

### 2c. As-implemented excess Social Security tax (Schedule 3 line 11)

Code at `TaxReturnComputeService.java:26662-26729`:

```java
maxSsTaxPerPerson = $10,918.20
taxpayerSsn = normalizeSsn(you.ssn)
spouseSsn   = normalizeSsn(spouse.ssn)

excessTaxpayer = computeExcessSsForPerson(w2Entries, taxpayerSsn, $10,918.20)
excessSpouse   = computeExcessSsForPerson(w2Entries, spouseSsn,   $10,918.20)

totalExcess = excessTaxpayer + excessSpouse
```

Where `computeExcessSsForPerson`:

```java
personW2s = w2Entries where employeeSSN matches personSsn
If personW2s.size() < 2:                                  // 1 employer → no excess refundable here
   return null
totalWithheld = sum of W-2 box 4 (socialSecurityTaxWithheldAmount) across personW2s
If totalWithheld <= $10,918.20:                           // not over the cap
   return null
return totalWithheld - $10,918.20                         // refundable excess
```

The per-spouse cap is applied independently (the spouses' over-withholding is not combined before capping). The "2 or more employers" condition is implemented as "2 or more W-2 entries for this SSN" — see Gap G-NEW-2 in section 4 for the edge case where two W-2s from the SAME employer (corrected W-2c + original) would be incorrectly counted as two employers.

### 2d. As-implemented `applyOtherPaymentsFormToSchedule3` (lines 12, 13b, 13d, 13z)

Code at `TaxReturnComputeService.java:25882-25942`. Reads the `31-other-payments` personal form and sets:

- `creditForFederalTaxOnFuels` from `hasFuelTaxCredit` + `creditForFederalTaxOnFuels` amount
- `section1341Credit` from `hasSection1341Credit` + `section1341Credit` amount
- `deferredNet965TaxLiability` from `hasDeferred965Tax` + `deferredNet965TaxLiability` amount
- `otherRefundableCredits` = sum of `otherRefundableCreditItems[].amount` when `hasOtherRefundableCredits=true`

Each gating boolean (`hasFuelTaxCredit`, `hasSection1341Credit`, etc.) hides or shows the relevant amount field in the user interface but does not directly drive the compute beyond the truthy check (the amount is read only when the boolean is true).

### 2e. As-implemented `finalizeSchedule3Totals` (lines 14 + 15)

Code at `TaxReturnComputeService.java:26030-26084`:

```java
// Line 14 = sum of lines 13a, 13b, 13d, 13z
// (Note: line 13c is omitted because the codebase does not implement Form 3800
// elective-payment election; the field defaults to null which sumAmounts treats as 0.)
line14 = round(form2439
              + section1341Credit
              + deferredNet965TaxLiability
              + otherRefundableCredits)
schedule3.otherPaymentsCredits.totalOtherPaymentsRefundableCredits = line14

// Line 15 = lines 9 + 10 + 11 + 12 + 14
line15 = round(netPremiumTaxCredit
              + amountPaidWithExtension
              + excessSocialSecurityRrtaTaxWithheld
              + creditForFederalTaxOnFuels
              + line14)
schedule3.otherPaymentsCredits.totalOtherPaymentsAndRefundableCredits = line15
```

### 2f. As-implemented wiring to Form 1040 line 31

Code at `TaxReturnComputeService.java:27130-27138`:

```java
line31 = null
if (schedule3 has otherPaymentsCredits and totalOtherPaymentsAndRefundableCredits > 0):
   line31 = totalOtherPaymentsAndRefundableCredits
payments.setOtherPaymentsSchedule3(line31)              // → Form 1040 line 31
```

Important convention: `payments.otherPaymentsSchedule3` is set to `null` (NOT zero) when Schedule 3 line 15 is zero or negative. The Portable Document Format export shows a blank line-31 cell in that case rather than "$0".

### Gap G-NEW-1 (LOW severity — out-of-scope form, project decision)

**Schedule 3 line 13c (Form 3800 Part III line 6 column (j) — net elective payment election amount) is not implemented.**

A taxpayer who elected the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 net elective payment for an eligible energy credit (Schedule 8812 not relevant — this is Form 3800 for general business credits) cannot have their line 13c value computed by this application. The dependencies file documents this as G5 with a partial mitigation: the user interface shows an `info-note` panel directing the user to enter the amount as a line 13z write-in instead.

**Effect:** the user can still produce a correct line 15 total by routing the Form 3800 elective payment through line 13z (with a description). The downstream arithmetic is identical because line 13z and line 13c both feed line 14 with equal weight. The only difference is the line label on the printed Schedule 3.

**Status:** out of scope per project decision; mitigated by the line-13z workaround. No code change recommended unless Form 3800 enters scope generally.

### Gap G-NEW-2 (LOW severity — edge case in excess-Social Security computation)

**The "2 or more employers" test for Schedule 3 line 11 is implemented as "2 or more W-2 entries for this Social Security Number," not as "2 or more distinct Employer Identification Numbers."**

Code at `computeExcessSsForPerson` line 26715: `if (personW2s.size() < 2) return null;`. When the taxpayer has two W-2s from the SAME employer (a common case: a Form W-2c "corrected wage and tax statement" issued alongside the original W-2; or two W-2s for the same employer covering pre-merger and post-merger periods; or two W-2s for partial-year payroll runs that the employer issued as separate forms instead of one combined form), the code treats them as two separate employers and computes excess Social Security tax that the Internal Revenue Service rule does not allow.

**Numerical example:** a taxpayer earned $180,000 from one employer in 2025. The employer over-withheld Social Security tax (because the wages exceeded the $176,100 cap and the payroll system did not auto-cap), reporting $11,160 in box 4 on the original W-2. Then a Form W-2c was issued correcting the wages to $176,100 with $10,918.20 in box 4. If both forms appear in the W-2 statement list (e.g., the user uploaded both), the code sees 2 W-2 entries for the same SSN and computes excess = $11,160 + $10,918.20 - $10,918.20 = $11,160 of Schedule 3 line 11 — incorrectly. The IRS-correct answer is $0 on line 11 (the user should request a refund of the over-withholding directly from the employer).

**Code fix sketch:** group `personW2s` by Employer Identification Number (W-2 box b "Employer's identification number (EIN)") instead of counting entries. The "2 or more employers" test becomes `if (distinctEmployerIdentificationNumbers(personW2s).size() < 2) return null;`. Edge case: when one or both W-2 entries are missing the Employer Identification Number, fall back to grouping by employer name + ZIP code (or, conservatively, fail to count them as separate employers).

### Gap G-NEW-3 (LOW severity — Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act not implemented)

**Schedule 3 line 11 includes excess Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act tax per the IRS rule, but the code only handles Social Security tax.**

The Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act tax is the railroad-industry analog of Social Security tax (Internal Revenue Code sections 3201 and 3221). For 2025 the wage base and rate are identical to Social Security ($176,100 × 6.2% = $10,918.20 maximum per employee). When a railroad employee with two or more railroad employers had over-withholding, the excess is refundable on Schedule 3 line 11.

The code at `computeExcessSocialSecurityTax` reads only `socialSecurityTaxWithheldAmount` (W-2 box 4) and not the railroad-tax fields (which on a W-2 appear in box 14 with codes like "RRT1" or similar, or on a Form W-2C variant CT-1 for railroad employers). The output field is named `excessSocialSecurityRrtaTaxWithheld` (suggesting the intent was to handle both) but only the Social Security side is computed.

**Effect:** railroad employees with multiple employers and over-withheld Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act tax have $0 on Schedule 3 line 11 instead of the actual excess. The user must manually compute the credit outside the application and enter it as a line-13z write-in, or accept an under-claim.

**Code fix sketch:** extend `computeExcessSsForPerson` to also sum Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act tax from a new statement field (`box14RrtaTier1TaxWithheldAmount` or similar), compute its own cap separately ($10,918.20 for 2025), and add the Social Security excess and Railroad Retirement Tax Act excess together for line 11. Also requires adding the box-14 RRTA field to the W-2 statement input form.

### Gap G-NEW-4 (LOW severity — constants not centralized)

**The 2025 maximum employee Social Security tax ($10,918.20) is hardcoded as a `new BigDecimal("10918.20")` literal inside `computeExcessSocialSecurityTax` at line 26671, not in `ReferenceData.java`.**

Every other 2025 statutory dollar amount used elsewhere in the project (Child Tax Credit per child, Adoption Credit threshold, Form 8863 phaseout, etc.) is centralized in `ReferenceData.java`. The Social Security maximum should be too, especially because:

1. The Social Security Administration adjusts the wage base annually based on the national-average wage index. The 2026 value will be different.
2. The same constant is needed for several other Form 1040 lines (Schedule 2 line 12 for self-employed taxpayers, Form 8959 Additional Medicare Tax, etc.).
3. The Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act maximum (when implemented per Gap G-NEW-3) will share the same value for 2025 — single constant reused.

**Code fix sketch:** add to `ReferenceData.java`:

```java
public static final BigDecimal SOCIAL_SECURITY_WAGE_BASE = new BigDecimal("176100");
public static final BigDecimal SOCIAL_SECURITY_EMPLOYEE_RATE = new BigDecimal("0.062");
public static final BigDecimal MAX_EMPLOYEE_SOCIAL_SECURITY_TAX = new BigDecimal("10918.20");
public static final BigDecimal MAX_EMPLOYEE_TIER_1_RAILROAD_RETIREMENT_TAX = new BigDecimal("10918.20");
```

Replace the hardcoded `new BigDecimal("10918.20")` in `computeExcessSocialSecurityTax` with `ReferenceData.MAX_EMPLOYEE_SOCIAL_SECURITY_TAX`.

### Gap G-NEW-5 (LOW severity — Form 2350 extension payment not implemented)

**Schedule 3 line 10 covers payments made with Form 2350 in addition to Form 4868, but only Form 4868 is implemented.**

Form 2350 is the "Application for Extension of Time To File U.S. Income Tax Return" used by U.S. citizens or residents abroad who need additional time beyond the usual extension to qualify for the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Form 2555). A taxpayer who paid an estimated balance with their Form 2350 cannot include that payment on Schedule 3 line 10.

**Effect:** narrow user population (U.S. taxpayers abroad). The user can still claim the payment by entering it manually on the `31-other-payments` personal form as a line-13z write-in, with a description like "Form 2350 extension payment $X". The arithmetic comes out the same.

**Status:** narrow use case; mitigated by the line-13z workaround. The dependencies file does not currently track this gap; this audit raises it for visibility.

### Gap G-NEW-6 (LOW severity — section 1341 $3,000 threshold not enforced)

**Internal Revenue Code section 1341 requires the repayment to exceed $3,000 for the credit method to be available. The code does not enforce this threshold.**

The `31-other-payments` personal form collects two fields:
- `section1341RepaymentAmount` (informational)
- `section1341Credit` (the amount entered as Schedule 3 line 13b)

The user can save the form with a `section1341RepaymentAmount` of $1,500 (below the threshold) AND a non-zero `section1341Credit`. The code reads `section1341Credit` directly without cross-validating against `section1341RepaymentAmount`. The Internal Revenue Service would disallow the credit on examination.

**Effect:** a user who incorrectly takes the section-1341 credit method for a repayment under $3,000 would have a too-high Schedule 3 line 13b and an overstated Form 1040 line 31.

**Code fix sketch:** emit a non-blocking flag `SECTION_1341_REPAYMENT_BELOW_THRESHOLD` when both fields are populated and `section1341RepaymentAmount < $3,000`. The user can then either correct the values or override.

## 9. What this means for the audit

The line 31 computation is **substantively correct for the common case**. The arithmetic at every level (line 14 subtotal, line 15 grand total, Form 1040 line 31 transcription) follows the IRS rule. The eight live source-line populations (lines 9, 10, 11, 12, 13a, 13b, 13d, 13z) all match the IRS data flow.

The new gaps identified in this audit are:

- **G-NEW-1 (LOW)** — Schedule 3 line 13c (Form 3800 elective payment) is out of scope. Mitigated by the project's existing line-13z workaround; no code change needed unless Form 3800 enters scope generally.
- **G-NEW-2 (LOW)** — Schedule 3 line 11 "2+ employers" test counts W-2 entries instead of distinct Employer Identification Numbers. A taxpayer with a corrected W-2c plus original from the same employer could get an incorrect excess Social Security credit.
- **G-NEW-3 (LOW)** — Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax Act excess is not computed. Railroad employees with multiple railroad employers must work around this with a line-13z write-in or accept an under-claim.
- **G-NEW-4 (LOW)** — Maximum employee Social Security tax ($10,918.20) is hardcoded inline rather than centralized in `ReferenceData.java`.
- **G-NEW-5 (LOW)** — Form 2350 extension payments (narrow user population: U.S. taxpayers abroad) are not implemented. Mitigated by the line-13z workaround.
- **G-NEW-6 (LOW)** — The Internal Revenue Code section 1341 $3,000 repayment threshold is not enforced; a user can erroneously claim a credit on a smaller repayment.

None of these gaps affects the dollar amount of Form 1040 line 31 for a taxpayer whose situation does not involve the specific edge case (same-employer W-2 duplicate, railroad employment, Form 2350, or sub-$3,000 section-1341 repayment). The previously-documented G1 through G6 and V1 gaps in `dependencies/31.md` were all closed on 2026-04-21 and remain closed.
