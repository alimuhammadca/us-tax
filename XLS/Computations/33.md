## Quick orientation

The Form 1040 label for line 33 (per the actual 2025 IRS Form 1040 page 2):

> **"33   Add lines 25d, 26, and 32. These are your total payments ............... 33"**

In one sentence: line 33 is a **pure subtotal**. It is the simple arithmetic sum of three preceding lines on the payments side of Form 1040. No new credit, no new payment, and no taxpayer election or input is collected on this line. The taxpayer cannot put a number on line 33 directly; the value is mechanical.

Six facts to keep in mind throughout this document:

1. **Line 33 is arithmetic only.** It is the sum of three specific lines: 25d, 26, and 32. There is no rounding rule special to line 33 beyond the ordinary whole-dollar rounding that applies to every Form 1040 line.

2. **Line 33 covers every dollar the taxpayer has paid toward the 2025 tax (or is treated as having paid).** It carries withholding (line 25d), estimated tax payments and applied prior-year overpayments (line 26), and the refundable side of credits plus Schedule 3 Part II (line 32). It does **not** include the regular tax (line 16), the nonrefundable credits (line 22 / line 21), or any other addition-tax line. Those belong elsewhere.

3. **The taxpayer's signature on line 33 is mechanical**, but the components include taxpayer-entered amounts (estimated tax payments on line 26) and amounts that come straight from third-party reports (federal income tax withheld from W-2 box 2, 1099 box 4, and similar). Errors on line 33 are nearly always errors in one of the three addends, not on line 33 itself.

4. **Line 33 is the payments-side pivot for the refund-or-amount-owed determination.** The very next decision on Form 1040 — refund (lines 34, 35a, 36) versus amount owed (lines 37, 38) — is made by comparing line 33 to line 24 (total tax). When line 33 is greater than line 24 the taxpayer is overpaid; the excess becomes the refund (after any election on line 36 to credit the next year's estimated tax). When line 24 is greater than line 33 the taxpayer owes the difference, possibly with an underpayment penalty stacked on top via Form 2210.

5. **A negative line 33 is structurally impossible.** Each addend is bounded below by zero. Withholding cannot be negative (you cannot have a negative box-2 amount on a W-2). Estimated tax payments cannot be negative (the taxpayer cannot send the Internal Revenue Service a negative dollar). The refundable credits and the Schedule 3 line 15 payments composing line 32 are all non-negative by their own definitions. If any addend computes to a negative number, the upstream computation is wrong; that should be corrected at the source, not by clamping line 33.

6. **Line 33 has no IRS reference data of its own.** No rates, no thresholds, no caps apply directly to line 33. Every constant that moves line 33 from one tax year to the next moves it by way of one of the three addends. Those constants are documented at length in `XLS\Computations\25a.md` (for withholding), `XLS\Computations\26.md` (for estimated tax payments), and `XLS\Computations\32.md` (for refundable credits). Section 5 of this file is a small index, not a reproduction, of those constants.

The equation:

```text
Form 1040 line 33 = Form 1040 line 25d
                  + Form 1040 line 26
                  + Form 1040 line 32

Form 1040 line 25d = Form 1040 line 25a + Form 1040 line 25b + Form 1040 line 25c
Form 1040 line 32  = Form 1040 line 27a + Form 1040 line 28 + Form 1040 line 29
                   + Form 1040 line 30  + Form 1040 line 31
```

Each of the three addends is described in section 1 below.

## 1. The Internal Revenue Service canonical computation

### 1a. The exact equation

```text
Form 1040 line 33 = (Form 1040 line 25d)
                  + (Form 1040 line 26)
                  + (Form 1040 line 32)
```

All three addends are non-negative. Each addend is rounded to whole dollars before it is placed on its own Form 1040 line; line 33 is then the whole-dollar sum.

There is no IRS reference data (no rates, no thresholds, no caps) that applies directly to line 33 itself. The reference data applies to the **upstream** computations of lines 25d, 26, and 32. Section 5 lists those constants by reference to their owning computation file.

### 1b. What each addend means

**Line 25d — Total federal income tax withheld.**

Plain English: Line 25d is the grand total of every dollar of federal income tax that was withheld from the taxpayer's pay or other reportable amounts during 2025. It is itself a sum of three sublines:

```text
Form 1040 line 25a = sum of W-2 box 2 amounts across every uploaded W-2 statement
Form 1040 line 25b = sum of federal income tax withheld from 1099-series statements
                     (1099-R, 1099-INT, 1099-DIV, 1099-B, 1099-OID, 1099-G, 1099-NEC,
                      1099-K, 1099-MISC, 1099-DA — all use box 4)
                   + SSA-1099 box 6 (voluntary federal income tax withheld from Social Security)
                   + RRB-1099 box 10 (railroad retirement Tier 1 benefits federal tax withheld)
                   + RRB-1099-R box 9 (railroad retirement Tier 2 / pension federal tax withheld)
Form 1040 line 25c = sum of W-2G box 4 amounts (federal income tax withheld from gambling winnings)
                   + Form 8959 line 24 (Additional Medicare Tax withholding adjustment, computed
                     on a separate Form 8959 for taxpayers with high wages or self-employment income)
Form 1040 line 25d = Form 1040 line 25a + Form 1040 line 25b + Form 1040 line 25c
```

Withholding is "deemed paid evenly throughout the year" for Form 2210 underpayment-penalty purposes. The taxpayer cannot allocate withholding to specific quarters except by election under Box D (out of scope in this implementation). The full detail of line 25d is in `XLS\Computations\25a.md`, `25b.md`, `25c.md`, and `25d.md`.

**Line 26 — Estimated tax payments and prior-year overpayment credited to 2025.**

Plain English: Line 26 is the total of every quarterly estimated tax payment the taxpayer made for 2025 (typically using Form 1040-ES), plus any 2024 overpayment the taxpayer chose to credit forward to 2025 (whether elected on the original 2024 return or via a subsequent amendment on Form 1040-X). The equation:

```text
Form 1040 line 26 = installment 1 (Q1 — due April 15, 2025)
                  + installment 2 (Q2 — due June 16, 2025)
                  + installment 3 (Q3 — due September 15, 2025)
                  + installment 4 (Q4 — due January 15, 2026)
                  + prior-year overpayment credited to 2025 from the original 2024 return
                  + prior-year overpayment credited to 2025 from an amended 2024 return
```

For married filing jointly the two spouses' estimated tax payments are combined on the joint return's line 26. For married filing separately each spouse reports only their own payments.

The taxpayer does not have to actually identify which dollars belong to which installment for the purpose of line 26 itself; the line 26 total is a simple sum across all six possible buckets above. The installment-level identification matters separately for Form 2210 (line 38 underpayment penalty), which is computed downstream. The full detail of line 26 is in `XLS\Computations\26.md`.

**Line 32 — Total other payments and refundable credits.**

Plain English: Line 32 is the grand total of refundable credits the taxpayer claimed for 2025 plus the Schedule 3 Part II payments-and-refundable-credits subtotal. It is itself a five-addend sum:

```text
Form 1040 line 32 = Form 1040 line 27a (Earned Income Credit)
                  + Form 1040 line 28  (Additional Child Tax Credit — Schedule 8812 Part II-A)
                  + Form 1040 line 29  (Refundable American Opportunity Credit — Form 8863 line 8)
                  + Form 1040 line 30  (Refundable Adoption Credit — Form 8839 Part II line 13;
                                        new for tax year 2025)
                  + Form 1040 line 31  (Amount from Schedule 3 line 15, which contains the
                                        Net Premium Tax Credit, the Form 4868 extension payment,
                                        excess Social Security and Tier 1 Railroad Retirement Tax,
                                        the federal tax on fuels credit, and the Section 1341
                                        / Form 2439 / Form 3800 / Section 965 / other refundable
                                        credit items)
```

For tax year 2025 the IRS reassigned line 30 to the Refundable Adoption Credit (Form 8839 Part II line 13, capped at $5,000 per child). In tax year 2024 line 30 held the Net Premium Tax Credit; that credit moved into Schedule 3 line 9 (and flows to Form 1040 line 31) starting in tax year 2025. Anything in the codebase that still names line 30 as the Net Premium Tax Credit is referring to the 2024 form, not the 2025 form.

The full detail of line 32 is in `XLS\Computations\32.md`. The seven downstream Form 1040 lines that follow line 33 (lines 34 through 38) are described in section 2 below.

### 1c. Why each addend is a separate line

The reason the IRS keeps lines 25d, 26, and 32 separate (instead of letting taxpayers just write a single "total payments" amount) is auditability:

- **Line 25d** matches third-party reports the IRS already received (W-2 copies, 1099 copies). The IRS can cross-check this number without asking the taxpayer for documentation.
- **Line 26** is what the taxpayer paid directly to the IRS during the year (and via the credit-forward election from the prior year). The IRS has its own internal record of payments received against each taxpayer identification number and can cross-check that record against line 26.
- **Line 32** is the refundable-credit subtotal. Each component has its own substantiation form (Schedule 8812 for the Additional Child Tax Credit, Form 8863 for the refundable American Opportunity Credit, Form 8839 for the Refundable Adoption Credit, Schedule 3 for the Premium Tax Credit and other payments). The IRS recomputes each component when processing the return.

If any of the three addends is off by a dollar, the IRS will find it; the discrepancy is between two sources, not within one number. Keeping the addends separate makes the corresponding correction transparent.

### 1d. Rules that apply to line 33 itself

There are exactly two:

1. **Line 33 must equal line 25d plus line 26 plus line 32 exactly.** No other addends are allowed.
2. **Line 33 must be non-negative.** Every addend is bounded below by zero and the sum of non-negatives is non-negative.

The Internal Revenue Service prints both rules on the actual Form 1040 line 33 label by saying "Add lines 25d, 26, and 32. These are your total payments." There is no "but" clause, no minimum threshold, and no exception.

## 2. The as-implemented computation (this codebase)

### 2a. Backend (Java) — Where line 33 is computed

Per `src\main\java\com\ustax\microservices\TaxReturnComputeService.java`, line 33 is computed inside `computeLine31ThroughLine38(...)` at lines 27293–27298 (the line numbers may shift across commits; the comment marker `// ── Line 33: total payments` is stable):

```java
// ── Line 33: total payments ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
// line33 = line25d + line26 + line32
BigDecimal line33 = roundMoney(safeAmount(totalWithholding)
        .add(safeAmount(line26))
        .add(safeAmount(line32)));
payments.setTotalPayments(line33.compareTo(BigDecimal.ZERO) > 0 ? line33 : null);
```

What each name in this block refers to:

| Local variable | Meaning | Computed earlier in `computeLine31ThroughLine38` |
|---|---|---|
| `totalWithholding` | line 25d — total federal income tax withheld | `BigDecimal totalWithholding = roundMoney(safeAmount(line25a).add(safeAmount(line25b)).add(safeAmount(line25c)));` |
| `line26` | line 26 — estimated tax payments plus prior-year overpayment credited | `computeLine26EstimatedTax(...)` |
| `line32` | line 32 — total other payments and refundable credits | five-addend sum at lines 27270–27274 |
| `roundMoney` | rounds a BigDecimal to whole dollars (zero scale, half-even rounding) | shared utility |
| `safeAmount` | replaces a null BigDecimal with BigDecimal.ZERO | shared utility |
| `payments` | the `Payments` Java output model for this return | written to `Form1040.payments` |

Translated into plain English, the code does exactly what the IRS canonical equation in section 1a calls for:

1. Treat `totalWithholding`, `line26`, and `line32` as their face values when present, or zero when null. This is what the `safeAmount(...)` calls do.
2. Add the three of them.
3. Round the sum to whole dollars.
4. Store the result on `Payments.totalPayments`, except: if the rounded sum is zero or less, store `null` instead of `BigDecimal.ZERO`. This last decision is the **null-when-zero storage convention** described in section 6 gap E.

The next thirty or so lines (27300–27360) of the same method use `line33` (not `payments.getTotalPayments()`) to drive the refund-or-amount-owed branch:

```java
BigDecimal totalTax = safeAmount(form1040.getTaxAndCredits() != null
        ? form1040.getTaxAndCredits().getTotalTax() : null);

if (line33.compareTo(totalTax) > 0) {
    // Line 34: overpayment = line33 − totalTax
    BigDecimal overpaid = roundMoney(line33.subtract(totalTax));
    // …line 36 cap, line 35a refund amount, lines 35b/c/d direct-deposit details…
} else if (totalTax.compareTo(line33) > 0) {
    // Line 37: amount owed = totalTax − line33
    // …
}
// otherwise line33 = totalTax exactly: neither refund nor amount owed
```

Because the local `line33` BigDecimal is used directly for the comparison, the null-when-zero storage convention has no effect on the refund-or-amount-owed determination. The variable holds the true sum (including a zero); only the stored copy on `Payments` is collapsed to `null` for a zero.

### 2b. Backend — Compute order

Per `dependencies\33.md` section 3, `computeLine31ThroughLine38()` is the thirteenth step in the overall compute sequence. Every upstream computation has finished before line 33 is built. The relevant excerpt:

```text

3.  computeForm8863()                     → form8863.line8RefundableAotc (line 29)
4.  computeSchedule8812()                 → ACTC pre-set for line 28 (refined inside step 13)
5.  applyAdoptionCredit()                 → finalizes refundable adoption credit (line 30)
6.  applyPremiumTaxCreditToSchedule3()    → Schedule 3 line 9
7.  applyForm4868ToSchedule3()            → Schedule 3 line 10
8.  computeExcessSocialSecurityTax()      → Schedule 3 line 11
9.  applyForm2439CreditToSchedule3()      → Schedule 3 line 13a
10. applyOtherPaymentsFormToSchedule3()   → Schedule 3 lines 13b / 13c / 13d / 13z
11. finalizeSchedule3Totals()             → Schedule 3 line 15 (becomes line 31)
12. computeLine20ThroughLine24()          → line 22 (tax after credits) and line 24 (total tax)
13. computeLine31ThroughLine38() {
       line25a/25b/25c from withholding helpers   → set line 25d
       computeLine26EstimatedTax()                → set line 26
       computeLine27aEIC()                        → set line 27a (refined inside this method)
       re-set line 29 from form8863
       re-set line 30 from adoption
       set line 31 from schedule3 line 15
       compute line 32 = 27a + 28 + 29 + 30 + 31
```

Line 33 is computed exactly once per return inside step 13. There is no second pass and no override path.

### 2c. Frontend (Angular) — Where line 33 is read for the preview and PDF export

Per `src\app\forms\form-tax-return-1040.component.ts` at lines 932–939:

```typescript
protected line33TotalPayments(): number | null {
  const payments = this.form1040()?.payments;
  if (!payments) return null;
  const totalPayments = this.toNumber(payments.totalPayments);
  if (totalPayments !== null) return totalPayments;
  const line32 = this.line32OtherPayments();
  return this.sumAmounts([this.line25dTotalWithholding(), payments.estimatedTaxPayments, line32]);
}
```

In plain English: if the backend stored a value for line 33 the frontend uses that value as-is; if the stored value is `null` the frontend recomputes the total client-side from the three component values. The fallback handles the null-when-zero storage convention (section 6 gap B) and the case where an older saved return has the three components but not yet a stored line 33 total. The three component reads themselves are:

- `this.line25dTotalWithholding()` — reads `payments.totalWithholding`; if null, sums `payments.withholdingW2 + payments.withholding1099 + payments.withholdingOther`.
- `payments.estimatedTaxPayments` — the raw line 26 field from the output model.
- `this.line32OtherPayments()` — reads `payments.totalOtherPaymentsAndRefundableCredits`; if null, sums the five line 32 addends.

So the frontend exhibits the same fallback-on-null pattern at every level (lines 25d, 26 implicit, 32, and 33). The behavior is consistent.

The frontend then writes the line 33 value into the PDF field map for the **Save as PDF** export at line 856:

```typescript
values['line33_total_payments'] = this.formatAmount(this.line33TotalPayments());
```

This semantic key (`line33_total_payments`) matches the 2025 IRS Form 1040 PDF field map exactly. **Unlike line 32, line 33 is correctly wired to its 2025 PDF field key.** This is one of the few lines on page 2 of Form 1040 where the frontend does not have the stale-2024-key bug described in `XLS\Computations\32.md` gap A.

### 2d. The as-implemented equation in one line

```text
line33 = max(0, round_to_whole_dollar(line25d + line26 + line32))
       stored as null if zero, else stored as the rounded BigDecimal
```

The `max(0, …)` clamp is structural — the inputs are all non-negative by construction — but it is not explicitly enforced in the code. See gap A in section 6 below.

## 6. Gap analysis — bugs, shortcomings, and missing fields

> Our objective is correctness. The following gaps are written from the standpoint of validating the application against the IRS 2025 Form 1040, not the other way around.

### Gap A — Line 33 has no non-negativity guardrail in the backend ✅ **CLOSED 2026-06-12**

Evidence: `TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine31ThroughLine38()` at the line-33 assignment:

```java
BigDecimal line33 = roundMoney(safeAmount(totalWithholding)
        .add(safeAmount(line26))
        .add(safeAmount(line32)));
payments.setTotalPayments(line33.compareTo(BigDecimal.ZERO) > 0 ? line33 : null);
```

Line 32 received an explicit non-negativity guardrail in commit `4f697c2` (line 33 audit, 2026-06-12): if any of the five line-32 addends or the resulting line-32 sum is negative, the backend logs a warning via `LOG.warnf(…)`. The same defensive guardrail is **absent** for line 33.

The three line-33 addends are individually non-negative by IRS construction:

- Line 25d is a sum of withholding amounts, each from a third-party report; payors cannot report negative withholding.
- Line 26 is a sum of taxpayer-initiated payments to the IRS plus credited prior-year overpayments. None of these can be negative.
- Line 32 is the line-32 subtotal, which is now guardrailed to log a warning if it goes negative.

So in normal operation line 33 is always non-negative. The gap is the absence of a defensive guardrail that would surface a future bug in any of the three addends.

Effect: a future code change that accidentally signs a withholding amount as negative (for example, by misreading a refund-of-withholding reversal as a negative box-2 amount), or that introduces a signed quantity into line 26, or that produces a negative line 32, would silently produce a negative line 33. The refund branch `if (line33.compareTo(totalTax) > 0)` would never fire, the amount-owed branch would fire with an inflated balance due, and the taxpayer would be told they owe more than they actually do.

Recommended fix: parallel the line 32 guardrail. After computing `line33`, log a warning if any addend or the sum is negative.

```java
BigDecimal line33_line25d = safeAmount(totalWithholding);
BigDecimal line33_line26  = safeAmount(line26);
BigDecimal line33_line32  = safeAmount(line32);
BigDecimal line33 = roundMoney(line33_line25d.add(line33_line26).add(line33_line32));
if (line33_line25d.signum() < 0
        || line33_line26.signum() < 0
        || line33_line32.signum() < 0
        || line33.signum() < 0) {
    LOG.warnf("Line 33 non-negativity guardrail tripped — line25d=%s line26=%s line32=%s line33=%s",
            line33_line25d, line33_line26, line33_line32, line33);
}
payments.setTotalPayments(line33.compareTo(BigDecimal.ZERO) > 0 ? line33 : null);
```

The cost is one local variable per addend and one `if` block. The benefit is that any future sign regression in an addend would be immediately visible in the logs.

**Resolution (2026-06-12):** the guardrail was applied in `TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine31ThroughLine38()` directly after the line-33 sum. Each addend is captured in a named local (`line33_line25d`, `line33_line26`, `line33_line32`) and a single `if` block calls `LOG.warnf` when any addend or the resulting sum is negative. Computation continues; the guardrail is a defensive paper trail, not a hard block. The existing line-32 / line-33 test cluster (four tests) still passes.

### Gap B — Implementation convention: line 33 stored as `null` when the sum is zero ✅ **DOCUMENTED 2026-06-12**

Evidence: the `payments.setTotalPayments(...)` call at the end of the line-33 assignment:

```java
payments.setTotalPayments(line33.compareTo(BigDecimal.ZERO) > 0 ? line33 : null);
```

When the rounded `line33` is exactly zero (which happens when withholding is zero, the taxpayer made no estimated payments, and no refundable credits apply), the setter receives `null` instead of `BigDecimal.ZERO`.

Effect on IRS correctness: none. Storing `null` instead of `0` does not change the printed Form 1040 value (a blank box is equivalent to zero), does not change line 24 versus line 33 comparison (a `null` line 33 still triggers the amount-owed branch via the local variable `line33` itself, which is `BigDecimal.ZERO`), and does not change the refund-or-amount-owed determination.

Effect on developer experience: minor. Any consumer that reads `payments.totalPayments` and does not pass through `safeAmount()` would see `null` and may produce a runtime error or a display inconsistency. The Angular `line33TotalPayments()` fallback at lines 932–939 already handles this by recomputing client-side from the three components when the stored value is null.

This is a project convention, not a bug. The note here is so the convention is documented for line 33 in one place, parallel to `XLS\Computations\32.md` gap E for line 32.

**Resolution (2026-06-12):** Javadoc was added to `Payments.setTotalPayments(BigDecimal)` (and to `Payments.setTotalOtherPaymentsAndRefundableCredits(BigDecimal)` for symmetry) describing the null-when-zero storage convention. The Javadoc explicitly tells consumers to null-check or pass the value through `safeAmount(...)` before arithmetic, points out that the convention does not affect Form 1040 printed values or the refund-or-amount-owed determination, and cross-references this gap.

### Gap C — Frontend fallback uses `payments.estimatedTaxPayments` directly without applying `sumAmounts` semantics for null

Evidence: `form-tax-return-1040.component.ts` at line 938:

```typescript
return this.sumAmounts([this.line25dTotalWithholding(), payments.estimatedTaxPayments, line32]);
```

`payments.estimatedTaxPayments` is the raw line-26 field on the output model. If the backend stored it as `null` (when line 26 is exactly zero — same convention as gap B at the line-26 setter), the frontend fallback would pass `null` into `sumAmounts(...)`, which is the correct behavior (`sumAmounts` treats null as zero).

If, however, the backend has been changed to store line 26 as zero in a future version, or if a future migration replaces `null` with `0` for historical data, the frontend would still sum correctly. So this is not actually a gap; the behavior is robust under both conventions.

Document this as a non-gap so a future audit does not relitigate it. No code change needed.

### Gap D — No explicit unit test for line 33 when all three addends are simultaneously non-zero ✅ **CLOSED 2026-06-12**

Evidence: `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.java` contains:

- `line33_line36_reducesRefundAmount()` — tests line 33 in the context of line 36 reducing the refund. The fixture has line 25d > 0 and either line 26 or line 32 zero.
- `line33_equals_line24_neither_refund_nor_amountOwed()` — tests the exactly-balanced edge case.
- No test fixture has line 25d, line 26, and line 32 simultaneously non-zero with a specific hand-computed pin for line 33.

Effect: a regression that broke the sum when more than two addends contribute would not be caught by the existing tests. Likelihood is low because the sum is a straight `BigDecimal.add` chain, but the lack of a three-addend test is a residual gap. Mirrors `XLS\Computations\32.md` gap D for line 32.

Recommended fix: add a unit test along the lines of `line33_allThreeAddendsContributeSimultaneously` that sets withholding, estimated tax payments, and a line-32 refundable credit each to a different non-trivial dollar amount, computes the return, and pins `payments.totalPayments` to the hand-summed total.

**Resolution (2026-06-12):** the test `line33_allThreeAddendsContributeSimultaneously` was added to `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.java`. Fixture: single filer with one Child Tax Credit qualifying child, W-2 wages of $30,000 with $1,500 of federal income tax withheld (line 25d), estimated tax payment installment 1 of $750 (line 26), and the Additional Child Tax Credit firing of $728 (line 28, the line-32 contribution). The test asserts each addend is non-null and positive, then pins `payments.totalPayments` to the hand-summed total ($1,500 + $750 + $728 = $2,978). The test passes against the current backend.

### Gap E — No frontend unit test for the `line33TotalPayments()` fallback path ✅ **CLOSED 2026-06-12**

Evidence: there is no Angular unit test for `form-tax-return-1040.component.ts` `line33TotalPayments()` that asserts the fallback (compute client-side from three components) returns the correct sum when `payments.totalPayments` is null.

## 7. Summary

The IRS canonical equation for Form 1040 line 33 in tax year 2025:

```text
Form 1040 line 33 = Form 1040 line 25d (total federal income tax withheld)
                  + Form 1040 line 26  (estimated tax payments plus prior-year overpayment)
                  + Form 1040 line 32  (total other payments and refundable credits)
```

The as-implemented computation in this codebase is **arithmetically correct** at every layer:

- **Backend** (`TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine31ThroughLine38()`) sums the three addends in the order required by the IRS, rounds to whole dollars, and stores the result on `Payments.totalPayments` (collapsed to `null` when zero, per the project convention).
- **Frontend** (`form-tax-return-1040.component.ts`'s `line33TotalPayments()`) prefers the backend-stored value and falls back to a client-side sum of the three components when the stored value is null.
- **PDF export** writes the line-33 value to the correct 2025 PDF field key `line33_total_payments`. This is one of the few lines on page 2 of Form 1040 where the frontend does not have the stale-2024-key bug described in `XLS\Computations\32.md` gap A.
- **Output model and YAML schema** name and type the field consistently (`Payments.totalPayments`, BigDecimal, `formField: line33_total_payments`).

The gaps are all low-severity and defensive. **As of 2026-06-12 all four fixable gaps are closed:**

1. ✅ **Non-negativity guardrail on line 33** (gap A) — added in `TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine31ThroughLine38()` parallel to the line-32 guardrail. Logs a warning if any addend or the sum is negative.
2. ✅ **Unit test where all three addends are simultaneously non-zero** (gap D) — `line33_allThreeAddendsContributeSimultaneously` added to `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.java`. Pins `payments.totalPayments` to the hand-summed total ($2,978 = $1,500 + $750 + $728). Passes against the current backend.
3. ✅ **Frontend Jasmine unit test for the `line33TotalPayments()` fallback** (gap E) — four-case `describe` block added to `form-tax-return-1040.component.spec.ts` covering stored value, top-level fallback, sub-fallback chain, and null-computation edge cases.
4. ✅ **Null-when-zero storage convention documented** (gap B) — Javadoc added to `Payments.setTotalPayments(BigDecimal)` and `Payments.setTotalOtherPaymentsAndRefundableCredits(BigDecimal)`. Cross-references the gap so future readers do not need to dig through the compute service.

The remaining gaps (C, F, G, H, I) are not bugs — they are positive verifications that the implementation is correct (no missing inputs, no race condition, no circular dependency, no output-channel inconsistency, fallback handles null robustly). They are documented for future readers so that a later audit does not relitigate them.

None of the gaps above change the **IRS-canonical formula**, which remains a three-addend whole-dollar sum. None of the gaps cause an incorrect tax return today; they all guard against possible future regressions in upstream computations or in PDF / output wiring.

The two upstream gaps that **do** affect taxpayer-visible behavior (gap A and gap B in `XLS\Computations\32.md` — the frontend writes lines 29, 30, 31, and 32 to stale 2024-era PDF field keys, and the line 30 refundable adoption credit is silently dropped from the exported PDF) are out of scope for this file; they are documented in `32.md`. Line 33 itself is correctly emitted to its 2025 PDF field key, so the line-33 box on the exported PDF is correct.

The bottom-line correctness verdict for Form 1040 line 33 in tax year 2025:

> **The line 33 computation is faithfully implemented. The Internal Revenue Service canonical formula and the as-implemented formula agree dollar for dollar. The two implementations diverge only on the null-when-zero storage convention, which has no effect on the printed return or on the refund-or-amount-owed determination.**
