## Quick orientation

The Form 1040 label for line 35d (per the actual 2025 IRS Form 1040 page 2):

> **"d   Account number"**

In one sentence: line 35d is a **conditional string passthrough**. It is the taxpayer's bank account number — the destination account at the financial institution that will receive a direct-deposited refund. It is not computed and not arithmetic. The taxpayer types it into an intake form; the backend reads it; the value is copied verbatim onto the printed Form 1040 in the line-35d box. Line 35d fires only when the taxpayer (a) has an overpayment, (b) elects direct deposit, and (c) has not elected the Form 8888 split-refund alternative.

Seven facts to keep in mind throughout this document:

1. **Line 35d is not arithmetic.** Nothing is added, subtracted, multiplied, or compared. The field is a pure passthrough from the taxpayer's intake input to the printed return.

2. **Line 35d is conditional on three gates.** It fires when all three are true: line 34 (overpaid) is positive (otherwise there is no refund to deposit anywhere), the taxpayer answered "Yes" to "Do you want direct deposit?" on the intake form, and the taxpayer has not elected Form 8888 split refund. When any gate fails, line 35d is blank.

3. **Line 35d is a sibling of lines 35b and 35c.** Together the three lines describe one direct-deposit destination: 35b is the bank's 9-digit American Bankers Association (ABA) routing number, 35c is a checkbox pair (checking or savings), and 35d is the deposit account number at that bank. All three must be present together or all three must be blank — they cannot be partially filled.

4. **Line 35d is mutually exclusive with Form 8888.** When the taxpayer elects to split the refund across two or three accounts, Form 8888 supplies the per-account routing/type/number for each account, and lines 35b/35c/35d on the Form 1040 itself must be blank. The IRS treats a Form 1040 that has both a Form 8888 attached and a populated line 35d as an error and may delay the refund while the IRS resolves the contradiction by hand.

5. **Line 35d has a length limit but no checksum.** The IRS specifies that the account number may be up to 17 characters and may include letters, digits, and hyphens but no spaces. Unlike line 35b (the routing number, which carries an ABA Mod-10 checksum), the account number itself is not self-validating — the IRS cannot tell from the account number alone whether it is correct.

6. **Line 35d is the second of two responsibility-shift fields.** Once the taxpayer signs the return, the IRS holds them responsible for the correctness of lines 35b and 35d. If the routing number passes the ABA checksum and the account number is the correct length, the IRS sends the refund to whatever bank/account that pair identifies — even if the taxpayer typed the wrong digits and the money lands in a stranger's account. The IRS will not recover funds sent to a wrong account; the taxpayer must contact the receiving bank directly.

7. **Line 35d has no reference data of its own.** No 2025 IRS rate, threshold, or constant applies to the account number itself. The only constants relevant to line 35d are the 17-character maximum length and the allowed-characters rule (alphanumeric plus hyphen).

The equation:

```text
if (Form 1040 line 34 > 0)
   AND (taxpayer elects direct deposit)
   AND (Form 8888 split refund is NOT elected):
       Form 1040 line 35d = (the account number string the taxpayer typed)
else:
       Form 1040 line 35d = blank
```

There is no arithmetic. The conditional is a triple gate; the result of the gate is either "copy the typed string verbatim" or "leave the line blank."

## 1. The Internal Revenue Service canonical content

### 1a. The exact rule

```text
if (Form 1040 line 34) > 0
   AND (intake answer to "Do you want direct deposit?") = "Yes"
   AND (Form 8888 split refund election) ≠ "Yes":
       (Form 1040 line 35d) = (taxpayer-typed account number)
else:
       (Form 1040 line 35d) = blank
```

There is no arithmetic. The "computation" is the conditional decision to either copy the typed string onto the return or leave the line blank.

### 1b. What line 35d means

**Account number — Plain English.**

The account number is the identifier the receiving bank uses to route the money to the taxpayer's specific account once it arrives at that bank. The 9-digit American Bankers Association (ABA) routing number on line 35b gets the money to the right bank; the account number on line 35d gets it to the right account within that bank.

The account number is printed at the bottom of a paper check, typically as the longest digit string between the routing number on the left and the check number on the right. For a checking account that has no paper checks, it is also visible in the taxpayer's online banking portal under "Account details" or similar.

The account number is independent of the taxpayer's identity. The IRS does not verify that the account number belongs to the taxpayer named on the return — the IRS only verifies that the account number reaches a valid account at the bank specified by the routing number. If the taxpayer typed an account number that belongs to someone else (intentionally or by mistake), and the routing number is valid, the IRS will send the refund there and consider its obligation satisfied. Recovering the misdirected funds is the taxpayer's problem.

### 1c. The three IRS validation rules that apply to line 35d itself

There are exactly three rules in the 2025 Form 1040 Instructions and Form 8888 instructions that bound what the account number on line 35d may contain:

1. **Maximum length 17 characters.** The PDF field on Form 1040 is a 17-character text field. If the taxpayer's actual account number is longer (some money-market accounts have 18+ digit account numbers), the taxpayer must omit leading zeros until the number fits, or must check with the bank to confirm a shorter form is accepted, or must choose paper check delivery instead.
2. **Allowed characters: digits 0-9, letters A-Z, and the hyphen "-". No spaces.** The IRS Document 1346 (e-file specifications) defines the character class. Anything else (slashes, periods, parentheses) is a rejection.
3. **The account number alone is not validated.** Unlike the routing number on line 35b (which carries the ABA Mod-10 checksum), the account number has no self-check. The IRS accepts any 17-or-fewer-character alphanumeric-plus-hyphen string at face value.

These three rules apply to the account number string regardless of any other characteristic of the return.

### 1d. The triple gate that decides whether line 35d is populated or blank

The three gates are evaluated in order; any one being false suppresses line 35d:

**Gate 1 — Is there an overpayment to deposit?** Line 34 (amount overpaid, computed earlier as `line 33 − line 24`) must be greater than zero. When line 24 ≥ line 33, the taxpayer owes money rather than getting a refund, and direct-deposit lines 35b/35c/35d are meaningless. When this gate fails, all of lines 35a/35b/35c/35d are blank.

**Gate 2 — Did the taxpayer elect direct deposit?** The taxpayer must have answered "Yes" to "Would you like your refund deposited directly into a bank account?" on the intake form (the unified Refund and Amount Owed form, or the legacy 35-direct-deposit form). When the taxpayer chose "No," the IRS mails a paper check for the line-35a refund amount, and lines 35b/35c/35d are blank.

**Gate 3 — Is the taxpayer NOT splitting the refund via Form 8888?** When the taxpayer elects Form 8888 (split refund across up to three accounts), Form 8888 supplies the per-account routing/type/number for each account, and Form 1040 lines 35b/35c/35d MUST be blank. The IRS rule is exact: a Form 1040 with both Form 8888 attached and a populated line 35d is an error. The Form 8888 election is recorded in the unified Refund and Amount Owed form (or in the legacy refund-allocation-taxpayer form) as `wantsRefundAllocation = true`.

Only when all three gates are true does line 35d copy the taxpayer-typed account number verbatim. When any gate is false, line 35d is blank.

### 1e. Why line 35d is conditional rather than always-set

The IRS leaves line 35d blank rather than printing a placeholder for three reasons:

1. **The line label is a destination, not a value.** "Account number" is a routing instruction. It has meaning only when there is a refund (line 34 > 0) and only when the taxpayer wants that refund deposited rather than mailed.
2. **Paper-check delivery has no routing destination.** When the taxpayer chose paper check, lines 35b/35c/35d are not just "unused" — they are structurally meaningless. There is no account to identify.
3. **The Form 8888 mutual-exclusivity rule.** When Form 8888 is attached, the per-account routing/type/number lives inside Form 8888 itself, and the Form 1040 destination fields must be blank to avoid contradiction. Treating line 35d as conditional rather than always-set lets the same row on Form 1040 represent three completely different delivery decisions (paper check, direct deposit to one account, or split via Form 8888) without ambiguity.

### 1f. The four rules that govern line 35d alongside line 35b and line 35c

Lines 35b (routing number), 35c (account type), and 35d (account number) are a tightly coupled triple. The four IRS rules that govern the triple as a unit:

1. **All three or none.** A direct-deposit destination requires all three of line 35b, line 35c, and line 35d. The IRS rejects a return that has any two of the three present and the third blank.
2. **Account type is exactly one of checking or savings.** Line 35c is a pair of checkboxes; exactly one must be checked when the triple fires. The IRS rejects "both" and rejects "neither" when line 35b and line 35d are populated.
3. **The routing number passes the ABA Mod-10 checksum.** Line 35b must be exactly 9 digits and must pass the American Bankers Association checksum (weights 3-7-1-3-7-1-3-7-1; sum divisible by 10). When the routing number fails the checksum, the IRS may convert the refund to a paper check rather than risk an incorrect deposit.
4. **The account number does not exceed 17 characters and uses only allowed characters.** Per section 1c above.

When all four rules are satisfied AND the triple gate of section 1d is true, lines 35b/35c/35d together identify one direct-deposit destination for the line-35a refund.

## 2. The as-implemented behavior (this codebase)

### 2a. Backend (Java) — Where line 35d is set

Per `src\main\java\com\ustax\microservices\TaxReturnComputeService.java`, line 35d is set inside `computeLine31ThroughLine38(...)` at lines 27368-27371, deep inside the overpayment branch (which fires only when `line33 > totalTax`, satisfying Gate 1 of section 1d):

```java
// ── Lines 35b/c/d: direct deposit details ───────────────────────────────────────────
// Only populate when Form 8888 split refund is NOT elected.
boolean hasSplitRefund = refundAllocationData != null
        && Boolean.TRUE.equals(getBoolean(refundAllocationData, "wantsRefundAllocation"));
if (!hasSplitRefund && directDepositData != null) {              // Gate 3 + presence check
    Boolean wantsDd = getBoolean(directDepositData, "wantsDirectDeposit");
    if (Boolean.TRUE.equals(wantsDd)) {                          // Gate 2
        refund.setDirectDeposit(true);
        String routing = getString(directDepositData, "routingNumber");
        if (routing != null && !routing.isBlank()) {
            refund.setRoutingNumber(routing);                    // line 35b
        }
        String acctType = getString(directDepositData, "accountType");
        if (acctType != null && !acctType.isBlank()) {
            refund.setAccountType(acctType);                     // line 35c
        }
        String acctNum = getString(directDepositData, "accountNumber");
        if (acctNum != null && !acctNum.isBlank()) {
            refund.setAccountNumber(acctNum);                    // line 35d  ← LINE 35d SET HERE
        }
    }
}
```

What each name in this block refers to:

| Local variable | Meaning | Source |
|---|---|---|
| `refundAllocationData` | The Map representing the saved unified Refund and Amount Owed form, OR the legacy refund-allocation-taxpayer form (Form 8888 election container) | resolved at line 206 of the same file |
| `directDepositData` | The Map representing the saved unified Refund and Amount Owed form, OR the legacy 35-direct-deposit form (lines 35b/35c/35d source) | resolved at line 208 of the same file |
| `hasSplitRefund` | Boolean: was Form 8888 split refund elected? Implements Gate 3 | local |
| `wantsDd` | Boolean: did the taxpayer elect direct deposit? Implements Gate 2 | local |
| `acctNum` | The trimmed account number string the taxpayer typed | local |
| `refund` | The `Refund` Java output model, lazily created earlier in the same method when `line 33 > totalTax` (Gate 1 — see `34.md` gap A) | shared output model |

Translated into plain English, the code does the following:

1. Check whether Form 8888 was elected. If yes, skip the entire direct-deposit block (Gate 3 fails → line 35d blank).
2. Check whether the legacy 35-direct-deposit / unified-RAO form exists at all. If not, skip (no input source).
3. Check whether the taxpayer answered "Yes" to direct deposit. If not, skip (Gate 2 fails).
4. Read the account number string from the intake form. If it is non-null and non-blank (after trim), set `refund.accountNumber = acctNum`. Otherwise leave it null.

The entire block is inside the `if (line33.compareTo(totalTax) > 0)` branch — Gate 1 of section 1d. When there is no overpayment, the Refund object is not even created and line 35d is unreachable.

### 2b. Backend — Storage convention: null-when-absent, not empty-string

The setter at line 27370 is only called when the account number string is both non-null AND non-blank. This is the **null-when-absent storage convention** (parallel to the null-when-zero convention for line 35a in `35a.md` gap A and the lazy-Refund convention in `34.md` gap A).

Effect on IRS correctness: matches the IRS rule that line 35d is blank when not applicable. A blank line on the printed return equals null in the Java model. The backend never emits `""` for line 35d.

Effect on developer experience: any consumer that reads `refund.accountNumber` without null-checking gets a `NullPointerException` in Java or a TypeError in TypeScript. The Angular frontend handles this via optional chaining (`form.refund?.accountNumber`).

### 2c. Backend — Compute order

Per `dependencies\33.md` section 3, `computeLine31ThroughLine38()` is step 13 in the overall compute sequence. Inside this method, the relevant ordering for line 35d is:

```text
13. computeLine31ThroughLine38() {
       ... (lines 25d, 26, 32, 33, totalTax computed) ...
       compute line 34 = line33 − totalTax    (only if line33 > totalTax — Gate 1)
       compute line 36Capped, line 35a        (sets refund.amountAppliedToNextYear, refund.refundAmount)
       if (NOT Form 8888 elected) AND (35-direct-deposit form present):     ← Gate 3
           if (wantsDirectDeposit == true):                                   ← Gate 2
               set refund.directDeposit, refund.routingNumber (35b),
                   refund.accountType (35c), refund.accountNumber (35d)  ← LINE 35d HERE
}
14. computeForm2210()
15. computeForm8888()
```

Line 35d is set exactly once per return, only inside the overpayment branch (Gate 1), only when Gate 3 is true (no Form 8888), and only when Gate 2 is true (direct deposit elected). The compute order has no race condition — the `directDepositData` map was resolved at line 208 (before any line computation) and is not mutated by any other method.

### 2d. Frontend (Angular) — Where line 35d is read for the preview and PDF export

Per `src\app\forms\form-tax-return-1040.component.ts` at line 870:

## 5. Reference data constants (2025)

Line 35d itself has very little reference data. Two structural constants govern the field content; one of them is enforced only at the user interface layer, and neither changes from one tax year to the next.

| Constant | Value | Source | Enforcement layer |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maximum length of the account number | 17 characters | 2025 Form 1040 PDF field width; IRS Document 1346 e-file specification | User interface `maxlength="17"` attribute on the input field (frontend only) |
| Allowed character class for the account number | Letters A-Z, digits 0-9, hyphen "-"; no spaces | IRS Document 1346 e-file specification | **Not enforced** (see Gap E below) |
| Account number is not self-validating | (no checksum exists) | IRS Form 1040 Instructions for line 35d | Implicit — there is no checksum algorithm to apply |

The 17-character maximum is enforced by the browser via the `maxlength="17"` attribute on the `<input>` element in `form-direct-deposit.component.ts` line 114 and in four locations in `form-refund-and-amount-owed.component.ts` (lines 282, 377, 433, 489 — single direct-deposit account plus three split-refund accounts). The backend does not enforce the length; a malicious or buggy client could PUT a 25-character string and the backend would store it without truncating or rejecting.

The allowed-character rule (digits, letters, hyphen, no spaces) is not enforced anywhere in this codebase. See Gap E below.

The IRS rule that the account number alone cannot be validated (no checksum) means there is no constant to define. Line 35d has no Mod-N divisor like line 35b's ABA Mod-10 checksum.

There are no rate constants, no threshold constants, and no cap constants that apply to line 35d.

## 6. Gap analysis — bugs, shortcomings, and missing fields

> Our objective is correctness. The following gaps are written from the standpoint of validating the application against the IRS 2025 Form 1040, not the other way around.

### Gap A — Null-when-absent storage convention is undocumented in Javadoc ✅ **CLOSED 2026-06-13**

Evidence: `TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine31ThroughLine38()` at line 27370 only calls `refund.setAccountNumber(acctNum)` when `acctNum != null && !acctNum.isBlank()`. The setter on `Refund.java` (`public void setAccountNumber(String value)`) has no Javadoc explaining the storage convention.

Effect on IRS correctness: **none**. A blank line 35d on the printed return equals null in the model. The convention matches the IRS rule that line 35d is blank when not applicable.

Effect on developer experience: minor. Any consumer that reads `refund.accountNumber` without null-checking will get a `NullPointerException` in Java or a TypeError in TypeScript. The Angular frontend handles this via optional chaining (`form.refund?.accountNumber`).

Recommended fix: add Javadoc to `Refund.setAccountNumber(String)` that says "line 35d. Stored as null when the field is absent (taxpayer chose paper check, OR chose Form 8888 split refund, OR there is no overpayment). The compute service only calls this setter when the account-number string is non-null and non-blank after trimming. Mirrors the null-when-zero convention for line 35a (`35a.md` gap A) and the lazy-Refund convention (`34.md` gap A)."

### Gap D — Backend does not enforce the 17-character maximum length ✅ **CLOSED 2026-06-13** (was MEDIUM severity)

Evidence: `TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine31ThroughLine38()` at line 27370 sets `refund.setAccountNumber(acctNum)` with no length check. The personal-form save endpoint `PUT /api/personal/refund-and-amount-owed-taxpayer` and `PUT /api/personal/35-direct-deposit` also have no length validation on the `accountNumber` field. Only the browser's `maxlength="17"` HTML attribute prevents a 17+-character string from reaching the server, and that attribute is enforceable only on keystroke input — a paste, a programmatic submit, or a non-browser client (curl, Postman, a malicious test) can submit any length.

Effect on IRS correctness: when the stored account number exceeds 17 characters and the bug from Gap C is fixed (so the value actually reaches the PDF), the PDF's 17-character `f2_33[0]` field would either truncate the rendered string or — depending on the PDF viewer — wrap into the surrounding form area. The IRS would receive an over-length account number that fails to parse against IRS Document 1346.

Recommended fix: in `computeLine31ThroughLine38()`, after `String acctNum = getString(...);`, add a length validation:

```java
String acctNum = getString(directDepositData, "accountNumber");
if (acctNum != null && !acctNum.isBlank()) {
    String trimmed = acctNum.trim();
    if (trimmed.length() > 17) {
        // emit a non-blocking TaxReturnFlag and store the truncated value, OR
        // emit a blocking flag asking the taxpayer to shorten/correct.
        flags.add(TaxReturnFlag.LINE_35D_ACCOUNT_NUMBER_TOO_LONG);
    }
    refund.setAccountNumber(trimmed.length() > 17 ? trimmed.substring(0, 17) : trimmed);
}
```

A symmetric `wantsDirectDeposit && accountNumber blank` blocking flag would also catch the case where the taxpayer chose direct deposit but forgot the account number — currently the backend silently leaves line 35d null and the IRS receives a Form 1040 with line 35b populated and line 35d blank (which is itself an IRS rejection per section 1f rule 1).

Severity: MEDIUM. Browser maxlength catches the typical-user case, but it does not protect against pasted long strings, programmatic submits, or malicious clients. Fix is small (one length check + one TaxReturnFlag enum entry).

**Resolution (2026-06-13):** `TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine31ThroughLine38()` now trims the supplied account number, checks `length > 17`, truncates defensively to the first 17 characters, and appends a non-blocking `LINE_35D_ACCOUNT_NUMBER_TOO_LONG` `TaxReturnFlag` with a human-readable message that shows both the original length and the truncated value. The advisory wording explicitly tells the taxpayer the truncated number will NOT reach the real account, so the surface message drives a correction rather than a silent IRS rejection. The flag is non-blocking by design — the value is still stored so compute can complete and the taxpayer can see the rest of the return; the warning surfaces at compute time alongside other advisories. Closure verified by the new Java unit test `line35d_account_number_too_long_truncated_and_flagged` which seeds a 20-character account number and asserts both the truncated storage (`12345678901234567`) and the presence of the flag.

### Gap E — No validation of the allowed character class on line 35d ✅ **CLOSED 2026-06-13** (was LOW-MEDIUM severity)

Evidence: per IRS Document 1346, line 35d may contain only digits 0-9, letters A-Z, and the hyphen "-" (no spaces). This codebase does not enforce the character class — neither the frontend `<input type="text">` (which accepts anything) nor the backend `getString(directDepositData, "accountNumber")` (which only trims). A taxpayer could type `"123 / 456"` (space and slash) and the backend would store it unchanged. The PDF would render it, but the IRS would reject the e-file submission.

Effect on IRS correctness: when a taxpayer enters disallowed characters, the IRS rejects the submission (paper or e-file), the refund is delayed, and the taxpayer must re-submit. For users using paper file the rejection is silent (the IRS converts the refund to a paper check).

Recommended fix: add a regex validator on the input control (frontend) and a server-side check (backend) for `^[0-9A-Z\-]{1,17}$` after uppercasing letters. Alternatively, declare alphabetic characters out of scope and restrict to `^[0-9\-]{1,17}$` since most U.S. bank account numbers are digits only.

Severity: LOW-MEDIUM. Most taxpayer-typed account numbers are digit-only, so the rule rarely bites. But a single space (common when reading from a paper check that has visual separators) breaks the entire submission.

**Resolution (2026-06-13):** `TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine31ThroughLine38()` now uppercases the trimmed account number and tests it against the regex `^[0-9A-Z\-]+$`. When the match fails, the backend appends a non-blocking `LINE_35D_ACCOUNT_NUMBER_INVALID_CHARACTERS` `TaxReturnFlag` with a message that names the common cause (spaces between digit groups from a paper check) so the user knows what to fix. The value is stored as-typed (we do not strip the offending characters automatically — the user must explicitly correct, because silently stripping could leave a different-looking but still-wrong account number). Two Java unit tests verify both directions: `line35d_account_number_invalid_characters_emits_flag` seeds `"1234 5678 9012"` and asserts the flag fires; `line35d_account_number_valid_characters_no_flag` seeds the hyphenated `"1234-5678-9012"` and asserts the flag does NOT fire (hyphens are allowed by IRS Document 1346).


The structural relationship of line 35d to its neighbors:

```text
                              line 34 (overpaid pool)
                                       |
                       line 35a (refund amount) — line 36 (apply to 2026)
                                       |
              +------------------------+------------------------+
              |                        |                        |
      Form 8888 elected:        Direct deposit elected:    Neither elected:
      lines 35b/c/d blank;      lines 35b + 35c + 35d      lines 35b/c/d blank;
      per-account routing       all populated;             IRS mails paper check
      lives in Form 8888        Form 8888 not filed        for the line-35a amount
              |                        |
              v                        v
        2-3 accounts             single account:
        each with their          line 35b → bank routing
        own routing/type/        line 35c → checking or savings
        account-number           line 35d → bank account number ← THIS LINE
```

Line 35d is the second of the two text fields that together direct the refund. Line 35b says "which bank"; line 35d says "which account at that bank." Lines 35b and 35d together carry the IRS's entire description of where the refund money goes — there is no second chance once the return is signed.
