## Quick orientation

The Form 1040 label for line 36 (per the actual 2025 IRS Form 1040 page 2):

> **"36   Amount of line 34 you want applied to your 2026 estimated tax ▶ 36"**

In one sentence: line 36 is a **voluntary taxpayer election** to redirect part or all of the overpayment (line 34) toward the first installment of next year's estimated tax (due April 15, 2026), instead of receiving that money as a refund. The amount is constrained by `0 ≤ line 36 ≤ line 34`. The election is **irrevocable** once the return is filed (Internal Revenue Code §6402(b)).

Eight facts to keep in mind throughout this document:

1. **Line 36 is a voluntary election, not arithmetic on income data.** The taxpayer chooses whether to elect and how much to elect. The amount is taxpayer-entered, not computed from W-2 or 1099 amounts.

2. **Line 36 is conditional on the same Gate 1 as the rest of the refund-side cluster.** It fires only when line 34 fires (only when there is an overpayment). When line 24 ≥ line 33, line 36 cannot be claimed — there is no overpayment to redirect — and the field is blank on the printed return.

3. **Line 36 is bounded.** Mathematically: `0 ≤ line 36 ≤ line 34`. The taxpayer can elect zero (no election), the full overpayment (entire refund redirected), or any whole-dollar amount in between. Anything entered above line 34 is silently capped at line 34 — the Internal Revenue Service will not credit more than the overpayment.

4. **Line 36 directly reduces line 35a.** The structural partition pinned by Internal Revenue Code §6402(b) is `line 34 = line 35a + line 36`. Every dollar elected on line 36 is one less dollar refunded on line 35a. There is no third destination — the overpayment goes either back to the taxpayer as cash (line 35a) or forward to next year's estimated tax (line 36).

5. **Line 36 is irrevocable once filed.** Per Internal Revenue Code §6402(b) and the 2025 Form 1040 instructions for line 36 (Publication 505 chapter 2 "Credit an Overpayment"): "You can't have any of the amount you credited to your estimated tax refunded to you until you file your tax return for the following year. You also can't use that overpayment in any other way." A change of heart after filing requires waiting until the 2026 return is filed (the credit then becomes part of the 2026 overpayment if the 2026 return shows an overpayment).

6. **The line-36 amount is credited to the first 2026 quarterly estimated tax installment.** That installment is due April 15, 2026. The taxpayer may attach a written statement directing the credit to a later installment (per Publication 505), but the default is the first quarter. The crediting timing matters for Form 2210 (underpayment-penalty) computations on the 2026 return; it does not matter for the 2025 return.

7. **Line 36 has no IRS rate, threshold, or cap constants of its own.** The only constraint that limits line 36 is the cap at line 34 (which depends on lines 33 and 24). There are no inflation-indexed thresholds and no filing-status-dependent rules.

8. **Line 36 is the only line on Form 1040 page 2 that is a pure taxpayer election with no computation.** Lines 34, 35a, 37, and 38 are all arithmetic; line 35b/35c/35d are string passthroughs gated by a triple condition; line 36 is the only line where the taxpayer types a dollar amount directly and the printed value equals the typed value (capped at line 34).

The equation:

```text
if (Form 1040 line 33) > (Form 1040 line 24):
    (Form 1040 line 34) = (Form 1040 line 33) − (Form 1040 line 24)
    (Form 1040 line 36) = min((taxpayer-typed amount), (Form 1040 line 34))
else:
    (Form 1040 line 36) = blank
```

When the gate fails, line 36 is blank on the printed return regardless of what the taxpayer typed on the intake form — the taxpayer cannot credit a non-existent overpayment.

## 1. The Internal Revenue Service canonical computation

### 1a. The exact equation

```text
if (Form 1040 line 33) > (Form 1040 line 24):
    (Form 1040 line 34)  = (Form 1040 line 33) − (Form 1040 line 24)
    (Form 1040 line 36)  = min((taxpayer election amount), (Form 1040 line 34))
else:
    (Form 1040 line 36)  = blank
```

The "taxpayer election amount" is the dollar value the taxpayer typed into the intake form question "Amount to apply to 2026 estimated taxes." When the taxpayer did not elect to apply any overpayment (answered No to "Do you want to apply part or all of your overpayment to your 2026 estimated tax?"), the election amount is treated as zero and line 36 is `min(0, line 34) = 0` — written as blank on the printed return.

The cap function `min((taxpayer election amount), (Form 1040 line 34))` is what implements the IRS rule that the credit cannot exceed the overpayment. When the taxpayer enters more than the overpayment, the excess is silently discarded — the IRS will only credit what is actually overpaid.

There is no IRS rate, threshold, or cap constant other than line 34 itself. No filing-status routing, no age-based rules, no MAGI phaseout. Line 36 is the simplest computation on Form 1040 page 2.

### 1b. What each component means

**Line 34 — Amount overpaid.**

Plain English: line 34 is the total dollar amount by which the taxpayer overpaid their 2025 tax. Computed as `line 33 (total payments) − line 24 (total tax)` when line 33 > line 24; blank otherwise. Line 34 is the upper ceiling for line 36 — the maximum amount the taxpayer can credit forward. See `XLS\Computations\34.md` for the full specification.

**The taxpayer election amount.**

Plain English: this is the dollar amount the taxpayer types into the intake form's `amountToApply` field after answering Yes to `electsApplyToNextYear`. It represents how much of the overpayment the taxpayer wants the Internal Revenue Service to keep on file as a credit toward next year's estimated tax, rather than send back as a cash refund. The taxpayer can enter any non-negative whole dollar amount; the backend caps at line 34.

**Line 36 — Amount applied to 2026 estimated tax.**

Plain English: line 36 is the result of the cap function. When the taxpayer-typed amount is at or below line 34, line 36 equals what the taxpayer typed. When the taxpayer-typed amount exceeds line 34, line 36 equals line 34 (the excess is discarded). When the taxpayer did not elect, line 36 is zero — blank on the printed return.

### 1c. The irrevocability rule (Internal Revenue Code §6402(b))

Plain English: once the 2025 return is filed (electronically or by mail), the line-36 amount is **permanently committed** to next year's estimated tax. The taxpayer cannot:

- Later ask the Internal Revenue Service to refund it as cash.
- Apply it to any tax other than 2026 estimated tax.
- Re-direct it to a different taxpayer (e.g., a spouse on a separately-filed return).

The only way to "recover" the amount is to file the 2026 return at the end of 2026; if the 2026 return shows an overpayment that the taxpayer wants refunded, the original 2025 line-36 credit (along with any 2026 withholding and estimated payments) flows into that 2026 overpayment.

The irrevocability is statutory. Internal Revenue Code §6402(b) reads: "Credit against estimated tax. The Secretary is authorized to prescribe regulations providing for the crediting against the estimated income tax for any taxable year of the amount determined by the taxpayer or the Secretary to be an overpayment of the income tax for a preceding taxable year." The "preceding taxable year" language means the credit is fixed to the year-pair (2025 overpayment → 2026 estimated tax); the taxpayer cannot retroactively decouple the two.

### 1d. The default crediting date

Plain English: the line-36 amount is credited to the **first** 2026 quarterly estimated tax installment, which is due April 15, 2026 (the same date as the 2025 Form 1040 filing deadline). The Internal Revenue Service treats the credit as if the taxpayer made a $X estimated-tax payment on April 15, 2026, where $X is the line-36 amount.

Publication 505 chapter 2 documents one exception: the taxpayer may attach a written statement directing the credit to a different (later) installment. This is rare in practice; the project does not model the override.

The default crediting date matters for the 2026 Form 2210 (underpayment-penalty) computation on the **next year's** return, because the timing of payments determines whether quarterly safe-harbor thresholds were met. It does not affect any line on the 2025 return.

### 1e. Why line 36 is conditional rather than always-set

The Internal Revenue Service leaves line 36 blank rather than printing `$0` when there is no overpayment for two reasons:

1. **The election is structurally meaningless without an overpayment.** Without a positive line 34, there is nothing to credit. The Internal Revenue Service does not accept "negative refund elections" — a taxpayer cannot pre-pay 2026 estimated tax by claiming a credit on their 2025 return when no 2025 overpayment exists.
2. **Mutual exclusivity with line 37.** When line 24 > line 33 (amount-owed branch), the entire refund-side cluster (lines 34, 35a, 35b/c/d, 36) is blank. Treating line 36 as conditional matches the structural mutual exclusivity rather than printing $0 in the apply-to-next-year box while $X is printed in the amount-you-owe box (line 37).

### 1f. The four rules that apply to line 36 itself

There are exactly four:

1. **Line 36 must be `min(taxpayer-typed amount, line 34)` exactly when line 34 fires.** The cap is non-negotiable per Internal Revenue Code §6402(b).
2. **Line 36 must be blank when line 34 is blank.** When `line 33 ≤ line 24`, both lines 34 and 36 are blank.
3. **Line 36 cannot be negative.** The taxpayer election is bounded below by zero (a "No" answer to the election question is equivalent to zero).
4. **Line 36 is whole-dollar.** The Internal Revenue Service rounds to whole dollars for all monetary fields on Form 1040; line 36 is no exception.

The Internal Revenue Service prints rules 1 and 4 on the actual Form 1040 line 36 label. Rules 2 and 3 are implicit but follow from the structure (line 36 inherits the blank-when-not-applicable rule from line 34).

## 2. The as-implemented computation (this codebase)

### 2a. Backend (Java) — Where the taxpayer-typed amount is read

Per `src\main\java\com\ustax\microservices\TaxReturnComputeService.java` lines 27315-27326, the line-36 election is read in **Stage 1** of a two-stage compute, BEFORE the overpayment branch is entered:

```java
// ── Line 36: amount of overpayment applied to 2026 estimated tax ────────────────────────
// Only relevant when the taxpayer is overpaid and has elected to apply.
BigDecimal line36 = BigDecimal.ZERO;
if (applyNextYearData != null) {
    Boolean elects = getBoolean(applyNextYearData, "electsApplyToNextYear");
    if (Boolean.TRUE.equals(elects)) {
        BigDecimal raw = getAmount(applyNextYearData, "amountToApply");
        if (raw != null && raw.compareTo(BigDecimal.ZERO) > 0) {
            line36 = roundMoney(raw);
        }
    }
}
```

What each name in this block refers to:

| Local variable | Meaning | Source |
|---|---|---|
| `line36` | The taxpayer-typed election amount, rounded to whole dollars; defaults to zero when no election is made | local `BigDecimal` |
| `applyNextYearData` | The Map representing the saved unified `refund-and-amount-owed-taxpayer` form, OR the legacy `36-apply-to-next-year` form | resolved at line 207 of the same file |
| `elects` | The Yes/No answer to "Do you want to apply part or all of your overpayment to your 2026 estimated tax?" | from the saved personal form |
| `raw` | The dollar amount the taxpayer typed into "Amount to apply to 2026 estimated taxes" | from the saved personal form |
| `roundMoney` | Rounds a `BigDecimal` to whole dollars (zero scale, half-even rounding) | shared utility |

Translated into plain English, Stage 1 does the following:

1. Start with `line36 = 0` (the "no election" default).
2. Check whether the personal form exists. If not, leave `line36 = 0`.
3. Check whether the taxpayer answered Yes to the election question. If not, leave `line36 = 0`.
4. Read the typed dollar amount. If null or non-positive, leave `line36 = 0`.
5. Otherwise, set `line36 = roundMoney(rawAmount)`.

After Stage 1, `line36` is either zero or a positive whole-dollar `BigDecimal` representing the taxpayer's uncapped election amount.

### 2b. Backend — Stage 2: cap at line 34 and store

Per the same file lines 27342-27346, inside the overpayment branch (`if (line33.compareTo(totalTax) > 0)`), the capped value is written to the output model:

```java
// ── Line 36: cap at overpaid; store on Refund ───────────────────────────────────────
BigDecimal line36Capped = line36.min(overpaid); // cannot exceed what was overpaid
if (line36Capped.compareTo(BigDecimal.ZERO) > 0) {
    refund.setAmountAppliedToNextYear(line36Capped);
}
```

Translated into plain English, Stage 2 does the following:

1. Compute `line36Capped = min(line36, overpaid)`. This implements the Internal Revenue Service rule that line 36 cannot exceed line 34. If the taxpayer typed `$999,999` but only overpaid `$5,000`, `line36Capped = $5,000`.
2. If the capped value is positive, write it to `refund.amountAppliedToNextYear`. Otherwise, leave the field null (the **null-when-zero storage convention** documented in `35a.md` gap A, applied symmetrically here).

The Stage-2 block is INSIDE the overpayment branch, so it never executes when there is no overpayment — implementing the Internal Revenue Service rule that line 36 is blank when line 34 is blank. Stage 1 still ran (the `line36` local variable still holds the taxpayer-typed amount), but with no `refund` object to store on, the value is structurally inert.

### 2c. Backend — The two-stage pattern (cross-region state via local variable)

Stage 1 runs at line 27317-27326 of `computeLine31ThroughLine38`, which is BEFORE Stage 2 at lines 27342-27346 (inside the `if (line33 > totalTax)` branch). The `line36` local variable carries state from Stage 1 across the intervening computations (totalTax read, line 34 subtraction, refund object creation) to Stage 2.

This is the **TWO-STAGE CAPPED NUMERIC PASSTHROUGH** pattern documented in `lines/33.md` verification log row 4 (issue #6 of the line-36 audit on 2026-05-17). It is distinct from a simple inline cap because:

- Stage 1 reads the raw value and applies the election gate (electsApplyToNextYear).
- Stage 2 applies the cap and writes the output.
- The intermediate code (line 34 computation) needs `line36` to be available so it can be used (Stage 2) but does NOT need `line36Capped` (the cap is applied immediately at Stage 2).

The pattern works because the cap depends on `overpaid` (line 34), which is only available inside the overpayment branch. Computing the cap outside the branch would require either (a) duplicating the line-34 computation or (b) pre-computing line 34 even when the amount-owed branch fires. Both alternatives are worse than the current two-stage design.

### 2d. Backend — Compute order

Per `dependencies\33.md` section 3, `computeLine31ThroughLine38()` is step 13 in the overall compute sequence. Inside this method, the relevant ordering for line 36 is:

```text
13. computeLine31ThroughLine38() {
       ... (lines 25d, 26, 32, 33 computed) ...
       Stage 1: read line 36 election amount (uncapped, may exceed line 34)  ← line 27317
       read totalTax (line 24) from form1040.taxAndCredits
       if (line33 > totalTax):
           line 34 = line33 − totalTax    (write to refund.overpaid)
           Stage 2: line36Capped = min(line36, line34)                       ← LINE 36 STORED HERE
                                          (write to refund.amountAppliedToNextYear)
           line 35a = line34 − line36Capped  (write to refund.refundAmount)
           lines 35b/c/d                  (direct deposit details)
       else if (totalTax > line33):
           line 37 = totalTax − line33
}
```

Line 36 is set exactly once per return inside Stage 2, only inside the `if (line33 > totalTax)` branch. The Stage-1 read at line 27317 is unconditional (always executes), but the value it produces is unused when the branch is not entered — the local variable goes out of scope at the end of the method.

### 2e. Frontend (Angular) — Where line 36 is read for the preview and PDF export

Per `src\app\forms\form-tax-return-1040.component.ts` at line 887:

```typescript
values['line36_applied_to_2025_estimated_tax'] = this.formatAmount(form.refund?.amountAppliedToNextYear);
```

The frontend reads `form.refund?.amountAppliedToNextYear` via optional chaining (correctly handling the lazy-Refund pattern from `34.md` gap A and the null-when-zero convention from section 2b) and writes the formatted amount to the PDF field key `line36_applied_to_2025_estimated_tax`.

**The PDF field key carries a stale 2024-era off-by-one in its year suffix.** The 2025 IRS Form 1040 line 36 label reads "Amount of line 34 you want applied to your **2026** estimated tax" — but the project's semantic CSV (and therefore the frontend emit) names the field `line36_applied_to_**2025**_estimated_tax`. The "2025" in the field name is a holdover from the 2024 tax year (when filing a 2024 return, the credit went to 2025 estimated tax; the field name was correct then). For the 2025 tax year, the credit goes to 2026, so the truthful name would be `line36_applied_to_2026_estimated_tax`.

This is **a documentation defect, not a functional defect.** The CSV name (`line36_applied_to_2025_estimated_tax`) maps to the correct 2025 AcroForm field position (`f2_34[0]` at coordinates `(410.4, 252, 481.65, 263.999)` on page 2), which is the correct box on the 2025 form. The frontend emit name matches the CSV name, so the exported PDF correctly shows the line-36 amount in the right box. The only thing wrong is the SEMANTIC NAME of the field — anyone reading the CSV or the frontend code would reasonably conclude the value is being routed to a 2025 box, when in fact the box exists on the 2025 form and applies to 2026 estimated tax.

This is documented as Gap C below.

### 2g. The as-implemented equation in one line

```text
Stage 1: line36 = (electsApplyToNextYear && amountToApply > 0) ? roundMoney(amountToApply) : 0
Stage 2 (inside `if line33 > totalTax`):
    line36Capped = min(line36, line33 − totalTax)
    if line36Capped > 0:
        refund.amountAppliedToNextYear = line36Capped
else:
    refund.amountAppliedToNextYear = (never set; remains null on null Refund object)
```

The compute layer is faithfully implementing the Internal Revenue Service rule. The defect is in the PDF emit layer (Gap C — semantic name carries a 2024-era off-by-one year suffix) and the JSON overlay layer (Gap D — in-browser overlay renders blank at the line-36 position because of a stale 2024 entry name).

## 5. Reference data constants (2025)

Line 36 has no rate, threshold, or cap constants of its own. The only constants relevant to line 36 are the structural ones that govern when and how the election can be made:

| Constant | 2025 value | Source | Enforcement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Line 36 upper bound | line 34 (the overpayment) | Internal Revenue Code §6402(b) | Backend cap at Stage 2 (`line36.min(overpaid)`) |
| Line 36 lower bound | zero | Internal Revenue Code §6402(b) | Backend initialization (`BigDecimal line36 = BigDecimal.ZERO;`) plus the `compareTo(BigDecimal.ZERO) > 0` gate at Stage 1 |
| Default crediting date | April 15, 2026 (first 2026 quarterly installment) | Publication 505 chapter 2 | NOT modeled (no compute or display) |
| Override to later installment | Yes, via attached written statement | Publication 505 chapter 2 | NOT modeled (rare in practice) |
| Election irrevocability | True (after return filed) | Internal Revenue Code §6402(b); 2025 Instructions for Form 1040 line 36 paragraph | NOT modeled at code level; communicated to the user via the Tier-1 amber "This election is permanent once you file" callout on the refund form (`form-refund-and-amount-owed.component.ts`) |
| Rounding | Whole dollars | IRS 2025 Form 1040 general instructions | Backend `roundMoney(raw)` at Stage 1 |

There are no Internal Revenue Service rate constants (no percentage of line 34, no flat-dollar minimum or maximum, no inflation-indexed cap). Line 36 is uniquely simple in this regard.

## 6. Gap analysis — bugs, shortcomings, and missing fields

> Our objective is correctness. The following gaps are written from the standpoint of validating the application against the Internal Revenue Service 2025 Form 1040, not the other way around.

### Gap A — Null-when-zero storage convention is undocumented in Javadoc ✅ **CLOSED 2026-06-13** (was LOW severity)

Evidence: `TaxReturnComputeService.computeLine31ThroughLine38()` at line 27344 only calls `refund.setAmountAppliedToNextYear(line36Capped)` when `line36Capped.compareTo(BigDecimal.ZERO) > 0`. The setter on `Refund.java` (`public void setAmountAppliedToNextYear(BigDecimal value)`) has no Javadoc explaining this convention.

Effect on Internal Revenue Service correctness: **none**. A blank line 36 on the printed return equals null in the model and equals zero in semantic terms. The convention matches the Internal Revenue Service rule that line 36 is blank when not applicable.

Effect on developer experience: minor. Any consumer that reads `refund.amountAppliedToNextYear` without null-checking gets a `NullPointerException` in Java or a TypeError in TypeScript. The Angular frontend handles this via optional chaining (`form.refund?.amountAppliedToNextYear`).

## 7. Summary

The Internal Revenue Service canonical equation for Form 1040 line 36 in tax year 2025:

```text
if Form 1040 line 33 > Form 1040 line 24:
    Form 1040 line 34  = Form 1040 line 33 − Form 1040 line 24
    Form 1040 line 36  = min(taxpayer-typed amount, Form 1040 line 34)
else:
    Form 1040 line 36  = blank
```

Plus the three IRS-rule constraints:

```text
0 ≤ line 36 ≤ line 34   (cap from Internal Revenue Code §6402(b))
line 36 is irrevocable once filed   (statutory, not enforceable in code)
default crediting date = April 15, 2026 (Q1 2026 installment)
```
