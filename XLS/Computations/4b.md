## Glossary

- **Form 1040 Line 4b** — "Taxable amount." The portion of total Individual Retirement Account distributions that is taxable for the year. **Line 4b feeds Line 9 (total income)**; the gross amount on Line 4a does NOT feed Line 9. This is the only one of the Line 4a / 4b / 4c trio that affects the tax computation.
- **Form 1040 Line 4a** — "IRA distributions" (gross). Companion to Line 4b. Stays BLANK when the full distribution is taxable; carries the gross amount when an exception applies. See `XLS/Computations/4a.md`.
- **Form 1040 Line 4c** — Disclosure checkboxes and write-in space explaining why Line 4a differs from Line 4b (rollover, Qualified Charitable Distribution, Health Savings Account Funding Distribution, other). See `XLS/Computations/4c.md`.
- **Individual Retirement Account (IRA)** — Personal retirement account. Comes in two main flavors: **traditional** (pre-tax contributions, taxable distributions) and **Roth** (after-tax contributions, generally tax-free qualified distributions). Two employer-sponsored variants — **Simplified Employee Pension (SEP) IRA** and **Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees (SIMPLE) IRA** — are taxed like traditional Individual Retirement Accounts for distribution purposes.
- **Form 1099-R** — "Distributions From Pensions, Annuities, Retirement or Profit-Sharing Plans, IRAs, Insurance Contracts, etc." Box 1 = gross distribution; **Box 2a = the payer's calculation of the taxable amount** (the starting point for Line 4b in the simple case); Box 2b = "taxable amount not determined" checkbox; Box 4 = federal income tax withheld; Box 7 = distribution code(s). The "IRA/SEP/SIMPLE" checkbox routes the statement to Line 4a/4b/4c (when checked) or Line 5a/5b/5c (when unchecked).
- **Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI)** — Not directly used by Line 4b, but referenced by some upstream computations (such as the Health Savings Account Funding Distribution eligibility).
- **Basis** — The taxpayer's after-tax "investment" in an Individual Retirement Account. Created by nondeductible contributions, after-tax rollovers, or inherited-Individual-Retirement-Account adjustments. When basis exists, a portion of each distribution is non-taxable because it's a return of the taxpayer's own after-tax money. Form 8606 tracks basis year over year.
- **Pro-rata basis-recovery rule** — Per Internal Revenue Code §72 and IRS Publication 590-B: when basis exists in any of a person's traditional / Simplified Employee Pension / Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees Individual Retirement Accounts, each distribution is treated as partly basis (non-taxable) and partly earnings (taxable). The fraction is `basis ÷ (basis + value of all traditional / Simplified Employee Pension / Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees IRAs at year-end + distributions + conversions)`. Form 8606 Part I implements this rule.
- **Rollover** — Moving distribution money from one retirement account to another within **60 days**. The rolled-over portion is excluded from Line 4b per Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(3).
- **Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD)** — Direct transfer from a traditional Individual Retirement Account to a qualified charity for taxpayers age 70½ or older. Excluded from Line 4b (up to **$108,000** per person for 2025, with up to **$54,000** of that for a one-time split-interest-entity election). Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(8).
- **Health Savings Account Funding Distribution (HFD)** — Once-in-a-lifetime direct transfer from an Individual Retirement Account to a Health Savings Account, up to the year's Health Savings Account contribution limit. Excluded from Line 4b. Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(9).
- **Form 8606** — "Nondeductible IRAs." Used to compute the taxable portion of an Individual Retirement Account distribution when basis exists, when a Roth conversion occurred, or when a non-qualified Roth distribution occurred. When Form 8606 applies, it **overrides** the simple `box 2a − rollover − Qualified Charitable Distribution − Health Savings Account Funding Distribution` computation.
- **Form 8606 Part I line 15c** — Traditional Individual Retirement Account taxable distribution amount after basis recovery (single value across all the person's traditional / Simplified Employee Pension / Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees Individual Retirement Accounts).
- **Form 8606 Part II line 18** — Taxable amount of traditional-to-Roth conversions for the year.
- **Form 8606 Part III line 25c** — Taxable amount of non-qualified Roth Individual Retirement Account distributions (basis-ordering: contributions first, then conversions, then earnings).
- **Roth distribution codes** (1099-R box 7) — Code **Q** = fully qualified Roth distribution (tax-free, no Form 8606 needed). Codes **J** and **T** = Roth distributions that are NOT fully qualified — Form 8606 Part III is required.
- **Married Filing Separately (MFS)** — Filing status under which each spouse files an independent return. The application's MFS guard suppresses spouse-side computation so spouse-attributed 1099-Rs cannot leak onto the taxpayer's return.
- **Zero-floor** — A taxable amount cannot be negative per Internal Revenue Code §61. When the exception subtractions exceed the payer's taxable amount, the result is clamped to zero (not allowed to go negative). The application uses a helper `subtractNonNegative(a, b) = max(0, a − b)` to enforce this.

## 1. IRS rule for Line 4b (computed before reading the code)

### 1.1 Plain-English equation

In words: **Line 4b is the part of your Individual Retirement Account distributions that is taxable this year.** The amount that flows into total income on Line 9.

How you arrive at that depends on what kind of distribution happened.

#### 1.1.1 The simple fully-taxable case (no exceptions, no basis)

If every dollar that came out of every one of your Individual Retirement Accounts is fully taxable, with no rollover, no Qualified Charitable Distribution, no Health Savings Account Funding Distribution, no Form 8606 trigger (no basis, no Roth conversion, no non-qualified Roth distribution, no return-of-contribution), and the 1099-R box 2b "taxable amount not determined" is unchecked, then:

```
line_4b  =  sum of 1099-R box 2a across all eligible 1099-Rs
```

Per the 2025 IRS Form 1040 instructions (text excerpt verified in `docs/books/i1040gi_2025.txt`): *"Report fully taxable distributions, including early distributions, on Form 1040 or 1040-SR, line 4b (no entry is required on Form 1040 or 1040-SR, line 4a)."*

#### 1.1.2 The partly-taxable case (an exception applies — but no basis)

When the distribution has a rollover, a Qualified Charitable Distribution, or a Health Savings Account Funding Distribution but the taxpayer has no basis in traditional Individual Retirement Accounts, the IRS rule is to subtract the excluded amounts from the payer's taxable amount:

```
line_4b_per_person  =  max(0,
                            sum of 1099-R box 2a
                          − rollover amount
                          − effective Qualified Charitable Distribution (capped at $108,000)
                          − Health Savings Account Funding Distribution amount
                        )
```

The `max(0, …)` clamp comes from Internal Revenue Code §61 — taxable income cannot be negative.

The "effective Qualified Charitable Distribution" is the total user-entered Qualified Charitable Distribution after two adjustments:
- **Post-70½ deductible-contribution reduction** (Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(8)(B)): if the taxpayer made any deductible traditional Individual Retirement Account contributions after reaching age 70½, the Qualified Charitable Distribution exclusion is reduced dollar-for-dollar to prevent double-dipping (taking a deduction AND a Qualified Charitable Distribution exclusion on the same money). **Carve-out:** the reduction applies only to Qualified Charitable Distributions from the taxpayer's own Individual Retirement Accounts, not from inherited Individual Retirement Accounts (which carry the decedent's separately-tracked basis).
- **$108,000 annual cap** (Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(8)(A)): only the first $108,000 per person per year is excludable. Any excess is taxable.

#### 1.1.3 The Form 8606 case (basis recovery, conversion, or non-qualified Roth)

When Form 8606 applies, the simple computation above is **replaced** by Form 8606's pro-rata basis-recovery math (Internal Revenue Code §72). Form 8606 handles the rollover / Qualified Charitable Distribution / Health Savings Account Funding Distribution exclusions internally via its line 7, so the application must not subtract them twice.

```
line_4b_per_person  =  Form 8606 Part I line 15c   (traditional IRA basis recovery)
                    +  Form 8606 Part II line 18    (Roth conversion taxable)
                    +  Form 8606 Part III line 25c  (non-qualified Roth distribution taxable)
```

Each of those three Form 8606 sub-amounts can be null (when that part of Form 8606 does not apply); the addition uses null-preserving arithmetic so null + null stays null.

#### 1.1.4 Aggregation to the return-level Line 4b

After computing the per-person taxable amount above for the taxpayer (and, on a joint return, the spouse), the return-level Line 4b is the sum:

```
line_4b  =  taxpayer.line_4b_per_person  +  spouse.line_4b_per_person
            (spouse path is suppressed under Married Filing Separately)
```

Then `round_to_whole_dollar(line_4b)` is the final number on the printed Form 1040.

### 1.4 Decision tree (which computation path applies)

```
For each person (taxpayer; spouse only if filing status ≠ Married Filing Separately):

    Collect 1099-R entries where iraSepSimple == true AND recipient_tin matches person.ssn
    Sum box 2a across those entries  →  taxableBox2a (BigDecimal, null if no entries match)

    Compute exception triggers from the person's ira-income form:
        hasRollover            =  totalIraRolloverAmount  > 0  OR  hadIraRollover  == true
        hasQcd                 =  totalQcdAmount          > 0  OR  hadQcd          == true
        hasHfd                 =  totalHfdAmount          > 0  OR  hadHfd          == true
        hasException2          =  hasNondeductibleTraditionalBasis  OR
                                  hasTraditionalToRothConversion    OR
                                  (hasRothIraDistributions AND NOT all-Q Roth codes)  OR
                                  hadReturnOfContributionOrRecharacterization  OR
                                  (1099-R box 7 contains J or T)
        hasOtherLine4cWriteIn  =  hasOtherLine4cWriteInCode == true
        hasTaxableNotDetermined = hasAnyTaxableAmountNotDeterminedIra1099R  OR
                                  (any uploaded 1099-R has box 2b checked)

    Compute the effective Qualified Charitable Distribution amount:
        ownIraQcdAmount        =  max(0, totalQcdAmount − inheritedIraQcdAmount)
        effectiveOwnQcd        =  max(0, ownIraQcdAmount − deductibleIraContributionsAfterAge70Half)
        effectiveQcdAmount     =  effectiveOwnQcd + inheritedIraQcdAmount
        effectiveQcdAmountCapped = min(effectiveQcdAmount, $108,000)  // silent annual cap

    Simple-case taxable amount (before Form 8606 override):
        taxableAfterExceptions =  max(0, taxableBox2a
                                          − totalIraRolloverAmount
                                          − effectiveQcdAmountCapped
                                          − totalHfdAmount)
        (null-preserving: if taxableBox2a is null, result is null)

    If hasException2:
        Build Form 8606 (per person):
            Compute Part I line 7 automatically from grossTraditionalIra minus exclusions
            Compute Part I lines 1-15c (pro-rata basis recovery)
            Compute Part II lines 16-18 (Roth conversion taxability)
            Compute Part III lines 19-25c (Roth basis ordering)
        form8606Taxable        =  line15c + line18 + line25c  (null-preserving)
        If form8606Taxable is not null:
            taxableAmount      =  form8606Taxable    // override the simple case
        Else:
            taxableAmount      =  taxableAfterExceptions  // unchanged

    Else:
        taxableAmount          =  taxableAfterExceptions

    person.taxableIra          =  roundMoney(taxableAmount)


Aggregate across persons:
    taxableIra_return          =  taxpayer.taxableIra + spouse.taxableIra
                                   (spouse suppressed under Married Filing Separately)

line_4b                        =  roundMoney(taxableIra_return)
```

### 1.6 What MUST NOT contribute to Line 4b (explicit exclusions)

Per IRS Publication 590-B and the 2025 Form 1040 instructions:

| Item | Where it goes | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| 1099-R with `iraSepSimple` UNCHECKED (pension, annuity, 401(k), 403(b), 457(b)) | Form 1040 Line 5b (taxable pensions) via `computePensionAnnuityIncome` | Lines 4a/4b are Individual-Retirement-Account-only by statute; non-Individual-Retirement-Account retirement plans use Lines 5a/5b/5c |
| Rolled-over portion of an Individual Retirement Account distribution (within the 60-day window, into an eligible retirement account) | Excluded from Line 4b | Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(3) — not a taxable event |
| Qualified Charitable Distribution amount (up to $108,000 per person; reduced by post-70½ deductible contributions to OWN Individual Retirement Accounts) | Excluded from Line 4b | Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(8) |
| Health Savings Account Funding Distribution amount | Excluded from Line 4b | Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(9) |
| Return of an excess contribution (or earnings on an excess contribution) returned by the due date of the return | Reported via Form 8606 if applicable, otherwise excluded | Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(4) |
| Trustee-to-trustee transfer (not a distribution at all) | Not reported anywhere | A trustee-to-trustee transfer is not a distribution per Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(3) and the Treasury regulations |
| Qualified disaster distribution repayment | Subtracted from taxable amount via Form 8606 line 15b / 25b | Internal Revenue Code §72(t) qualified-disaster provisions |
| Distribution that is part of a divorce-incident transfer | Excluded from the distributing spouse's Line 4b | Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(6) |
| Code Q on a Roth 1099-R (fully qualified Roth distribution — 5-year holding period met AND age 59½ / death / disability / first-time-home-purchase) | Excluded from Line 4b (tax-free) | Internal Revenue Code §408A(d)(2) |
| 1099-R box 4 federal withholding | Routes to Line 25b (payments section), not Line 4b | Withholding is a payment, not income |

### 1.7 Worked examples

**Example A — Fully taxable simple case (Line 4b = box 2a):**
- One 1099-R: IRA/SEP/SIMPLE checked, box 1 = $5,000, box 2a = $5,000, box 2b unchecked, no Roth codes.
- User confirms no rollover, no Qualified Charitable Distribution, no Health Savings Account Funding Distribution, no Form 8606 triggers, no other write-in.
- Computation: `taxableBox2a = $5,000`; no exceptions; `taxableAfterExceptions = max(0, 5000 − 0 − 0 − 0) = $5,000`; no Form 8606 override.
- **Line 4b = $5,000.**
- Line 4a stays blank (gross = taxable, no exceptions).

**Example B — Pure rollover (Line 4b drops to $0):**
- One 1099-R: IRA/SEP/SIMPLE checked, box 1 = $20,000, box 2a = $20,000.
- User entered `totalIraRolloverAmount = $20,000`.
- Computation: `taxableBox2a = $20,000`; `taxableAfterExceptions = max(0, 20000 − 20000 − 0 − 0) = $0`.
- **Line 4b = $0.**
- Line 4a = $20,000 (gross carried because the rollover exception applies).
- Line 4c box 1 (rollover) = TRUE.

**Example C — Qualified Charitable Distribution within the $108,000 cap:**
- One 1099-R: IRA/SEP/SIMPLE checked, box 1 = $50,000, box 2a = $50,000. Taxpayer is age 75.
- User entered `totalQcdAmount = $50,000`, `inheritedIraQcdAmount = $0`, `deductibleIraContributionsAfterAge70Half = $0`.
- Computation: `ownIraQcdAmount = $50,000`; `effectiveOwnQcd = $50,000`; `effectiveQcdAmount = $50,000`; `effectiveQcdAmountCapped = min($50,000, $108,000) = $50,000`; `taxableAfterExceptions = max(0, 50000 − 50000) = $0`.
- **Line 4b = $0.**
- Line 4a = $50,000.

**Example D — Qualified Charitable Distribution over the cap:**
- One 1099-R: IRA/SEP/SIMPLE checked, box 1 = $200,000, box 2a = $200,000. Taxpayer is age 75.
- User entered `totalQcdAmount = $150,000`.
- Computation: `effectiveQcdAmount = $150,000`; `effectiveQcdAmountCapped = min($150,000, $108,000) = $108,000`; `taxableAfterExceptions = max(0, 200000 − 108000) = $92,000`. Advisory flag `QCD_EXCEEDS_ANNUAL_CAP_TAXPAYER` fires.
- **Line 4b = $92,000** (the $42,000 over-cap excess becomes taxable per Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(8)(A)).

**Example E — Form 8606 basis recovery overrides the simple computation:**
- One 1099-R: IRA/SEP/SIMPLE checked, box 1 = $1,000, box 2a = $1,000.
- User entered `hasNondeductibleTraditionalBasis = true`, `nondeductibleContributionsCurrentYear = $100`, `basisPrevFromPriorYear8606Line14 = $100`, `valueAllTraditionalSepSimpleIrasAtYearEnd = $1,000`, `totalTraditionalIraConvertedToRothFor8606Line8 = $200`, plus rollover $200 + Qualified Charitable Distribution $100 + Health Savings Account Funding Distribution $50.
- Form 8606 Part I: line 1 = $100, line 2 = $100, line 3 = $200, line 5 = $200, line 6 = $1,000, line 7 = $1,000 − $200 − $100 − $50 − $200 = $450, line 8 = $200, line 9 = $1,650, line 10 = 200/1650 ≈ 0.12121, line 11 = $200 × 0.12121 ≈ $24, line 12 = $450 × 0.12121 ≈ $55, line 13 = $79, line 14 = $121, line 15a = $450 − $55 = $395, line 15c = $395. Part II: line 16 = $200, line 17 ≈ $24, line 18 ≈ $176. Part III: not applicable, line 25c = null.
- `form8606Taxable = $395 + $176 + null = $571`. `taxableAmount` is overridden to $571.
- **Line 4b = $571.**
- Line 4a = $1,000.
- Note: this example matches Example D in `XLS/Computations/4a.md` and lock-in test `Line 4a/4b/4c flow computes and adds Form 8606 outputs when exceptions apply` in `e2e/tests/line4abc-ira-income.spec.ts`.

**Example F — Married Filing Separately spouse-leakage guard:**

## 2. Code implementation (what the code actually does)

The Line 4b compute path lives in `TaxReturnComputeService.java`. Three methods participate, the same three that produce Line 4a (since both lines share the same per-person pipeline):

| Method | Approx. location | Role for Line 4b |
|---|---|---|
| `computeIraDistributions()` | `:7910–8270` | Orchestrator. Runs the Married Filing Separately suppression, calls `computeIraForPerson` twice, sums taxpayer + spouse taxable amounts, rounds once, sets `line4bTaxableIraDistributions` on the output record. |
| `computeIraForPerson()` | `:8218–8514` | Per-person worker. Computes `taxableBox2a` from 1099-R box 2a, applies the three simple-case subtractions (rollover, capped effective Qualified Charitable Distribution, Health Savings Account Funding Distribution) with `subtractNonNegative` for zero-floor and null-preservation, optionally builds Form 8606 and overrides the result, returns a per-person `IraPersonComputation` with `taxableIraDistributions` as a rounded BigDecimal. |
| `buildForm8606()` | `:8517–8610` | Builds the per-person Form 8606 when `hasException2` is true. Computes Part I (lines 1-15c traditional Individual Retirement Account basis recovery via pro-rata), Part II (lines 16-18 traditional-to-Roth conversion taxability), Part III (lines 19-25c Roth distribution basis ordering). The three "taxable subtotal" lines (15c, 18, 25c) sum to the Form 8606 override value used for Line 4b. |

### 2.2 Married Filing Separately guard

Same as Line 4a — the orchestrator at `:7918–7949` is one of nine orchestrators in the codebase participating in the single-guard Married Filing Separately cascade. Under Married Filing Separately, the spouse's `ira-income-spouse` form is read as null, the spouse Social Security Number is read as null, and the spouse-side `computeIraForPerson` returns an empty `IraPersonComputation` whose `taxableIraDistributions` is null. The aggregation at `:8216` becomes taxpayer-only. Lock-in test `mfsExcludesSpouseIraFromLine4a` (also verifies Line 4b).

### 2.3 The Form 8606 override is an override, not an addition

Critical correctness rule: when Exception 2 applies, the Form 8606 result **replaces** `taxableAfterExceptions`. It does not add to it. The reason: Form 8606's own internal arithmetic (specifically Part I line 7) already accounts for rollovers, Qualified Charitable Distributions, Health Savings Account Funding Distributions, Roth conversions, returns-of-contribution, and inherited-Individual-Retirement-Account distributions by subtracting them from the gross before computing line 7. If the application added Form 8606's result to `taxableAfterExceptions`, those exceptions would be subtracted twice.

The override condition at `:8459` is `form8606Taxable != null`. The condition is a strict null check (not a "nonzero" check): per the canonical zero-vs-null convention, a null `form8606Taxable` means "Form 8606 was built but does not apply to Line 4b" (for example, only Part III is populated and its line 25c is zero); a zero `form8606Taxable` means "Form 8606 applies but the taxable result happens to be zero" (full basis recovery). In both non-null cases the override fires, replacing `taxableAfterExceptions`.

### 2.4 The five Exception 2 (Form 8606) triggers

`hasException2` is true when any of:

| # | Trigger | Source field | Form 8606 path activated |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | `hasNondeductibleTraditionalBasis == true` | `form-ira-income-{taxpayer,spouse}.xlsx` | Part I |
| 2 | `hasTraditionalToRothConversion == true` | `form-ira-income-{taxpayer,spouse}.xlsx` | Parts I + II |
| 3 | `hasRothIraDistributions == true` AND not all Roth-coded 1099-Rs are code Q | `form-ira-income-{taxpayer,spouse}.xlsx` + 1099-R box 7 | Part III |
| 4 | `hadReturnOfContributionOrRecharacterization == true` | `form-ira-income-{taxpayer,spouse}.xlsx` | Part I (excluded from line 7) |
| 5 | At least one uploaded Individual Retirement Account 1099-R has box 7 containing code J or T (auto-detected) | `form-1099-r.xlsx` | Part III |

Trigger #3's "not all Roth-coded entries are code Q" guard (`allRothEntriesFullyQualified`) was the fix for Known Limitation #1 from `lines/4abc.md`, applied 2026-04-14. Without it, fully qualified Roth distributions (code Q) would have wrongly generated a Form 8606.

### 2.5 The post-70½ own-vs-inherited Qualified Charitable Distribution carve-out

Internal Revenue Code §408(d)(8)(B) requires the Qualified Charitable Distribution exclusion to be reduced dollar-for-dollar by deductible Individual Retirement Account contributions made after age 70½. IRS Publication 590-B clarifies that the reduction applies only to Qualified Charitable Distributions from the taxpayer's own Individual Retirement Account, not from an inherited Individual Retirement Account (which has the decedent's separately-tracked basis).

The application's five-step computation flow at `:8375–8381`:

1. `totalQcdAmount` — user-entered (includes own + inherited).
2. `inheritedIraQcdAmount` — user-entered (subset of total).
3. `ownIraQcdAmount = max(0, totalQcdAmount − inheritedIraQcdAmount)`.
4. `effectiveOwnQcd = max(0, ownIraQcdAmount − deductibleIraContributionsAfterAge70Half)`.
5. `effectiveQcdAmount = effectiveOwnQcd + inheritedIraQcdAmount` (inherited rejoins unscathed, bypassing the reduction).

This was the fix for Known Limitation #2 from `lines/4abc.md`, applied 2026-04-15.

### 2.6 Canonical zero-vs-null compliance

All BigDecimal fields on `IraPersonComputation` (`grossIraDistributions`, `taxableIraDistributions`) follow the "null when the concept does not apply, zero only when the concept applies but sums to zero" convention. The taxable path:

- `taxableBox2a` starts at null;
- `addNonNull(null, parseAmount(entry.taxableAmountAmount))` returns `parseAmount(entry.taxableAmountAmount)` if any IRA 1099-R matches this person, else stays null;
- `subtractNonNegative(null, x)` returns null per helper semantics;
- `taxableAmount = taxableAfterExceptions` passes through null when no entries matched;
- Form 8606 override fires only when `hasException2` and `form8606Taxable != null`; when `form8606Taxable` is null the value stays as the null `taxableAfterExceptions`;
- `roundMoney(null)` returns null;
- The constructor returns null.

This is critical for downstream behavior — `buildIncome` at `:6711-6713` only sets `setTaxableIraDistributions(line4b)` when `line4b != null`. Zero would display "$0" on the PDF — wrong for users with no Individual Retirement Account activity.

### 2.7 Whole-dollar rounding applied once at the end

Each per-person `taxableAmount` is rounded once via `roundMoney()` before going into `IraPersonComputation`. The orchestrator then adds (already-rounded) taxpayer and spouse amounts and rounds again — but since both inputs are whole-dollar values, the second rounding is a no-op. This matches the IRS general instruction to round each line independently to the nearest whole dollar.

## 6. Common mistakes (Line 4b specifically)

- **Forgetting to subtract the rollover amount.** Users sometimes assume the 1099-R payer already excluded the rollover from box 2a; in practice box 2a typically still includes the full distribution. The application's `taxableAfterExceptions` subtraction at `:8424-8427` is critical.
- **Subtracting the rollover both in Form 8606 line 7 AND in the simple `taxableAfterExceptions` — double counting.** The application avoids this by using `taxableAfterExceptions` as a fallback only; when `hasException2` triggers, Form 8606 takes over via the **override** branch at `:8459-8461` (not an addition). Without this override-vs-addition distinction, the rollover would be subtracted twice.
- **Including a code Q Roth 1099-R in the taxable amount.** Fully qualified Roth distributions are tax-free per Internal Revenue Code §408A(d)(2). Box 2a on a code-Q 1099-R is typically zero, so the arithmetic happens to work out. The application's `allRothEntriesFullyQualified` test at `:8346-8351` additionally prevents code-Q-only Roth distributions from triggering Form 8606 — that avoids generating an unnecessary form.
- **Computing the pro-rata basis fraction (Form 8606 line 10) at low precision.** The Internal Revenue Service requires at least 5 decimals; the application uses scale 10 HALF_UP for safety.
- **Mistaking a trustee-to-trustee transfer for a rollover.** A trustee-to-trustee transfer is not a distribution at all; no 1099-R should be issued, and nothing should reach Line 4a or Line 4b. The application has no detection for this case; it depends on the 1099-R presence.
- **Adding Form 8606 Part I line 15c, Part II line 18, and Part III line 25c when only one Part applies.** Each line can be null (when its Part doesn't apply); the `addNonNull` helper preserves null so the final `form8606Taxable` is null when no Part contributes. Without null-preservation, `null + null + null = $0` would falsely trigger the override.
- **Rounding intermediate values inside Form 8606.** The application rounds only at the end (in `roundMoney(line15c)`, `roundMoney(line18)`, etc., before the sum). Rounding intermediate values (for example, rounding the pro-rata ratio to 2 decimals before multiplying) would introduce errors on the order of dollars.
- **Computing line 4b for a Married-Filing-Separately spouse.** Each Married-Filing-Separately spouse files an independent return. The application's spouse suppression at the orchestrator level ensures the spouse's 1099-Rs cannot leak onto the taxpayer's Line 4b.
