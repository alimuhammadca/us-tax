# Form 1040 — Line 6a — Social Security benefits (gross net-benefit amount)

**Tax year: 2025**

This document explains how the dollar amount that goes on **Line 6a** of Form 1040 is calculated. Line 6a is the **total of all net Social Security benefits** the taxpayer (and the spouse, on a joint return) received from the Social Security Administration and from the Railroad Retirement Board during the tax year, before any taxability test is applied. The amount that is actually subject to income tax is computed separately on Line 6b; the formula and audit for Line 6b live in `6b.md` in this same folder.

The document is organized in three parts:

1. **The IRS rule.** What the controlling Internal Revenue Service publications say must happen. This is the source of truth — the application must match it.
2. **The code implementation.** What the application's backend Java code currently does.
3. **The comparison.** A side-by-side check showing whether the code follows the IRS rule honestly, and where the two diverge.

Note on terminology used throughout:

- **Internal Revenue Service** is abbreviated as IRS. The IRS is the United States federal agency that collects taxes.
- **Social Security Administration** is abbreviated as SSA. The SSA is the United States federal agency that pays Social Security retirement, survivor, and disability benefits.
- **Railroad Retirement Board** is abbreviated as RRB. The RRB is a separate federal agency that administers retirement benefits for railroad workers.
- **Form SSA-1099** (Social Security Benefit Statement) is the year-end form the SSA sends to anyone who received Social Security benefits. It reports the gross benefits, repayments, and net benefits for the year.
- **Form RRB-1099** (Payments by the Railroad Retirement Board) is the year-end form the RRB sends to railroad retirees. It reports the **Social Security Equivalent Benefit (SSEB)** portion of Tier 1 railroad retirement benefits — the portion that is taxed under the same rules as Social Security.
- **Form RRB-1099-R** (Annuities or Pensions by the Railroad Retirement Board) is a *different* RRB statement that reports the *non*-SSEB portion of Tier 1 plus all of Tier 2 plus Vested Dual Benefits plus Supplemental Annuity. These amounts are taxed as pensions and do **not** belong on Line 6a — they belong on Form 1040 Line 5a/5b. Do not confuse Form RRB-1099 (blue form, SSEB only) with Form RRB-1099-R (green form, pension-equivalent).
- **Supplemental Security Income** is abbreviated as SSI. SSI is a separate federal cash-assistance program for low-income aged, blind, or disabled individuals. It is administered by the SSA but is **not** Social Security retirement or disability income. SSI is **never** taxable and must never appear on Line 6a.
- **Married Filing Separately** is abbreviated as MFS. It is one of the five Form 1040 filing statuses.
- **Married Filing Jointly** is abbreviated as MFJ. It is the filing status used by most married couples.

## Part 1 — The IRS rule (the source of truth)

### 1.1 Authority

The IRS publications used to write this document are in `C:\us-tax\docs\books\`:

- **2025 Instructions for Form 1040 / 1040-SR** (`i1040gi_2025.pdf` / `i1040gi_2025.txt`) — the controlling rule for the Line 6a label, routing, and worksheet selection.
- **Publication 17 — Your Federal Income Tax** (`p17.pdf`) — Chapter 7 covers Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits.
- **Publication 915 — Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits** — the deep reference for who-is-taxed, what-is-included, the standard worksheet, and the lump-sum election worksheets. (The 2025 edition of Pub. 915 is not in the `books` folder of this repository; the relevant rules are quoted in the 2025 Form 1040 Instructions which are present.)
- **Publication 525 — Taxable and Nontaxable Income** (`p525.pdf`) — confirms SSI is excluded from gross income.
- **Form SSA-1099 instructions** (printed on the back of the form) — defines what Box 3, Box 4, and Box 5 of Form SSA-1099 contain.
- **Form RRB-1099 instructions** (printed on the back of the form) — defines what Box 3, Box 4, Box 5 of Form RRB-1099 contain, and confirms that Form RRB-1099 reports SSEB only.

### 1.2 The basic rule (verbatim from the 2025 Instructions)

From the 2025 Instructions for Form 1040, Line 6a (also reproduced from Pub. 17 Chapter 7):

> Report your net benefits (the total amount from box 5 of all your Forms SSA-1099 and Forms RRB-1099) on line 6a and the taxable part on line 6b.
>
> Even if none of your benefits are taxable, you still need to enter the total amount on line 6a.

In plain English: Line 6a is the **sum of Box 5 from every Form SSA-1099 and every Form RRB-1099** received by the taxpayer (and the spouse, on a joint return). The fact that the resulting amount may turn out to be entirely non-taxable on Line 6b does *not* allow the taxpayer to leave Line 6a blank. The IRS audit-matching process compares Line 6a to the totals SSA and RRB report to the IRS, so the field must always be populated whenever the concept applies.

### 1.3 What "Box 5 net benefits" already includes (no further math required)

Per the Form SSA-1099 instructions printed on the back of the form:

- **Box 3 (Benefits Paid in [year])** is the total *gross* Social Security benefits paid during the tax year.
- **Box 4 (Benefits Repaid to SSA in [year])** is any benefits the recipient repaid to the SSA during the same tax year. The SSA may require repayment, for example, when an earlier overpayment is recovered, or when the recipient returned to work and earned over the annual earnings limit.
- **Box 5 (Net Benefits for [year])** is defined as **Box 3 minus Box 4**.

Form RRB-1099 (blue form) uses the same structure for the SSEB portion:

- **Box 3 (Gross SSEB Paid in [year])** is the gross SSEB amount paid.
- **Box 4 (SSEB Repaid in [year])** is the SSEB amount the recipient repaid.
- **Box 5 (Net SSEB for [year])** is defined as **Box 3 minus Box 4**.

Because Box 5 already reflects the net-of-repayments computation, the Line 6a calculation reads Box 5 directly. It does **not** independently subtract Box 4 from Box 3 (doing so would double-subtract the repayments).

### 1.4 What is included and what is excluded

#### Included on Line 6a (per Pub. 17 Chapter 7 and Pub. 915)

| Category | Why included |
|---|---|
| Social Security retirement benefits | Reported on Form SSA-1099 Box 5 |
| Social Security survivor benefits | Reported on Form SSA-1099 Box 5 |
| Social Security disability benefits (Social Security Disability Insurance) | Still a Social Security benefit; reported on Form SSA-1099 Box 5 |
| Lump-sum back payment of Social Security benefits for an earlier year | Reported in the current-year Form SSA-1099 Box 5; full amount belongs on Line 6a even when the recipient elects the lump-sum-election method (the election only changes Line 6b, never Line 6a) |
| Social Security Equivalent Benefit portion of Tier 1 railroad retirement | Reported on Form RRB-1099 Box 5 (blue form) |
| Spouse's benefits and repayments (on a joint return only) | Joint return aggregates both spouses' net benefits on Line 6a |

#### Excluded from Line 6a (per Pub. 17 Chapter 7 and Pub. 915)

| Category | Why excluded | Where it goes instead (if anywhere) |
|---|---|---|
| Supplemental Security Income (SSI) | Per Internal Revenue Code §86(d)(1)(D), SSI is not "Social Security benefits" for tax purposes; it is a need-based welfare payment funded from general revenues, not from the Social Security trust fund. SSI is **never** taxable. | Not reported anywhere on Form 1040 |
| Non-SSEB portion of Tier 1 railroad retirement (the part of Tier 1 that exceeds what Social Security would have paid) | Taxed as a pension under Internal Revenue Code §72, not under the Social Security taxability rules of §86. Reported on Form RRB-1099-R (green form), not on Form RRB-1099 (blue form). | Form 1040 Line 5a (gross) / Line 5b (taxable) — pension and annuity income |
| Tier 2 railroad retirement | Also taxed as a pension under §72. Reported on Form RRB-1099-R (green form). | Form 1040 Line 5a / Line 5b |
| Vested Dual Benefit (a railroad-retirement add-on for certain dual-railroad-Social-Security retirees) | Also taxed as a pension. Reported on Form RRB-1099-R. | Form 1040 Line 5a / Line 5b |
| Supplemental Annuity (a Tier 2 add-on for certain career railroad workers) | Also taxed as a pension. Reported on Form RRB-1099-R. | Form 1040 Line 5a / Line 5b |
| One-time SSA / RRB lump-sum death benefit (a small one-time payment to a surviving spouse or family member when the wage-earner dies) | Per Pub. 17 Chapter 7: "No part of the lump-sum death benefit is subject to tax." Not reported on Form SSA-1099 Box 5 in the first place. | Not reported anywhere on Form 1040 |
| Foreign social-security benefits | Not US Social Security; taxed as a foreign pension or annuity unless a tax treaty applies. | Form 1040 Line 5a / Line 5b or under treaty rules |
| Workers' compensation offset (the part of an SSA disability benefit that is reduced because the recipient also receives Workers' Compensation) | Box 5 of Form SSA-1099 *includes* the offset amount because the IRS treats the offset as still being Social Security for tax purposes. No action needed at Line 6a; this is mentioned only to dispel a common confusion. | Already included in Box 5 — no separate handling |

### 1.5 Special situations that change how Line 6a is reported

#### 1.5.1 Repayments exceed gross benefits (negative Box 5)

If the taxpayer repaid the SSA more than the SSA paid them during the tax year, Box 5 will be a negative number. Per Pub. 17 Chapter 7 (under "Repayment of benefits"):

- If the total repayment of benefits across all of the taxpayer's Forms SSA-1099 and RRB-1099 is more than the gross benefits, **the taxpayer does not enter a negative amount on Line 6a**. The taxpayer enters zero on Line 6a (or follows the alternative reporting paths spelled out in Pub. 915 for "Repayments More Than Gross Benefits", which may permit a deduction on Schedule A or a credit under Internal Revenue Code §1341 depending on the size and other facts).
- The application must not silently propagate a negative Line 6a to downstream lines.

#### 1.5.2 Benefits paid on behalf of a child or an incompetent person

Per Pub. 17 Chapter 7 (under "Who is taxed"): the benefits belong, for tax purposes, to the person who has the **legal right** to receive them, not to the person who physically deposited the check. If the taxpayer's name is on the SSA payee record because the taxpayer is acting as a representative payee for a child or for an incompetent adult, those benefits are *not* the taxpayer's for Line 6a purposes. They belong to the child or the incompetent person.

#### 1.5.3 Lump-sum back payment for an earlier year (the lump-sum election method)

If 2025 Box 5 includes a lump-sum back payment for one or more earlier years, **the full Box 5 amount still goes on Line 6a** unchanged. The lump-sum election method, if chosen, only affects Line 6b (the taxable amount). The taxpayer checks the Line 6c checkbox when the election is used. See `6c.md` and `6b.md` for the election mechanics.

### 1.6 The complete IRS equation for Line 6a (plain English)

Putting all parts together:

```text
For each Form SSA-1099 that belongs to the taxpayer:
    if the form belongs to a child or incompetent person for whom the
    taxpayer is only the representative payee:
        skip this form — the benefits are not the taxpayer's for tax purposes
    otherwise:
        add Form SSA-1099 Box 5 (Net Benefits for [year]) to running total

For each Form RRB-1099 (blue form) that belongs to the taxpayer:
    add Form RRB-1099 Box 5 (Net SSEB for [year]) to running total
    (Skip Form RRB-1099-R entirely — those amounts belong on Lines 5a/5b.)

If the filing status is Married Filing Jointly, repeat both loops above
for the spouse and add the spouse's totals to the running total.

If the taxpayer (or, on a joint return, the spouse) received any
Supplemental Security Income during the year, do NOT add the SSI to
the running total. (SSI is reported on a separate SSA notice, not on
Form SSA-1099 Box 5, so the running total should already exclude SSI;
the rule is restated here as a guardrail in case any SSI was mistakenly
lumped in.)

If the running total is negative (the rare case in which repayments
exceeded gross benefits), follow the Pub. 915 "Repayments More Than
Gross Benefits" rules. Otherwise:

    Line 6a = running total, rounded to whole dollars
```

### 1.7 Reference-data constants used by Line 6a (none)

Line 6a is a pure sum of statement values. It does **not** depend on any IRS-published threshold, base amount, percentage, or filing-status-specific number. All of the IRS reference-data constants for the Social Security area (the $25,000 / $32,000 / $34,000 / $44,000 base and adjusted-base amounts; the 50% and 85% taxability multipliers) apply only to the dependent calculation for Line 6b and are listed in `6b.md`.

This is important to recognize: a future change in tax law that adjusts those thresholds will not change the Line 6a number.

### 2.4 The code's implemented equation for Line 6a (plain English of the algorithm)

```text
Step 1 — Detect Married Filing Separately and suppress spouse reads if so.
    Read the filing-status form. If filing status is "Married filing separately":
        spouseSocialSecurityForm = null
        spouseSsn = null
    Otherwise:
        use the actual spouse personal form and the actual spouse Social Security Number.

Step 2 — Compute the per-person net-benefits total for the taxpayer
         by calling computeSocialSecurityForPerson(...) with spousePerson=false.

    Inside the per-person aggregator:

        taxpayerGrossBenefits = null         (start with no value)

        For each uploaded Form SSA-1099 entry:
            decide if it belongs to the taxpayer by calling belongsToPersonSsa1099:
                if the entry has a beneficiarySSN value:
                    it belongs to the taxpayer if and only if
                    beneficiarySSN matches the taxpayer's Social Security Number.
                if the entry has no beneficiarySSN value (fallback heuristic):
                    it belongs to the taxpayer when either:
                        the spouse did NOT claim Social Security benefits, OR
                        the taxpayer did claim Social Security benefits.
                    (This is the catch-all: a statement with a missing
                    beneficiarySSN that could only sensibly belong to the
                    taxpayer is attributed to the taxpayer.)
            if it belongs to the taxpayer:
                add the entry's netBenefitsAmount (Box 5) to taxpayerGrossBenefits.

        For each uploaded Form RRB-1099 entry:
            decide if it belongs to the taxpayer by calling belongsToPersonRrb1099,
            using the same rule but checking recipientIdNumber against the
            taxpayer's Social Security Number.
            if it belongs to the taxpayer:
                add the entry's netSSEBAmount (Box 5) to taxpayerGrossBenefits.

        If the taxpayer's personal-form field hasSupplementalSecurityIncome is true:
            taxpayerGrossBenefits = taxpayerGrossBenefits - supplementalSecurityIncomeAmount
            (with two safety guards:
              (a) if taxpayerGrossBenefits was null at this point, leave it null —
                  do not turn it into a negative number, and
              (b) if the subtraction would produce a negative number, clamp to zero.
              The clamp is done by a helper called subtractNonNegative.)

        Round taxpayerGrossBenefits to whole dollars (using ROUND_HALF_UP) and
        return it.

Step 3 — Compute the per-person net-benefits total for the spouse the same way,
         by calling computeSocialSecurityForPerson(...) with spousePerson=true.
         (When the MFS guard fired in Step 1, the spouse's personal form is null,
         the spouse Social Security Number is null, and the attribution helper
         will reject every Form SSA-1099 and every Form RRB-1099 — so the spouse
         total ends up as null, contributing nothing to Line 6a.)

Step 4 — Add the two per-person totals.

    Line 6a = roundMoney( addNonNull(taxpayerGrossBenefits, spouseGrossBenefits) )

    addNonNull is a helper that treats null as zero only when at least one operand
    is non-null. If both operands are null, the result is null (meaning "the
    Social Security concept does not apply to this return"), not zero.

Step 5 — Negative-Line-6a guardrail (§17 + auto-remedy, post-2026-06-05).

    If Line 6a is non-null and is less than zero (the rare case where SSA / RRB
    repayments exceed gross benefits during the year):
      (a) Clamp Line 6a (and Line 6b) to exactly $0 explicitly per IRS Pub. 17
          Chapter 7 reporting rule. The previous behavior of returning null was
          replaced because a null Line 6a created an IRS document-matching /
          CP2000 risk.
      (b) Record the absolute-value excess on the returned SocialSecurityComputation
          record's `excessSsaRrbRepayment` field. This value is consumed downstream
          by `buildScheduleA` (which augments Schedule A line 16 if no §1341 credit
          is on file) and by `maybeEmitExcessSsaRrbRepaymentFlag` (which conditionally
          fires either the §17 non-overrideable block or the non-blocking advisory).
    Flag firing is DEFERRED to after Line 12 (see Step 7 below) so the itemize-vs-
    standard election is known. The advisory-only flag from the pre-fix design
    (SOCIAL_SECURITY_NEGATIVE_NET_BENEFITS_MANUAL_REVIEW) was removed in this build.

Step 6 — Concept-not-applicable guardrail.

    If Line 6a came out null and neither the taxpayer nor the spouse had said
    "Yes" to the screening question hadSocialSecurityBenefits, the whole Social
    Security computation returns null (meaning the Line 6a and Line 6b fields are
    omitted from the output JSON entirely, rather than showing a misleading $0.00).

Step 7 — SSI-amount-required validator (added 2026-06-05; Gap 3a closure).

    Per spouse, BEFORE computing benefits: if hasSupplementalSecurityIncome=true
    but supplementalSecurityIncomeAmount is missing or zero, emit the §17 non-
    overrideable flag SOCIAL_SECURITY_SSI_AMOUNT_REQUIRED_<PERSON>. Without the
    amount the helper subtractNonNegative treats the missing right operand as
    zero, leaving any SSI lumped into Box 5 inside Line 6a and over-stating
    taxable income. Resolution paths are pure user-side data fixes (enter the
    amount, or un-check the SSI flag); no override path is offered.

Step 8 — Excess-repayment flag emitter (added 2026-06-05; Gap 1 closure).

    Called from prepare() AFTER computeLine12 completes, so the itemize-vs-
    standard election on scheduleA is known. Reads:
      - socialSecurity.excessSsaRrbRepayment() (from Step 5)
      - the user-entered §1341 credit on the Other Payments personal form
      - scheduleA.usedForItemizedDeduction()
    Decision matrix:
      * No excess → no flag.
      * §1341 credit > 0 → no flag (alternative remedy in place; Schedule A
        line 16 auto-add was already suppressed in buildScheduleA).
      * Itemized in use → no flag (Schedule A line 16 auto-add carried through).
      * Standard deduction in use AND excess > $3,000 AND no §1341 → §17
        non-overrideable block SOCIAL_SECURITY_EXCESS_REPAYMENT_REQUIRES_MANUAL_HANDLING.
        User must enter §1341 on the Other Payments form, or switch the
        Standard Deductions personal form to itemize.
      * Standard deduction in use AND excess ≤ $3,000 → non-blocking advisory
        SOCIAL_SECURITY_EXCESS_REPAYMENT_NO_IN_RETURN_REMEDY. No IRS-available
        in-return remedy; advisory points the user at Form 1040-X for the
        prior year (within 3 years per IRC §6511).
```

### 2.5 The code's reference-data constants used by Line 6a

The pure Line 6a aggregation uses no numeric constant. (As noted in section 1.7, the $25,000 / $32,000 / $34,000 / $44,000 base amounts and the 50% / 85% multipliers belong to Line 6b.)

The post-2026-06-05 Gap 1 closure introduces **one** Line-6a-adjacent threshold inside `maybeEmitExcessSsaRrbRepaymentFlag`: the **$3,000 IRC §1341 qualifying-excess threshold** (referenced as `new BigDecimal("3000")` in the comparison). This threshold is an IRS-defined statutory boundary (IRC §1341(a)(3)), not a reference-data parameter that varies year-over-year. It selects between the §17 hard block (excess > $3,000, where the §1341 credit becomes the user's remedy) and the non-blocking advisory (excess ≤ $3,000, where Schedule A line 16 is the only available remedy and a standard-deduction-filing user has no in-return path).

### 3.1 Side-by-side equation comparison

| Aspect | IRS rule (Part 1) | Code (Part 2) | Match? |

| Inputs | Box 5 of every Form SSA-1099 plus Box 5 of every Form RRB-1099 (SSEB form, blue) | `netBenefitsAmount` for SSA-1099 entries, `netSSEBAmount` for RRB-1099 entries | **Match** |

### 3.2 Verified-correct items

- **Box-5 direct read.** The code reads `netBenefitsAmount` (Box 5) and `netSSEBAmount` (Box 5) directly, exactly as the IRS rule prescribes. It does not re-derive net from gross (Box 3) and repayment (Box 4), which would double-subtract.
- **RRB scope limit.** The code uses a different input collection (`formRrb1099Entries`, sourced from `form-rrb-1099`) for the SSEB-only blue form, distinct from `formRrb1099REntries` (sourced from `form-rrb-1099-r`) which feeds the pension cluster on Line 5a/5b. There is no risk of a single RRB recipient having their Tier 1 SSEB portion and Tier 2 portion both arriving at Line 6a.
- **Attribution by Social Security Number.** Both `belongsToPersonSsa1099` and `belongsToPersonRrb1099` perform exact-match comparison of normalized (digit-only) Social Security Numbers, after which the entry is routed to the correct spouse. Fallback heuristics apply only when the statement has no Social Security Number recorded.
- **MFS guard.** When the filing status is Married Filing Separately, the orchestrator suppresses every spouse-side read: the spouse personal form is set to null, the spouse Social Security Number is set to null, and the fallback heuristic in the attribution helpers refuses to route entries to the spouse without a matching Social Security Number. The result is that an MFS taxpayer never picks up the absent spouse's stale Form SSA-1099 data.
- **Null-vs-zero discipline.** Three distinct outcomes are preserved: (a) when neither the taxpayer nor the spouse has any Social Security activity at all, Line 6a is emitted as null (the field is absent from the output JSON); (b) when at least one person had Social Security activity but the running total computes to zero because the entire amount was offset by SSI, Line 6a is emitted as 0.00; (c) **post-2026-06-05**, when net benefits are negative (excess repayments), Line 6a is emitted as exactly 0.00 explicitly per IRS Pub. 17 Ch. 7 (was: null pre-fix, which created an IRS document-matching / CP2000 risk). This distinction matters because downstream consumers of `form1040.income.socialSecurityBenefits` use the field's presence to decide whether to render a Line 6a row on the printed Form 1040 PDF.
- **Whole-dollar rounding.** The code wraps the final sum in `roundMoney`, which uses banker's-half-up rounding to whole dollars, matching the IRS practice for Form 1040 line entries.
- **Unit-test coverage.** Nine unit tests in `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest` exercise the Line 6a / 6b / 6c / 6d compute (five original + the four added 2026-06-05 for the §17 + auto-remedy build: `blocksWhenSsiFlagSetButNoSsiAmountProvided`, `smallExcessRepaymentWithStandardDeductionEmitsNoInReturnRemedyAdvisory`, `largeExcessRepaymentWithStandardDeductionAndNoSection1341FiresSection17Block`, `largeExcessRepaymentWithSection1341OnOtherPaymentsSuppressesFlag`). Eleven e2e tests in `e2e/tests/line6abcd-social-security-benefits.spec.ts` cover the full UI-to-compute round trip — see `test_cases/6a.md` for the per-test audit and gap closures.

Each gap below is rated by severity:

- **Material:** the code may produce a wrong Line 6a in a real scenario; an end user would be filing an incorrect Form 1040.
- **Advisory:** the code produces the right Line 6a in normal cases but does not actively help the user when the IRS prescribes a special workflow.

#### Gap 1 — Repayments exceed gross benefits — CLOSED 2026-06-05 (§17 + auto-remedy)

**Status:** **CLOSED 2026-06-05** with §17 non-overrideable promotion + auto-remedy build. The §18 blocking-with-override was promoted to §17 non-overrideable per the product principle that *the application must not allow filing a return that produces avoidable user financial loss*, paired with an auto-remedy build so users with legitimate excess repayments are not locked out of filing.

**IRS rule (Pub. 17 Chapter 7; Pub. 915 "Repayments More Than Gross Benefits"):** When the total repayment of benefits across all of the taxpayer's Forms SSA-1099 and RRB-1099 exceeds the gross benefits received:

1. **Line 6a is reported as zero** (not negative), and **Line 6b is also zero**.
2. The taxpayer may be entitled to a **deduction on Schedule A line 16** ("Other Itemized Deductions") if the excess repayment is $3,000 or less, **or** a **tax credit under Internal Revenue Code §1341** ("Claim of Right" credit) if the excess repayment is over $3,000. The taxpayer chooses whichever of the deduction or the credit produces the lower tax (alternative remedies — not both).

**Fixes applied (2026-06-05) — no schema changes; reuses existing output-model fields:**

1. **Gap 1a (IRS document-matching protection — code-only):** in `computeSocialSecurityBenefits` the negative-Line-6a branch now sets `line6a = BigDecimal.ZERO` explicitly (was: returned null and omitted the field). The excess-repayment magnitude is captured on the `SocialSecurityComputation` record's new `excessSsaRrbRepayment` field for downstream wiring.

2. **Gap 1b — Schedule A line 16 auto-add (auto-remedy):** in `prepare()`, the excess is propagated into `computeLine12` via a new `autoOtherItemizedAddon` parameter. Inside `buildScheduleA`, this addon is **added to the existing `otherAllowedItemizedDeductions` field on `ScheduleA`** (no new output-model field). The existing line-12 itemize-vs-standard election logic then naturally picks itemized over standard when the augmented Schedule A total exceeds the standard deduction. **Suppressed** when the user has already entered a §1341 credit on the Other Payments personal form (alternative remedies — not both).

3. **Gap 1b — §1341 credit wiring (auto-remedy):** the §1341 credit path was already fully built — `hasSection1341Credit` + `section1341Credit` fields on the existing `31-other-payments` personal form, wired into the existing `Schedule3OtherPaymentsCredits.section1341Credit` field (Schedule 3 line 13b) via `applyOtherPaymentsFormToSchedule3` at `TaxReturnComputeService.java:22173-22180`. No new fields required. The §17 flag message points the user at this existing field.

4. **Gap 1b — Conditional §17 firing (replaces the §18 promotion):** `SOCIAL_SECURITY_EXCESS_REPAYMENT_REQUIRES_MANUAL_HANDLING` is now registered in `NonOverrideableFlags` and fires only in the narrow case where no in-app remedy is in effect AND the IRS provides one:
   - Excess > $3,000 AND user takes standard deduction AND no §1341 credit entered → **§17 non-overrideable block.** User must either enter §1341 on the Other Payments form, or switch the Standard Deductions personal form to itemize.
   - Excess ≤ $3,000 AND user takes standard deduction → **non-blocking advisory** `SOCIAL_SECURITY_EXCESS_REPAYMENT_NO_IN_RETURN_REMEDY`. No IRS-available in-return remedy (≤$3,000 requires Schedule A; standard deduction is mathematically optimal). Advisory points the user at Form 1040-X for the prior year.
   - User itemizes (regardless of excess size) → **no flag**. The Schedule A line 16 auto-add carried through.
   - §1341 entered → **no flag**. Schedule A auto-add is suppressed; §1341 flows to Schedule 3 line 13b.

5. The flag-firing decision is **deferred to after `computeLine12`** so that the itemize-vs-standard election is known before the flag-emission helper `maybeEmitExcessSsaRrbRepaymentFlag` is called.

**Tests:**
- `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.smallExcessRepaymentWithStandardDeductionEmitsNoInReturnRemedyAdvisory` — $1k excess + std ded → advisory (non-blocking), no §17.
- `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.largeExcessRepaymentWithStandardDeductionAndNoSection1341FiresSection17Block` — $10k excess + std ded + no §1341 → §17 fires.
- `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.largeExcessRepaymentWithSection1341OnOtherPaymentsSuppressesFlag` — $10k excess + §1341 $2,400 → no flag, §1341 flows to Schedule 3 line 13b.
- E2E test in `line6abcd-social-security-benefits.spec.ts` — updated to use the 409 §17 response pattern.

**Residual scope:** the §1341 credit amount itself is still user-provided (not auto-computed) because the §1341 calculation requires the prior-year tax recomputed without the repaid amount, which the single-tax-year application does not have. The user is expected to compute the credit externally (with a CPA or prior-year tax software using the IRS Publication 525 §1341 worksheet) and enter the figure on the Other Payments form. Auto-computation of §1341 from prior-year tax data is out of scope.

#### Gap 2 — Representative-payee case is already handled correctly by the existing SSN-attribution logic (NOT a gap)

**IRS rule (Pub. 17 Chapter 7 "Who is taxed"):** When the taxpayer receives an SSA benefit check on behalf of someone else (typically a minor child or an incompetent adult relative for whom the taxpayer is the legal representative payee), those benefits belong on the *beneficiary's* return, not the taxpayer's.

**SSA practice:** The Social Security Administration **prints Form SSA-1099 in the beneficiary's name and with the beneficiary's Social Security Number, not the representative payee's**, even when the actual benefit check is mailed to the representative payee. This is the SSA's standard issuance rule (confirmed in the SSA's representative-payee guidance and in the form instructions printed on the back of Form SSA-1099). The representative payee never appears on the form's identification fields.

**Code behavior:** The application's attribution logic at `belongsToPersonSsa1099` matches the form's `beneficiarySSN` field (Box 2) against the taxpayer's and spouse's Social Security Numbers. When a representative payee uploads a child's Form SSA-1099 in error:

- The `beneficiarySSN` will be the child's SSN.
- It will not match the taxpayer's SSN nor the spouse's SSN.
- The attribution helper will return `false` for both calls.
- The entry is silently excluded from Line 6a, which is the IRS-correct outcome (the child's benefits belong on the child's return, not the parent's).

**Resolution:** The existing code is correct. No new field is needed and none can be added — Form SSA-1099 is an SSA-issued statement whose field layout is fixed by the federal agency. Adding an `isRepresentativePayeeOnly` toggle would have been a workaround for an SSA misfile scenario that does not arise under normal SSA operations. The user-facing remedy for the rare SSA-misfile case is to ask the SSA to reissue the form with the beneficiary's correct SSN — not to add a field in the application.

**Impact:** No defect. The representative-payee scenario is handled correctly by attribution-by-SSN.

#### Gap 3 — No guard against accidentally lumping SSI into Form SSA-1099 Box 5 — FULLY CLOSED 2026-06-05

**Status (re-analyzed 2026-06-05):**

- **Gap 3a — SSI flag set without an amount (Material; user-financial-loss only, no IRS audit risk):** When `hasSupplementalSecurityIncome=true` but `supplementalSecurityIncomeAmount` is missing/zero, the helper `subtractNonNegative` treats the missing right operand as zero and performs no subtraction. The user's stated intent (subtract the SSI they lumped into Box 5) silently fails, leaving the SSI in Line 6a and over-stating taxable income. **CLOSED 2026-06-05 — §17 non-overrideable flag added (`NonOverrideableFlags.SOCIAL_SECURITY_SSI_AMOUNT_REQUIRED_TAXPAYER` / `_SPOUSE`).** Initially classified as §18 blocking-with-override; promoted to §17 the same day under the product principle that the application must not allow filing a return with avoidable user financial loss.
- **Gap 3b — UX guidance to detect Box-5 / SSI confusion at data-entry time (Advisory; out of code scope):** The original gap "user might lump SSI into Box 5 in the first place" is a data-entry UX concern (transcription mistake on the SSA-1099 statement form), not a compute-time gap. The compute layer cannot detect this mistake because there is no way for it to know whether a Box 5 number was correctly or incorrectly transcribed. **Reclassified as UX-guidance-only** — to be addressed on the Form SSA-1099 input form via a "Box 5 should not include SSI" hint, tracked separately under the input-form UX roadmap.

**IRS rule:** SSI is administered by the SSA but is not reported on Form SSA-1099. The SSA sends a separate notice for SSI. Per the IRS, Box 5 of Form SSA-1099 should never include any SSI.

**Fix applied (2026-06-05) — new method `validateSocialSecuritySsiAmountProvided` in `TaxReturnComputeService.java`, called twice from the orchestrator (once for taxpayer, once for spouse) immediately after `validateSocialSecurityStatementGating`:**

- Reads `hasSupplementalSecurityIncome` from the per-person Social Security personal form. If the flag is not set, returns immediately.
- Reads `supplementalSecurityIncomeAmount`. If the amount is present and positive, returns.
- Otherwise emits a §17 non-overrideable flag `SOCIAL_SECURITY_SSI_AMOUNT_REQUIRED_TAXPAYER` (or `_SPOUSE`) with `blocking=true`. Message instructs the user to (a) enter the SSI amount they received during the tax year, or (b) un-check the SSI flag if no SSI was actually received this tax year. (Both resolution paths are pure user-side data fixes; the override path was removed in the §17 promotion 2026-06-05.)
- Registered in `NonOverrideableFlags.CODES` so the HTTP layer returns 409 regardless of `overrideFlags`.

**Tests added:**
- `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.blocksWhenSsiFlagSetButNoSsiAmountProvided` — unit test asserts the new flag is emitted with blocking=true when the SSI flag is set without an amount.
- `e2e/tests/line6abcd-social-security-benefits.spec.ts` "SSI flag with no amount fires blocking-with-override SSI_AMOUNT_REQUIRED flag" — full UI-to-compute round trip asserting the new flag.

**Why §17 non-overrideable (revised 2026-06-05):** Initially classified as §18 blocking-with-override on the grounds that the only consequence of overriding is user-side financial loss (over-payment of tax on never-taxable SSI). Promoted to §17 the same day under the product principle that the application must not allow filing a return that produces avoidable user financial loss. The override path was removed because both resolution paths (enter the amount, or un-check the flag) are pure user-side data fixes requiring zero external computation — there is no legitimate reason a user would *need* to override.

### 3.4 Missing input fields (consolidated list)

**None.** Every value that the IRS-prescribed Line 6a formula needs is already captured by an existing input field, either on a statement form (Form SSA-1099, Form RRB-1099) or on a personal form (`social-security-benefits-taxpayer`, `social-security-benefits-spouse`).

Important constraint that drives this conclusion: **statement forms are IRS-defined documents and we cannot add fields to them.** The application's statement schemas (`form-ssa-1099.xlsx`, `form-rrb-1099.xlsx`, `form-rrb-1099-r.xlsx`) mirror the boxes on the printed federal forms exactly. Adding a custom field to a statement schema would mean storing data the issuing agency (the Social Security Administration or the Railroad Retirement Board) never reported, which would be a data-fidelity violation. Therefore, the only places where the application can legitimately add a field are the **personal forms** and the **return-level forms** — not the statement schemas.

For Line 6a specifically, the IRS rules in Publication 17 Chapter 7 and the 2025 Form 1040 instructions do not require any taxpayer input that is not already captured by an existing field. The exhaustive inventory:

| What IRS Line 6a needs | Source | Captured by which existing field? |
|---|---|---|
| Total Form SSA-1099 Box 5 amounts | Form SSA-1099, Box 5 | `netBenefitsAmount` (statement) |
| Total Form RRB-1099 Box 5 SSEB amounts | Form RRB-1099, Box 5 | `netSSEBAmount` (statement) |
| Person-to-statement attribution | Form SSA-1099 Box 2 / Form RRB-1099 Box 2 | `beneficiarySSN` / `recipientIdNumber` (statement), compared to taxpayer / spouse SSN from `identification-taxpayer` / `identification-spouse` personal forms |
| Filing status (for the spouse-inclusion rule and the MFS guard) | Personal | `filingStatus` (filing-status form) |
| SSI exclusion screening flag | Personal | `hasSupplementalSecurityIncome` (`social-security-benefits-*` form) |
| SSI exclusion amount | Personal | `supplementalSecurityIncomeAmount` (`social-security-benefits-*` form) |
| Workers' compensation offset adjustment | Form SSA-1099 Box 6 / Form RRB-1099 Box 6 | Captured as `workersCompOffsetAmount` on Form RRB-1099 input; not needed by Line 6a math because the SSA / RRB has already incorporated the offset into Box 5 for tax purposes |
| Lump-sum back-payment allocation by prior year | Personal (Lump-Sum Election Method only) | `lumpSumDetails[].priorTaxYear` / `lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear` (`social-security-benefits-*` form) — used by Line 6b / 6c, not by Line 6a |

What about the §1341-credit / Schedule A-deduction discussion in Gap 1? The data needed for that calculation (Form SSA-1099 Box 3 gross paid and Box 4 repaid) is already captured by the statement input fields `benefitsPaidGrossAmount` and `benefitsRepaidAmount`. The fields exist but are not currently read by the Line 6a code. Gap 1 is therefore a **code gap, not a missing-field gap.**

What about the representative-payee discussion in Gap 2? That case is handled correctly by the existing SSN-based attribution logic — no new field is required. Gap 2 is therefore **not a gap** in either sense.

Result: the IRS-defined statement schemas are immutable for this application, and the personal-form schemas already capture every Line 6a-relevant input. No suggested new field would be appropriate.

### 3.6 Summary verdict

For Line 6a specifically:

- **The code correctly computes the IRS-defined Line 6a amount in all normal cases.** The Box-5-direct-read approach, the per-person aggregation, the SSI subtraction, the MFS guard, the null-vs-zero discipline, and the whole-dollar rounding are all consistent with the 2025 IRS instructions and Publication 915.
- **Gap 1 closed 2026-06-05** (Material; §17 + auto-remedy). Negative-net handling rewritten: Line 6a now reports as $0 (was: silently nulled — IRS document-matching risk). Schedule A line 16 is **auto-augmented** with the excess repayment when no §1341 credit has been entered (alternative remedies — not both); the existing line-12 itemize-vs-standard election naturally picks the better path. The §1341 credit path is end-to-end wired (existing `hasSection1341Credit` + `section1341Credit` fields on the Other Payments personal form → existing Schedule 3 line 13b). Promoted to §17 non-overrideable; fires only in the narrow case where excess > $3,000 AND the user takes standard deduction AND no §1341 credit was entered (the one path where user financial loss would result from the application allowing the file). Non-blocking advisory `SOCIAL_SECURITY_EXCESS_REPAYMENT_NO_IN_RETURN_REMEDY` covers the ≤$3,000 + std-ded case where no IRS-available in-return remedy exists.
- **Gap 2 not-a-gap** — handled correctly by the existing SSN-based attribution logic: Form SSA-1099 is always issued in the actual beneficiary's name with the beneficiary's SSN, so a representative payee's misuploaded child-SSA-1099 is silently excluded from the taxpayer's Line 6a as required by the IRS rule.
- **Gap 3a closed 2026-06-05** (Material; §17 promotion). New `validateSocialSecuritySsiAmountProvided` emits a §17 non-overrideable flag `SOCIAL_SECURITY_SSI_AMOUNT_REQUIRED_TAXPAYER` / `_SPOUSE` when `hasSupplementalSecurityIncome=true` but the amount is missing/zero. Both resolution paths (enter the amount, or un-check the flag) are pure user-side data fixes — no override path is needed or offered.
- **Gap 3b reclassified as out-of-code-scope** — detecting a transcription mistake (SSI lumped into Box 5 at data entry) is a UX-on-the-input-form concern, tracked separately under the input-form UX roadmap.
- **No input fields are missing.** The IRS-defined statement schemas (Form SSA-1099, Form RRB-1099, Form RRB-1099-R) are fixed by the issuing agencies and cannot be extended; the application's personal-form schemas already capture every Line 6a-relevant taxpayer input. All closures above are code-side, not schema-side.

For Lines 6b, 6c, 6d, see the dedicated files `6b.md`, `6c.md`, `6d.md` in this same folder. Those files inherit Line 6a as an input and document any further gaps in the taxable-portion calculation, the lump-sum election method, and the Married-Filing-Separately-lived-apart checkbox.
