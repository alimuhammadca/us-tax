# Form 1040 — Line 6b — Taxable amount of Social Security benefits

**Tax year: 2025**

This document explains how the dollar amount that goes on **Line 6b** of Form 1040 is calculated. Line 6b is the **taxable portion** of the Social Security benefits the taxpayer (and the spouse, on a joint return) received during the tax year. The total net benefits go on Line 6a (computed by `6a.md` in this same folder); Line 6b applies the Internal Revenue Service's "provisional-income" test to figure out how much of that total is actually subject to federal income tax.

The document is organized in three parts:

1. **The IRS rule.** What the controlling Internal Revenue Service publications say must happen. This is the source of truth — the application must match it.
2. **The code implementation.** What the application's backend Java code currently does.
3. **The comparison.** A side-by-side check showing whether the code follows the IRS rule honestly, and where the two diverge.

Note on terminology used throughout (defined once, used repeatedly):

- **Internal Revenue Service** is abbreviated as IRS. The IRS is the United States federal agency that collects taxes.
- **Internal Revenue Code** is abbreviated as IRC. The IRC is the body of federal tax law passed by Congress; section numbers like "IRC §86" cite specific subsections.
- **Social Security Administration** is abbreviated as SSA. The SSA pays Social Security retirement, survivor, and disability benefits.
- **Railroad Retirement Board** is abbreviated as RRB. The RRB administers retirement benefits for railroad workers.
- **Social Security Equivalent Benefit** is abbreviated as SSEB. SSEB is the portion of railroad retirement tier 1 benefits that is taxed under the same rules as Social Security. It is reported on the blue Form RRB-1099.
- **Form SSA-1099** is the year-end statement the SSA sends to anyone who received Social Security benefits. Box 5 reports the net benefits for the year.
- **Form RRB-1099** (blue form) reports the SSEB portion of Tier 1 railroad retirement benefits.
- **Form RRB-1099-R** (green form, not blue) reports the *non*-SSEB Tier 1 portion + Tier 2 + Vested Dual Benefit + Supplemental Annuity. These are taxed as pensions and belong on Form 1040 Lines 5a / 5b — not on Lines 6a / 6b.
- **Supplemental Security Income** is abbreviated as SSI. SSI is a separate federal cash-assistance program for low-income aged, blind, or disabled individuals. It is administered by the SSA but is *not* taxable Social Security income; it is excluded from Lines 6a and 6b.
- **Adjusted Gross Income** is abbreviated as AGI. AGI is Form 1040 Line 11.
- **Provisional income** is a working term Pub. 915 uses for the modified-income figure that the Social Security Benefits Worksheet computes on its line 8 (one-half of benefits + most other income + tax-exempt interest + certain exclusion add-backs − certain Schedule 1 adjustments). The provisional income is what the worksheet compares to the filing-status thresholds.
- **Married Filing Jointly** is abbreviated as MFJ.
- **Married Filing Separately** is abbreviated as MFS.
- **Head of Household** is abbreviated as HOH.
- **Qualifying Surviving Spouse** is abbreviated as QSS.
- **Modified Adjusted Gross Income** is abbreviated as MAGI. The Social Security worksheet's "modified" figure is the provisional income defined above, which is *not* the same MAGI used by other forms (e.g., IRA deduction, education credits) but the terminology is intentionally similar.

## Part 1 — The IRS rule (the source of truth)

### 1.1 Authority

The IRS publications used to write this document are in `C:\us-tax\docs\books\`:

- **2025 Instructions for Form 1040 / 1040-SR** (`i1040gi_2025.pdf` / `i1040gi_2025.txt`) — contains the printed **Social Security Benefits Worksheet** (the 19-line standard worksheet) that the application implements as the default path.
- **Publication 17 — Your Federal Income Tax** (`p17.pdf`) — Chapter 7 ("Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits") narrates the rule in plain English. Chapter 7 is the consumer-readable version of Publication 915.
- **Publication 915 — Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits** — the deep reference. It contains the *standard* Worksheet 1 (when special add-backs apply) and the *lump-sum-election* Worksheets 2, 3, and 4. The printed Pub. 915 (2025 edition) is not in the `books` folder of this repository; the rules it contains are quoted in the 2025 Form 1040 Instructions which are present and in Publication 17.
- **Publication 590-A — Contributions to Individual Retirement Arrangements** (`p590a.pdf`) — contains the special coordinated worksheet used when the taxpayer takes a traditional Individual Retirement Arrangement deduction AND is covered by a workplace retirement plan AND has taxable Social Security benefits. The interaction between the IRA deduction and the taxable-SS worksheet is circular (each one's input depends on the other), so Publication 590-A prescribes an iterative two-worksheet method.
- **Form SSA-1099 instructions** (printed on the back) — define Box 3 (gross paid), Box 4 (benefits repaid), Box 5 (net benefits = Box 3 − Box 4).
- **Internal Revenue Code §86** — the statute that authorizes the taxation of Social Security benefits and sets the $25,000 / $32,000 base amounts (1983) and the $34,000 / $44,000 second-tier amounts (1993). These statutory thresholds are **not inflation-indexed**.

### 1.2 The basic rule (verbatim from the 2025 Instructions, Line 6b)

From the 2025 Instructions for Form 1040, Line 6a/6b:

> Use the **Social Security Benefits Worksheet** in these instructions to see if any of your benefits are taxable. If part of your benefits are taxable, how much is taxable depends on the total amount of your benefits and other income.

The "other income" the worksheet picks up is the rest of the taxpayer's gross-income lines from Form 1040 plus tax-exempt interest plus a small set of exclusion add-backs.

### 1.3 The conceptual rule (provisional-income test)

The IRS uses a tiered "how much is taxable" rule:

1. Add up half of the Social Security benefits plus most other income plus tax-exempt interest plus certain exclusion add-backs.
2. Subtract a specific subset of Schedule 1 Part II adjustments.
3. Compare the resulting **provisional income** to a **base amount** that depends on filing status.
   - If provisional income is at or below the base amount → **none** of the benefits are taxable; Line 6b = 0.
   - If provisional income is above the base amount but at or below the **second-tier amount** → up to **50 percent** of benefits become taxable, capped by half of the excess over the base amount.
   - If provisional income is above the second-tier amount → up to **85 percent** of benefits become taxable, capped by half of the gap between the two thresholds plus 85 percent of the excess over the second-tier amount.
4. Line 6b can never exceed **85 percent of Line 6a** (the statutory ceiling in IRC §86(a)(2)).

### 1.4 Which worksheet to use (routing rule per Pub. 915 §2.2)

The IRS does **not** treat the printed 1040 worksheet as universally valid. Pub. 915 §2.2 prescribes a worksheet-selection cascade. In order of priority, choose the *first* applicable path:

1. **If the taxpayer (or, on a joint return, the spouse) contributed to a traditional IRA, AND either spouse is covered by a retirement plan at work → use the Publication 590-A coordinated worksheet** (which iteratively reconciles the IRA deduction and the taxable-SS computation, both of which depend on each other).
2. **If condition (1) does not apply, but the taxpayer is filing any of: Form 8815 (savings-bond interest exclusion), Form 8839 (employer-provided adoption benefits), Form 2555 (foreign earned income / housing exclusion), Form 4563 (bona-fide residents of American Samoa), or has bona-fide-resident-of-Puerto-Rico excluded income → use Worksheet 1 in Publication 915** (which differs from the 1040 worksheet only by including these specific exclusion add-backs on worksheet line 5).
3. **If the taxpayer received a lump-sum back payment of Social Security benefits for one or more earlier years → also complete the applicable Publication 915 lump-sum worksheets (Worksheets 2 / 3 / 4)** in addition to the standard worksheet, and report the **lower** of the two results on Line 6b (the lump-sum election is opt-in; the taxpayer checks Line 6c only if they elect it and it produces a lower amount).
4. **Otherwise → use the standard Social Security Benefits Worksheet printed in the 2025 Form 1040 Instructions.**

The four paths produce different Line 6b values for the same taxpayer in scenarios with savings-bond interest, foreign earned income exclusion, IRA contributions plus workplace coverage, or back payments.

### 1.7 The MFS-and-lived-with-spouse-any-time restrictive branch

This is a deliberate IRC §86(c)(1)(D) anti-loophole rule from 1983: a married couple cannot avoid Social Security taxation by filing separately while still living together.

When the filing status is Married Filing Separately AND the taxpayer lived with the spouse at any time during the tax year, the worksheet **skips lines 9 through 16** and applies a direct 85% computation:

```text
Line 17 (restrictive) = Line 8 × 85% (0.85)
Line 19 (restrictive) = min(Line 17, Line 18) = min(Line 8 × 0.85, Line 1 × 0.85)
```

This puts essentially the entire benefit amount in the taxable bucket — there is no base-amount shield. Tax-impact contrast: an MFS taxpayer with $20,000 in benefits and $30,000 in wages who lived with the spouse pays tax on roughly $17,000 (85% × $20,000); the same taxpayer who lived apart all year falls into the Single-equivalent path and pays tax on roughly $9,600.

This restrictive-branch determination is *independent* of the Line 6d checkbox (which is "MFS and lived apart all year" — its own positive assertion). The two are logical inverses on MFS returns. (See `6d.md` for the Line 6d mechanics and a discussion of the soft mutual-exclusion gap on the two boolean inputs.)

### 1.8 The lump-sum election method (Pub. 915 Worksheets 2 / 3 / 4)

If 2025 Box 5 includes a lump-sum back payment for one or more earlier years (typically when an SSA disability claim is approved retroactively, or when the SSA recovers from an overpayment correction), the taxpayer **may elect** to compute the taxable portion of the lump-sum using *each prior year's* income context instead of the current year's.

The election does NOT change Line 6a (the full Box 5 amount still goes there — see `6a.md` §1.5.3). It only changes Line 6b.

**The election is opt-in.** It is checked by setting the Line 6c checkbox.

**The method works as follows (Pub. 915 Worksheets 2, 3, and 4, summarized):**

1. **Step A — separate the current-year benefit from each prior-year allocation.**  
   `currentYearRegularBenefits = Line 6a − sum(lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYearᵢ)`  
   where the sum is over each prior year for which a portion of the 2025 lump-sum is attributed.

2. **Step B — compute the taxable amount of the current-year regular benefits using the standard worksheet on 2025 income** (i.e., re-run the 19-line worksheet with Line 1 = `currentYearRegularBenefits` instead of Line 6a, but keep all the 2025 income inputs on worksheet lines 3 / 4 / 5 / 7 unchanged).  
   Call this result `taxableCurrentYearBase`.

3. **Step C — for each prior year `i`:**  
   a. Recompute the prior-year provisional income using prior-year other income, prior-year tax-exempt interest, and prior-year Schedule 1 adjustments.  
   b. Pretend the prior-year benefits would have been the *actual* prior-year benefits PLUS the portion now allocated.  
   c. Run the standard worksheet using prior-year filing status and prior-year thresholds. Get the would-have-been-taxable amount.  
   d. Subtract whatever was *actually* reported as taxable for that prior year (from the prior-year return).  
   e. The positive remainder is the additional taxable amount that this lump-sum allocation adds to 2025 Line 6b.  
   Call the sum of these positive remainders `additionalFromLumpSum`.

4. **Step D — assemble.**  
   `taxableLumpSum = taxableCurrentYearBase + additionalFromLumpSum`

5. **Step E — compare and choose.**  
   - If `taxableLumpSum < taxableNormal` → Line 6b = `taxableLumpSum`, Line 6c is checked.  
   - Otherwise → Line 6b = `taxableNormal`, Line 6c is unchecked.

The election can substantially reduce Line 6b when the prior years had little or no other income.

### 1.9 The Form 8815 carveout on worksheet line 3 (Pub. 915 Worksheet 1 note)

There is one subtle worksheet-input carveout that applies when the taxpayer is filing Form 8815 (the savings-bond interest exclusion form): on worksheet line 3, the taxpayer must use the amount from **Schedule B (Form 1040) line 2** instead of the amount from **Form 1040 line 2b**. This is because Form 8815 takes a deduction from Schedule B line 2 that flows through to Form 1040 line 2b; if the worksheet used the post-deduction Form 1040 line 2b, the worksheet would effectively count the savings-bond exclusion twice.

This carveout matters only for taxpayers filing Form 8815. For everyone else, the Form 1040 line 2b amount and the Schedule B line 2 amount are identical.

### 1.10 The 85% statutory ceiling (IRC §86(a)(2))

No matter how high the provisional income climbs, the taxable amount on Line 6b can never exceed **85% of Line 6a**. The worksheet enforces this on Line 18 (= Line 1 × 85%) and Line 19 (= smaller of Line 17 or Line 18). The application should also enforce this as a defense-in-depth cap after the worksheet computation finishes.

### 1.11 Out of scope for Line 6b

The following situations are reported but do not affect Line 6b directly; they are documented in sibling files:

- **Repayments more than gross benefits.** See `6a.md` Gap 1 (Line 6a is clamped to $0; Line 6b is also $0; the remedy flows through Schedule A line 16 or IRC §1341 credit).
- **SSI accidentally lumped into Box 5.** See `6a.md` Gap 3 (the SSI subtraction happens before Line 6a is finalized; Line 6b inherits the cleaned figure).
- **The Line 6d MFS-lived-apart checkbox itself.** See `6d.md` (it is set independently in the orchestrator and only *informs* Line 6b indirectly via the base-amount selection).
- **The Line 6c lump-sum election checkbox itself.** Documented here in §1.8 and §1.10; the checkbox logic also lives in `6c.md`.

### 2.4 The code's implemented equation for Line 6b (plain English of the algorithm)

The code follows the same structure as the IRS worksheet, but with one offset readers must remember: the *variable names* `w1, w2, w5, w7` inside `computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal` do **not** map 1-to-1 to IRS worksheet line numbers because the method was named before the 2025 worksheet layout was reconciled. The mapping is:

| Code variable | IRS worksheet line |
|---|---|
| `w1` | Line 1 (Line 6a — total net benefits) |
| `w2` | Line 2 (50% of Line 1) |
| `worksheetLine3` (parameter) | Line 3 (Form 1040 1z+2b+3b+4b+5b+7+8) |
| `worksheetLine4` (parameter) | Line 4 (Form 1040 line 2a) |
| `worksheetLine5` (parameter) | Line 5 (exclusion add-backs) |
| `w5` (code) | Line 6 ("Combine lines 2, 3, 4, and 5") |
| `worksheetLine6` (parameter) | Line 7 (Schedule 1 subset — see §2.2) |
| `w7` (code) | Line 8 ("Subtract line 7 from line 6") |

Reading the IRS worksheet alongside the code requires this offset.

```text
Step 1 — Assemble worksheet line 3 (other income).
    line1z, line2b, line3b, line4b, line5b, line7a, line8 are passed in from
    upstream computations. Add them with addNonNull (treats null as zero only
    when at least one operand is non-null; if all null → result is null):
        autoLine3 = line1z + line2b + line3b + line4b + line5b + line7a + line8
    If the user provided a manual override `importedReturnWorksheetLine3IncomeTotal`,
    use that instead (first-non-null wins).

Step 2 — Assemble worksheet line 4 (tax-exempt interest).
    If `importedReturnWorksheetLine4TaxExemptInterest` is set, use that.
    Otherwise, use the upstream `interest.line2aTaxExemptInterest()` value.

Step 3 — Assemble worksheet line 5 (exclusion add-backs).
    worksheetLine5 = computeForm2555ExclusionForSsWorksheet(taxpayer-foreign-form)
                   + computeForm2555ExclusionForSsWorksheet(spouse-foreign-form)
                   + adoption.line1f()
    Each piece can be null; addNonNull treats null as zero only when at least
    one operand is non-null. Form 8815 (savings-bond) and Form 4563 (Samoa /
    Puerto Rico) exclusions are NOT included here — see Gap 1 in Part 3.

Step 4 — Assemble worksheet line 6 (the Schedule 1 subset).
    If `importedReturnWorksheetLine6AdjustmentsTotal` is set, use that.
    Otherwise, use the upstream `incomeAdjustments.line10FromSchedule1Pub915Subset()`
    value, which is the IRS-specified subset of Schedule 1 Part II:
    lines 11 through 20, plus 23, plus 25. Line 21 (student loan interest),
    line 22 (reserved), and line 24 (write-ins) are correctly EXCLUDED.

Step 5 — Run the 19-line worksheet via computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal.

    if line6a is null or zero:
        return null (no benefits) or BigDecimal.ZERO (concept-applies, zero result)

    w1 = line6a                                  (IRS worksheet line 1)
    w2 = w1 × 0.50                               (IRS line 2)

        secondBase = 44000     (implicit; = base + line 11)

        secondBase = 34000

        taxable = taxableUpTo50

    return min(w1 × 0.85, taxable)               (IRS line 19 = min(line 17, line 18))

    if hasLumpSumBackPaymentForPriorYears is true:

            taxableNormal, line6a, worksheetLine3..6, taxpayerForm, filingStatus)

        if taxableNormal is null OR line6a is null OR lumpSumDetails is empty:
            return null (caller falls back to taxableNormal; advisory flag may fire)

        # Step A — total allocated to prior years
        totalLumpSumAllocated = sum(row.lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear for each row)

            regularCurrentYear, worksheetLine3, worksheetLine4, worksheetLine5,
            worksheetLine6, filingStatus, mfsLivedWithSpouse=false)

        additionalFromLumpSum = 0

            if allocated <= 0: skip

            priorAdjustments     = row.priorYearAdjustmentsForRecompute

            # (prevBenefits + allocated) had been received that year.
            # NOTE: prior-year worksheetLine5 is hardcoded null (no Form 2555 / 8839
            # data for prior years), and the prior-year filing status is ASSUMED to
            # match the current year's filing status.
            recomputedTaxable = computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal(
                totalPriorYearSS,
                priorOtherIncome,
                priorTaxExemptInt,
                null,                             # prior-year worksheetLine5
                priorAdjustments,
                filingStatus,                     # prior-year filing status assumed same
                mfsLivedWithSpouse=false)

            # The "additional" amount this row contributes to current-year line 6b
            # is the recomputed minus the actually-reported (zero-floored).
            additional = max(0, recomputedTaxable - prevTaxable)
            additionalFromLumpSum += additional

        return taxableCurrentYearBase + additionalFromLumpSum

Step 7 — Choose Line 6b and Line 6c.

    line6b = taxableNormal           # default
    line6c = false                   # default

    if electsLumpSumElection
       AND taxableLumpSum is non-null
       AND taxableNormal is non-null
       AND taxableLumpSum < taxableNormal:
        line6b = taxableLumpSum
        line6c = true

Step 8 — Apply the three-protection final cap chain.

    # Protection (1) — force-zero when concept applies but worksheet returned null
    # (defensive; line 6b is never blank when line 6a is populated)
    if line6a is not null AND line6b is null:
        line6b = BigDecimal.ZERO

    # Protections (2) and (3) composed — clamp to [0, 0.85 × line6a]
    if line6b is not null AND line6a is not null:
        maxTaxable = line6a × 0.85    # IRC §86(a)(2) statutory ceiling
        line6b = min(maxTaxable, max(0, line6b))

    # Concept-not-applicable guardrail (inherited from line 6a logic):
    # if line 6a is null AND nobody had benefits → entire SS computation returns null
    # (output JSON omits Line 6a and Line 6b)
```

### 3.2 Verified-correct items

- **Schedule 1 subset on worksheet line 7.** The IRS Social Security Benefits Worksheet line 7 specifies the *subset* of Schedule 1 Part II that consists of lines 11 through 20, plus 23, plus 25 — explicitly excluding line 21 (student loan interest), line 22 (reserved in 2025), and line 24 (write-in). The code reads this through the dedicated accessor `incomeAdjustments.line10FromSchedule1Pub915Subset()`, which was added during the 2026-05-12 closure of `6b.xlsx` Code Validation #5 after a previous version of the code mistakenly read the full Schedule 1 line 26 total. A regression unit test `socialSecurityWorksheetExcludesStudentLoanInterestFromLine6Subset` locks in the correct behavior (Single, $20,000 SS + $25,000 wages + $2,500 student loan interest → Line 6b = $5,350 with the subset; the full-line-26 path would have given $3,750, under-stating Line 6b by $1,600).
- **MFS-with-spouse restrictive branch.** When the filing status is MFS and the taxpayer answers "yes" to `livedWithSpouseAnyTimeDuringTaxYear`, the code correctly skips IRS worksheet lines 9 through 16 and returns `min(w1 × 0.85, w7 × 0.85)`. This forces essentially the entire benefit amount through the 85% bracket (no base-amount shield), implementing the IRC §86(c)(1)(D) anti-loophole rule. A regression unit test `computesMfsLivedWithSpouseSocialSecurityUsingWorksheetBranch` locks this in.
- **The two STOP gates** (worksheet line 8 ≤ 0 and worksheet line 10 ≤ 0). The code returns `BigDecimal.ZERO` (not `null`) at both early-exit sites, matching the IRS worksheet's "STOP. Enter -0- on Form 1040 line 6b" instruction.
- **The three-protection final cap chain at the orchestrator** (force-zero when concept applies, 85% statutory cap, zero-floor) is documented in §2.4 Step 8.
- **The lump-sum-election routing** (`taxableLumpSum < taxableNormal` strict-less-than) follows the silent-best-of-two convention used by mainstream tax software (TurboTax, H&R Block). It diverges from a strict IRC §86(e) reading where the user's election should always be reflected on Line 6c regardless of which amount is lower; the divergence is documented as a deliberate UX choice (`6c.xlsx` Code Validation #7) and not a defect.
- **Whole-dollar rounding** is applied consistently via `roundMoney`.

#### Gap 2 — Worksheet line 13/14 collapse — CLOSED 2026-06-05 (documentation + lock-in tests)

**Status:** **CLOSED 2026-06-05** with a code-block comment in `computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal` proving the algebraic equivalence + two lock-in unit tests covering both branches. No production-code change was needed — the compact formula was always correct in every reachable state; the gap was purely in the explanation.

**IRS worksheet (§1.5, lines 13 + 14 + 15):**

```text
Line 13. Enter the smaller of line 10 or line 11.
Line 14. Multiply line 13 by 50% (0.50).
Line 15. Enter the smaller of line 2 or line 14.
```

If we expand: `w15 = min(w2, min(w10, w11) × 0.50)`.

**Code (`computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal` ~line 13183 post-comment-block):**

```java
BigDecimal taxableUpTo50 = minNonNull(
    multiply(w1, new BigDecimal("0.50")),
    multiply(overBase, new BigDecimal("0.50"))
);
```

This computes `min(w1 × 0.50, overBase × 0.50) = min(w2, w10 × 0.50)` — NOT the IRS literal `min(w2, min(w10, w11) × 0.50)`. The two formulas differ when `overBase > w11` (equivalently `overSecond > 0`).

**Why the code is nonetheless correct everywhere:** the code routes that case into a different branch.

- **When `overSecond ≤ 0`** (provisional income between baseAmount and secondBase): `taxableUpTo50` is used. In this regime `overBase ≤ w11` by construction, so `min(w10, w11) = w10`, and the two formulas collapse to the same value.
- **When `overSecond > 0`** (provisional income above secondBase): the `plateau` branch fires and uses `min((secondBase − baseAmount) × 0.50, w1 × 0.50) = min(w11 × 0.50, w2)` — which is exactly IRS line 15 with the line-11 cap applied.

**Closure artifacts:**

1. **Code comment** at `TaxReturnComputeService.computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal` ~line 13183-13225 — 50-line block walking through the IRS-literal formula, the compact formula, the algebraic difference, and the control-flow safety proof that the difference never manifests in a reachable state. Cross-references the two lock-in tests.

#### Gap 3 — Lump-sum election prior-year worksheet line 5 hard-coded null — CLOSED 2026-06-05

**IRS rule (§1.8 Step C, Pub. 915 Worksheet 3):** when recomputing a prior year's would-have-been-taxable benefit amount, the prior year's *own* Form 2555 / Form 8839 / Form 4563 / Puerto Rico exclusions must be added back to worksheet line 5, exactly as for the current year's standard worksheet.

**Code behavior:** Inside `computeTaxableSocialSecurityLumpSum` (`TaxReturnComputeService.java` ~line 13272–13280), the recursive call to `computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal` for the prior-year recomputation passes `null` for worksheet line 5:

```java

    priorYearOtherIncome,
    priorYearTaxExemptInterest,
    null,                         // ← prior-year worksheetLine5 hard-coded null

    normalizedFilingStatus,
    false

```

**Impact:** Taxpayers who had Form 2555 / 8839 / 4563 / Puerto Rico exclusions in the prior year(s) covered by the lump-sum back payment will have *that prior year's* would-have-been-taxable amount under-stated, which *over-states* the lump-sum-election Line 6b (the lump-sum total is `additionalFromLumpSum = max(0, recomputed − prevTaxable)` — a smaller recomputed value gives a smaller `additional`, but only when `recomputed > prevTaxable`; in cases where the exclusion add-back would push `recomputed` above `prevTaxable`, the under-statement matters).

**Affected population:** Narrow — taxpayers with both (a) a 2025 lump-sum SS back payment for an earlier year AND (b) Form 2555 / 8839 / 4563 / Puerto Rico exclusions in that earlier year.

A taxpayer who was Single in 2023 but is MFJ in 2025 must use the 2023 Single thresholds, not the 2025 MFJ thresholds, when recomputing the 2023 portion.

**Code behavior:** The recursive call (same site as Gap 3) passes `normalizedFilingStatus` — the *current-year* filing status — for the prior-year recomputation. Any prior-year filing-status change is ignored.

**Impact:** When a taxpayer's filing status changed between the prior year(s) covered by the lump-sum and 2025 — most commonly marriage, divorce, or death of spouse — the prior-year recompute uses the wrong threshold table:

- Was Single ($25k base) in prior year, now MFJ ($32k base): prior-year recompute uses $32k → over-states the gap to the base → under-states `recomputedTaxable` → under-states `additionalFromLumpSum` → under-states Line 6b (user-favorable but IRS-incorrect).
- Was MFJ in prior year, now Single after death of spouse: opposite direction → over-states Line 6b.
- Was Married (filing status MFJ or MFS) in prior year, now MFS+lived-with-spouse-this-year: the code passes `mfsLivedWithSpouse=false` regardless, so the prior-year restrictive branch is never triggered.

**Missing input fields:** The `lumpSumDetails` repeating list needs a per-prior-year `priorYearFilingStatus` field (and ideally a `priorYearMfsLivedWithSpouseAnyTime` flag for the rare MFS-with-spouse prior-year case).

**Affected population:** Taxpayers with a lump-sum back payment for a prior year in which their filing status was different from 2025. Common scenarios: marriage / divorce in the intervening years, spouse death, change from MFS to MFJ.

**IRS rule (§1.4, routing condition 1):** when the taxpayer (or spouse) contributed to a traditional IRA AND either is covered by a retirement plan at work AND has Social Security benefits, the IRA deduction and taxable Social Security amount are *mutually dependent* (IRA deduction depends on MAGI, which depends on taxable SS; taxable SS depends on Schedule 1 adjustments, which include the IRA deduction). Publication 590-A provides a coordinated two-worksheet method that iterates between the two amounts until they stabilize.

**Code behavior:** The application uses the plain 1040 Social Security Benefits Worksheet unconditionally. It does not detect the coordination case and does not perform the Pub. 590-A iteration. When the IRA deduction is non-zero AND taxable Social Security benefits are non-zero, the orchestrator emits a non-blocking advisory flag `SOCIAL_SECURITY_IRA_COORDINATION_MANUAL_REVIEW` at `TaxReturnComputeService.java:558` reminding the user to verify the coordination manually.

**Impact:** Subtle. For most taxpayers the difference between the plain worksheet and the Pub. 590-A iterative method is small (often a few dollars to a few hundred dollars). The plain worksheet is generally taxpayer-favorable because it under-deducts the IRA contribution (worksheet line 7 uses the IRA deduction as-claimed without checking whether the claimed amount actually qualifies under MAGI phaseouts that include taxable SS).

**Population:** Taxpayers who: (a) take a traditional IRA deduction, (b) are covered by a workplace retirement plan (or have a spouse who is, on a joint return), AND (c) receive Social Security benefits. This is moderately common — most retirees who still contribute to IRAs and have workplace coverage from a part-time job.

**Missing input fields:** None — all necessary data is already captured. The gap is purely in the code, not in the schemas. Implementing this gap requires building the iterative coordination loop in `computeIncomeAdjustments` (the IRA deduction site) and `computeSocialSecurityBenefits` (the taxable-SS site).

#### Gap 6 — Form 8815 line-2b → Schedule B line 2 carveout — CLOSED 2026-06-05

**Status:** **CLOSED 2026-06-05** in `computeSocialSecurityBenefits`: when `interest-income-taxpayer.claimsSavingsBondExclusionForm8815` is true, the `savingsBondExclusionAmount` is added back to `line2b` for `worksheetLine3FromIncome` only (Form 1040 line 2b unchanged). Lock-in test: `form8815CarveoutAddsExclusionBackToWorksheetLine3`. (Original gap text preserved below for historical reference.)


**IRS rule (§1.9):** when the taxpayer is filing Form 8815 (the savings-bond interest exclusion), worksheet line 3 must use the amount from Schedule B (Form 1040) line 2 instead of the amount from Form 1040 line 2b. The reason is that Form 8815 reduces the Schedule B line 2 amount down to the Form 1040 line 2b amount; using the post-deduction line 2b on worksheet line 3 would effectively count the savings-bond exclusion as a deduction twice (once on Form 1040 line 2b and once on worksheet line 3 by virtue of the line 2b being lower than the true gross interest).

**Code behavior:** The orchestrator (`TaxReturnComputeService.java` ~line 12617) sums `line1z + line2b + line3b + line4b + line5b + line7a + line8` for `worksheetLine3FromIncome`. It always uses `line2b` directly — no detection of Form 8815 filing, no substitution of Schedule B line 2.

**Impact:** Taxpayers filing Form 8815 will have worksheet line 3 *under-stated* by exactly the Form 8815 exclusion amount, which *under-states* the provisional income, which *under-states* Line 6b. Direction is user-favorable but IRS-incorrect.

**Affected population:** Taxpayers who hold Series EE or I savings bonds, redeemed them in 2025 to pay qualified higher-education expenses, AND received Social Security benefits.

**Missing input fields:** None directly — Form 8815's `excludedInterestAmount` (the exclusion that flows from Schedule B line 3 to Form 1040 line 2b) is already in scope as part of the Line 2b computation. The fix is in the orchestrator: when Form 8815 is filed, add the Form 8815 exclusion back to `line2b` for the worksheet-line-3 computation only (the Form 1040 line 2b stays unchanged).

#### Gap 7 — Worksheet line 6 manual override does not validate against the auto-computed value — CLOSED 2026-06-05

**Status:** **CLOSED 2026-06-05** with a non-blocking advisory `SOCIAL_SECURITY_WORKSHEET_LINE6_OVERRIDE_DISAGREES_WITH_AUTO_COMPUTED`. The override still wins (escape hatch preserved); the advisory surfaces when the override and auto-computed Pub. 915 subset differ by more than $1. Lock-in test: `worksheetLine6OverrideAdvisoryFiresWhenOverrideDisagreesWithAutoComputed`. (Original gap text preserved below for historical reference.)


**IRS rule:** When the user enters `importedReturnWorksheetLine6AdjustmentsTotal`, they are asserting "I am importing this return from an external system and the Schedule 1 subset for my return is this exact amount." There is no IRS validation rule between the override and the auto-computed value because the IRS does not know about the override.

**Code behavior:** The override silently wins (`firstNonNullAmount(override, auto)`). If the user enters an override that disagrees with the auto-computed Schedule 1 subset, the auto-computed value is silently discarded.

**Impact:** A user who enters an override by mistake (e.g., entering the *full* Schedule 1 line 26 instead of the Pub. 915 subset) will silently get a different Line 6b than the auto-computed return. There is no advisory.

**Affected population:** Imported-return users only. The override is an "I am importing this return" escape hatch.

**Missing input fields:** None. The fix is to emit a non-blocking advisory flag when the override and the auto-computed value disagree by more than a threshold (e.g., > $1 absolute difference). This is a usability gap, not a correctness gap.

#### Gap 8 — Lump-sum-allocation flag promoted to §17 — CLOSED 2026-06-05

**Status:** **CLOSED 2026-06-05**. `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_ALLOCATION_REQUIRED` promoted from §18 to §17 (registered in `NonOverrideableFlags.CODES`; message rewritten to §17 spec format). Rationale: silently producing `taxableLumpSum = taxableNormal` after the user explicitly elected the lump-sum method is a user-financial-loss case per `rules.md` §17. Lock-in test: `lumpSumElectionWithoutDetailsFiresSection17AllocationRequiredFlag`. (Original gap text preserved below for historical reference.)


**Code behavior:** When `electsLumpSumElectionMethod=true` AND `lumpSumDetails` is empty, the orchestrator (`TaxReturnComputeService.java` ~line 12676) emits a flag with `blocking=true`:

```java

    "SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_ALLOCATION_REQUIRED",
    "Lump-sum election is selected for line 6c but prior-year allocation details are missing.",
    true

```

But the existing documentation (knowledge file §6 row, `6abcd.md` dependency file row) describes this flag as a non-blocking advisory. The flag is also not in `NonOverrideableFlags` (so it is overrideable per `overrideFlags=true`), meaning it is functionally a §18 blocking-with-override.

**Impact:** A user who elects the lump-sum method on the form and then forgets to add allocation rows is blocked at compute time. Without override, they cannot file. With override, they file with `taxableLumpSum = taxableNormal` (the fallback) — i.e., no benefit from the election, but the return is still IRS-correct.

**Severity:** Documentation drift. The behavior is consistent with the §18 (blocking-with-override) semantic described in `XLS/Computations/6a.md` for the SS family. The drift is that the historical "advisory" label in the knowledge / dependency files is stale.

**Fix direction:** Update the knowledge file and the dependency file to describe this flag as §18 blocking-with-override; or, alternatively, evaluate whether it should be a non-blocking advisory (which would allow the user to file knowingly forgoing the election). User-financial-loss principle suggests blocking-with-override is correct: the user explicitly elected and is now mid-action without the data to complete the election.

#### Gap 9 — Missing prior-year previously-reported-taxable now §17 — CLOSED 2026-06-05

**Status:** **CLOSED 2026-06-05** with new §17 code `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_PRIOR_YEAR_REPORTED_REQUIRED` and validator method `validateLumpSumPriorYearReportedAmounts`. Fires per-row when `electsLumpSumElectionMethod=true` AND `lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear > 0` AND `priorYearTaxableBenefitsPreviouslyReported` is null. Lock-in test: `lumpSumElectionWithMissingPreviouslyReportedFiresSection17Flag`. (Original gap text preserved below for historical reference.)


**IRS rule (§1.8 Step D):** the lump-sum-election method computes `additional = max(0, recomputedTaxable − previouslyReportedTaxable)`. The "previously reported" value comes from the actual taxable benefits the taxpayer reported on the prior year's Form 1040 line 6b (or pre-2018, Form 1040 line 20b).

**Code behavior:** `priorYearTaxableBenefitsPreviouslyReported` is read from the row's input. If the user leaves it blank, it is treated as `null` → `max(0, recomputedTaxable − null) = recomputedTaxable` (per `subtractNonNegative`). This *over-states* `additional`, which *over-states* the lump-sum result, which **defeats the purpose of the election** (the election is supposed to reduce Line 6b; over-stating it means `taxableLumpSum >= taxableNormal` and the election does not fire).

**Impact:** A user who elects the lump-sum method but does not fill in the prior-year previously-reported amount silently loses the election's benefit. The election would *appear* to do nothing because the lump-sum result equals or exceeds the normal result.

**Missing input field:** None — the field exists. The gap is in the lack of validation: when `electsLumpSumElectionMethod=true` AND any row has a non-null `lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear` BUT a null `priorYearTaxableBenefitsPreviouslyReported`, emit a blocking-with-override flag.

**Affected population:** Lump-sum-electing users who forget the prior-year-previously-reported field. This is a UX failure where the election silently does nothing.

### 3.6 Summary verdict

For Line 6b specifically:

- **The normal-tier worksheet computation matches the IRS rule exactly.** The 19-line Social Security Benefits Worksheet is implemented honestly via `computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal`, including the two STOP gates at worksheet lines 8 and 10, the base-amount / second-tier-amount tiering, the 50% and 85% multipliers, and the final 85% statutory ceiling. The Schedule 1 line-7 subset is correctly limited to lines 11–20 + 23 + 25 (excluding line 21 student loan interest, line 22 reserved, line 24 write-ins). **Gap 2 closed 2026-06-05**: the compact `taxableUpTo50` formula and the plateau-branch formula together produce the same answer as the IRS-literal worksheet lines 13–15 in every reachable state — proven by a 50-line code-comment block + two lock-in unit tests covering the within-plateau case and the above-second-tier plateau case.
- **The MFS-with-spouse restrictive branch matches the IRC §86(c)(1)(D) anti-loophole rule exactly.** When the filing status is MFS and the taxpayer lived with the spouse at any time, the code correctly skips worksheet lines 9 through 16 and applies `min(w1 × 0.85, w7 × 0.85)`.
- **The lump-sum election method matches Pub. 915 Worksheet 3 in structure** but has three deliberate fidelity gaps (Gaps 3, 4, 9): prior-year worksheet line 5 is hard-coded null, prior-year filing status assumes same as current year, and the previously-reported-taxable field can silently default to null. The deferred-by-design assumption is that the affected population (lump-sum-back-payment recipients with changing filing status or prior-year exclusions or incomplete prior-year data) is narrow.
- **Gap 1 closed 2026-06-05** via the Option A unified possession-residence-exclusion build (covers all five US possessions per Pub. 570 — Am Samoa, Puerto Rico, Guam, USVI, CNMI). Worksheet line 5 now includes the user-entered possession exclusion. Backend compute + schema + mappers + 3 unit tests done; front-end intake UI + Form 4563 PDF-embed upgrade tracked as separate Option A phases.
- **One special-mechanic case is not implemented: Form 8815 line-2b carveout** (Gap 6). Affects savings-bond redeemers using the exclusion AND receiving Social Security — narrow but real, with material under-statement of Line 6b for that population.
- **One coordinated-worksheet case falls back to an advisory: Pub. 590-A IRA-deduction coordination** (Gap 5). Most affected users would see only small differences from the iterative method, but the rule is real and the advisory does not actually compute anything.
- **Three documentation / validation hygiene items** remain (Gaps 7, 8, 9).
- **All hard-coded reference-data constants** ($25k/$32k/$34k/$44k base + second-tier, 0.50/0.85 multipliers, $3,000 IRC §1341 threshold from Line 6a) are statutory and have not moved since 1983/1993. Migration to centralized `ReferenceData` is low-priority but would help future audits.
- **Unit-test coverage** for Line 6b includes the MFS-with-spouse branch, the lump-sum election, the below-base-amount short-circuit, and the Schedule 1 subset isolation. End-to-end coverage in `e2e/tests/line6abcd-social-security-benefits.spec.ts` exercises the full UI-to-compute round trip (11 tests after the 2026-06-05 sweep).
