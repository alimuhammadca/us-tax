# Form 1040 ‚Äî Line 6c ‚Äî Lump-sum election method checkbox

**Tax year: 2025**

This document explains how the **Line 6c** checkbox on Form 1040 is set. Line 6c is a single Yes / No box (the application stores it as a boolean true / false). It is **checked** when the taxpayer files using the *lump-sum election method* described in Publication 915 (Worksheets 2, 3, and 4) to determine the taxable portion of a lump-sum back payment of Social Security or Tier 1 Railroad Retirement benefits received in 2025. It is **unchecked** in every other situation.

Line 6c is a label-only checkbox on the printed Form 1040 ‚Äî it carries no dollar amount of its own. Its purpose is to inform the Internal Revenue Service that the figure shown on Line 6b was computed using the *prior-year recompute* method rather than the *current-year* method. The dollar consequence of the election lives entirely on **Line 6b**, which is documented separately in `6b.md`.

The document is organized in three parts:

1. **The IRS rule.** What the controlling Internal Revenue Service publications say must happen. This is the source of truth ‚Äî the application must match it.
2. **The code implementation.** What the application's backend Java code currently does.
3. **The comparison.** A side-by-side check showing whether the code follows the IRS rule honestly, and where the two diverge.

Note on terminology used throughout (defined once, used repeatedly):

- **Internal Revenue Service** is abbreviated as IRS. The IRS is the United States federal agency that collects taxes.
- **Internal Revenue Code** is abbreviated as IRC. The IRC is the body of federal tax law passed by Congress; section numbers like "IRC ¬ß86(e)" cite specific subsections.
- **Social Security Administration** is abbreviated as SSA. The SSA pays Social Security retirement, survivor, and disability benefits.
- **Railroad Retirement Board** is abbreviated as RRB. The RRB administers retirement benefits for railroad workers.
- **Social Security Equivalent Benefit** is abbreviated as SSEB. SSEB is the portion of railroad retirement Tier 1 benefits taxed under the same rules as Social Security and reported on the blue Form RRB-1099.
- **Form SSA-1099** is the year-end statement the SSA sends to anyone who received Social Security benefits. Box 5 reports net benefits; Box 3 reports the portion attributable to one or more earlier years (the lump-sum back payment) when applicable.
- **Form RRB-1099** (blue) reports the SSEB Tier 1 portion of railroad retirement benefits and can also contain lump-sum back-payment information for prior years.
- **Lump-sum back payment** is the IRS term for a 2025 Social Security or SSEB payment that compensates the recipient for benefits owed in *prior* years. The most common cause is a disability claim that is approved retroactively, sometimes years after the application date. A second common cause is a recomputation that increases a prior-year monthly amount and is paid as a single 2025 catch-up amount.
- **Lump-sum death benefit** is a separate one-time SSA payment to certain survivors after a worker's death. It is **not** taxable Social Security income and is **not** what Line 6c is about; the lump-sum election method does not apply to it.
- **Election** means the taxpayer's affirmative choice to use a particular tax computation method. Per IRC ¬ß86(e), the lump-sum election is *opt-in* ‚Äî silence means the standard current-year worksheet applies.
- **Pub. 915 Worksheet 3** is the worksheet in IRS Publication 915 that performs the lump-sum recompute (one column per prior year). Worksheets 2 and 4 are companion worksheets used in narrow situations (see ¬ß1.9).
- **Adjusted Gross Income** is abbreviated as AGI. AGI is Form 1040 Line 11.
- **Modified Adjusted Gross Income** is abbreviated as MAGI. The Social Security worksheet's "modified" figure is a working term used by Publication 915.
- **Married Filing Jointly** is abbreviated as MFJ.
- **Married Filing Separately** is abbreviated as MFS.
- **Head of Household** is abbreviated as HOH.
- **Qualifying Surviving Spouse** is abbreviated as QSS.

## Part 1 ‚Äî The IRS rule (the source of truth)

### 1.1 Authority

The IRS publications used to write this document are in `C:\us-tax\docs\books\`:

- **2025 Instructions for Form 1040 / 1040-SR** (`i1040gi_2025.pdf` / `i1040gi_2025.txt`) ‚Äî the printed line-6 instructions describe Line 6c as: *"If you elect to use the lump-sum election method, check here ... See instructions."* The "instructions" referenced are the more detailed treatment in Publication 915.
- **Publication 17 ‚Äî Your Federal Income Tax** (`p17.pdf`) ‚Äî Chapter 7 ("Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits") contains a short narrative description of the lump-sum election method and points readers to Publication 915 for the worksheets.
- **Publication 915 ‚Äî Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits** ‚Äî the deep reference. It contains the standard Worksheet 1 and the lump-sum-election Worksheets 2, 3, and 4. The printed Pub. 915 (2025 edition) is not in the `books` folder of this repository; the rules it contains are summarized in the 2025 Form 1040 Instructions and in Publication 17 Chapter 7.
- **Internal Revenue Code ¬ß86(e)** ‚Äî the statutory authority for the lump-sum election. The statute says, in substance, that the taxpayer *may elect* to limit the taxable portion of a back payment to the amount that would have been taxable if the payment had been received in the earlier year for which it was paid.
- **Form SSA-1099 and Form RRB-1099 instructions** (printed on the back) ‚Äî define the Box 3 *Description of Amount in Box 3* section, where the SSA / RRB lists the prior-year allocation of any lump-sum back payment.

### 1.2 What Line 6c is (and is not)

**What it is:**

- A label-only checkbox on Form 1040 Line 6c, immediately to the right of the Line 6b dollar amount.
- A *flag* that tells the IRS that Line 6b was computed using the Publication 915 lump-sum election method rather than the standard current-year Social Security Benefits Worksheet.
- Driven by the taxpayer's *election*. The taxpayer makes the election by completing Pub. 915 Worksheets 2, 3, and 4 (as applicable) and reporting the lower of the two methods on Line 6b.

**What it is not:**

- It is not a dollar amount.
- It is not affected by Line 6a (the gross benefits figure), Line 6d (the MFS-lived-apart checkbox), the filing status, or the standard Social Security Benefits Worksheet result by itself.
- It is **not** about the SSA / RRB lump-sum *death* benefit, which is a separate non-taxable payment. The lump-sum *back-payment election* is exclusively about prior-year benefit allocations.

### 1.3 The IRS rule for checking Line 6c (verbatim spec, paraphrased)

Per IRC ¬ß86(e) plus the 2025 Form 1040 line 6c instruction plus Publication 915 Worksheet 3 plus `C:\us-tax\lines\6abcd.md` ¬ß7.4:

```text
Definitions
-----------
electsLumpSumElection := the taxpayer affirmatively elects the lump-sum
                         method (an opt-in user choice)
taxableNormal         := the Line 6b dollar amount produced by the standard
                         current-year Social Security Benefits Worksheet
                         (Pub. 915 Worksheet 1 or the printed 1040 worksheet,
                         depending on the routing in 6b.md ¬ß1.4)
taxableLumpSum        := the Line 6b dollar amount produced by the lump-sum
                         election method (Pub. 915 Worksheet 3)

The IRS rule (strict reading)
-----------------------------
if electsLumpSumElection is true:
    line6c_checked = true
    line6b = the smaller of (taxableLumpSum, taxableNormal)
else:
    line6c_checked = false
    line6b = taxableNormal
```

This is the strict IRC ¬ß86(e) reading. It says: the election is the taxpayer's choice, and the checkbox reflects the choice. Line 6b separately reflects the *better* of the two outcomes because the election operates as a cap ‚Äî Publication 915 instructs the taxpayer to use the smaller value, never the larger.

### 1.4 The mainstream-software convention (silent-best-of-two)

In practice every major United States consumer tax-preparation product (TurboTax, H&R Block, TaxAct, FreeTaxUSA, etc.) implements a *slightly different* rule that is friendlier to the unsophisticated user:

```text
Mainstream-software convention
------------------------------
if electsLumpSumElection is true AND taxableLumpSum < taxableNormal:
    line6c_checked = true
    line6b = taxableLumpSum
else:
    line6c_checked = false
    line6b = taxableNormal
```

The difference is one situation only: when the user elects the method but the recompute produces a *worse* (higher) taxable amount ‚Äî for example because the prior years had high income and the election would *raise* Line 6b. The strict IRC reading says the checkbox should be checked anyway because the user elected. The mainstream-software convention silently *drops* the election in that case, leaving the checkbox unchecked and using `taxableNormal` for Line 6b.

The rationale for the convention is that the lump-sum election was designed by Congress as a *taxpayer-favorable* relief mechanism. A taxpayer who explicitly elects it almost certainly intends to benefit, not to volunteer a higher tax bill. Silent best-of-two protects the taxpayer from a confusing failure mode where checking a "tax-saving" box raises their tax.

**Both interpretations are defensible.** The IRS has never publicly objected to the convention. A strict IRC ¬ß86(e) reading is more disclosure-precise but less user-friendly. The application currently implements the mainstream-software convention; see ¬ß3.1 for the gap analysis.

### 1.5 The Publication 915 Worksheet 3 recompute (summary)

Line 6c only fires when the lump-sum recompute has actually been performed. The recompute itself produces `taxableLumpSum`, the input that the Line 6c comparison consumes. The mechanics live in `6b.md` ¬ß1.8 ‚Äî repeated here in summary form so this document can stand alone:

```text
Step A ‚Äî separate current-year regular benefits from each prior-year allocation
    currentYearRegular = Line 6a ‚àí Œ£ (lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear_i)
                                    over each prior year i in lumpSumDetails

Step B ‚Äî compute the standard worksheet on the current-year regular benefits
         using the 2025 income context
    taxableCurrentYearBase = standardWorksheet(
        line1 = currentYearRegular,
        and all other worksheet inputs from 2025 income unchanged)

Step C ‚Äî for each prior year row i, compute the additional taxable amount
         attributable to the lump-sum allocation
    for each row i in lumpSumDetails:
        # Re-run the standard worksheet using prior-year inputs, but treat
        # prior-year benefits as if they had been (actual + allocated).
        totalPriorYearSS_i = prevBenefits_i + allocated_i
        recomputed_i = standardWorksheet(
            line1 = totalPriorYearSS_i,
            other-income inputs = prior-year inputs from row i,
            filing status = prior-year filing status,
            mfsLivedWithSpouse = prior-year MFS-lived-with-spouse flag)
        additional_i = max(0, recomputed_i ‚àí prevTaxable_i)
        additionalFromLumpSum += additional_i

Step D ‚Äî assemble
    taxableLumpSum = taxableCurrentYearBase + additionalFromLumpSum
```

`taxableLumpSum` then enters the Line 6c / Line 6b decision in ¬ß1.3 / ¬ß1.4.

### 1.6 Reference-data constants used by Line 6c

Line 6c has **no reference-data constants of its own**. The checkbox is a pure boolean function of `(electsLumpSumElection, taxableLumpSum, taxableNormal)`. Every constant used to compute `taxableLumpSum` and `taxableNormal` belongs to Line 6b (see `6b.md` ¬ß1.6 for the IRC ¬ß86 thresholds, the 50% and 85% taxability fractions, and the $25,000 / $32,000 base and $34,000 / $44,000 second-tier amounts).

### 1.7 Inputs required (per IRS Publication 915 Worksheet 3)

To complete Worksheet 3 honestly, the taxpayer must provide, for each prior year covered by the back payment:

1. **The dollar amount of the 2025 lump-sum attributable to that earlier year.** This figure comes from the *Description of Amount in Box 3* section of Form SSA-1099 or RRB-1099. The Social Security Administration is required to itemize the prior-year allocation; the taxpayer is not free to invent or re-allocate it.
2. **The earlier year's filing status.** Critical because a taxpayer who was Single in 2022 and is now MFJ in 2025 (or vice versa) faces a *different* base-amount threshold for the recompute.
3. **The earlier year's other income, tax-exempt interest, and Schedule 1 adjustments** as actually reported on that prior-year return. These determine the prior-year provisional income.
4. **The earlier year's exclusion add-backs** (Form 8839 line 28 adoption benefits, Form 2555 lines 45 + 50 foreign-earned income, Form 4563 line 15 American Samoa, Puerto Rico bona-fide-resident exclusion) as actually reported on the prior-year return. These determine the prior-year worksheet Line 5.
5. **The earlier year's MFS-lived-with-spouse flag** if the filing status was MFS, because the prior year may have fallen into the restrictive branch.
6. **The earlier year's actual taxable Social Security benefits** (the amount reported on the prior year's Form 1040 Line 6b or equivalent). This is the subtrahend used to compute `additional_i`.

Without all six per-prior-year inputs the election cannot be computed *correctly*. The application's gap status for each of the six is in ¬ß3.2.

### 1.8 Out of scope for Line 6c

The following situations are reported but do not affect Line 6c directly:

- **Repayments more than gross benefits** (see `6a.md` Gap 1) ‚Äî the special repayment rule produces a deduction or credit on a separate form; it does not flow through Line 6c.
- **SSI accidentally included in Box 5** (see `6a.md` Gap 3) ‚Äî handled before Line 6a is finalized; does not interact with the lump-sum election.
- **The Line 6d MFS-lived-apart checkbox** ‚Äî set independently in the orchestrator; only informs Line 6b indirectly via the base-amount selection (see `6d.md`).
- **The Pub. 590-A IRA-coordination iterative method** ‚Äî produces `taxableNormal` (the *input* to the Line 6c comparison) under a different worksheet path; the Line 6c logic itself is unchanged. See `6b.md` ¬ß1.4 and Gap 5.

### 1.9 The other Publication 915 worksheets (Worksheets 2 and 4)

Publication 915 contains four worksheets:

| Worksheet | Purpose | Affects Line 6c? |
|---|---|---|
| Worksheet 1 | Standard current-year computation with exclusion add-backs (Form 2555, 8815, 8839, 4563, Puerto Rico). Produces `taxableNormal` when any exclusion applies. | Indirectly ‚Äî it produces the `taxableNormal` input to the Line 6c comparison. |
| Worksheet 2 | Treats the lump-sum back payment for a prior year that is itself a *current-year* recomputation context. Used when Worksheet 1 was used for the standard computation. | Indirectly ‚Äî produces `taxableLumpSum` in the carveout case. |
| Worksheet 3 | The standard lump-sum recompute. One column per prior year. The default worksheet used in almost all real-world lump-sum scenarios. | Yes ‚Äî produces `taxableLumpSum`. |
| Worksheet 4 | Special: used when the taxpayer's *prior-year* Social Security benefits were *repaid in 2025* (a rare reversal of the usual mechanic). | Indirectly ‚Äî replaces Worksheet 3 in this narrow case. |

For Line 6c, the *checkbox decision* is identical regardless of which of Worksheets 2 / 3 / 4 produced `taxableLumpSum`. The application currently implements Worksheet 3 only; Worksheets 2 and 4 are open gaps (see ¬ß3.4).

### 2.4 The code's implemented equation for Line 6c (plain English of the algorithm)

```text
Step 1 ‚Äî Gather the two raw inputs.
    electsLumpSumElection = getBoolean(socialSecurityTaxpayer,
                                       "electsLumpSumElectionMethod") == true
    hasLumpSumBackPayment = getBoolean(socialSecurityTaxpayer,
                                       "hasLumpSumBackPaymentForPriorYears") == true

Step 2 ‚Äî Compute taxableNormal unconditionally (always; even if no election).
    taxableNormal = computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal(line6a, ...)
        ‚Äî runs the standard 19-line worksheet (see 6b.md ¬ß2.4 for full detail)

Step 3 ‚Äî Fire ¬ß17 blocker if election is on but prior-year reported amounts are missing.
    if electsLumpSumElection:
        for each row in lumpSumDetails:
            if row.lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear > 0 AND
               row.priorYearTaxableBenefitsPreviouslyReported is null:
                flags.add(SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_PRIOR_YEAR_TAXABLE_REQUIRED,
                          blocking=true)   # ¬ß17 non-overrideable per 6b Gap 9

Step 4 ‚Äî Conditionally compute taxableLumpSum.
    taxableLumpSum = taxableNormal     # default initial value
    if hasLumpSumBackPayment:
        taxableLumpSum = computeTaxableSocialSecurityLumpSum(
            taxableNormal, line6a, worksheetLine3, worksheetLine4,
            worksheetLine5, worksheetLine6, socialSecurityTaxpayer,
            filingStatusValue)

        # Returns null when lumpSumDetails is empty or unparseable
        if electsLumpSumElection AND taxableLumpSum is null:
            flags.add(SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_ALLOCATION_REQUIRED,
                      blocking=true)   # ¬ß17 non-overrideable per 6b Gap 8

Step 5 ‚Äî Apply the three-condition AND gate (coupled assignment).
    line6c = false                     # default
    line6b = taxableNormal             # default

    if electsLumpSumElection
       AND taxableLumpSum is not null
       AND taxableNormal is not null
       AND taxableLumpSum.compareTo(taxableNormal) < 0:    # STRICTLY less than
        line6b = taxableLumpSum
        line6c = true

    # All three legs must be true. Equal values (taxableLumpSum == taxableNormal)
    # do NOT trigger ‚Äî strictly-less-than semantics per 6c.xlsx Code Validation #6.

Step 6 ‚Äî Persist Line 6c into the SocialSecurityComputation record.
    socialSecurityComputation.line6cLumpSumElection(line6c)
        # The orchestrator never writes null here ‚Äî it always writes true or
        # false. The output JSON field is therefore boolean-typed when present
        # and absent only when Line 6a itself is null (concept-not-applicable).
```

### 2.5 Reference-data constants used by Line 6c

Line 6c has **no reference-data constants of its own** in the code ‚Äî the decision is a pure boolean comparison. The constants used to produce its two inputs (`taxableLumpSum` and `taxableNormal`) live inside `computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal` and are documented in `6b.md` ¬ß2.5.

### 2.6 The ¬ß17 blocker flags that defend the Line 6c decision

Two compute-time ¬ß17 blockers fire when the lump-sum election is on but the data needed to perform a *correct* recompute is missing. They are listed here because they directly defend the Line 6c decision's integrity ‚Äî without them the Line 6c checkbox would silently be `false` (because `taxableLumpSum` would be `null` or numerically equal to `taxableNormal`) even though the user explicitly elected the method.

| Flag code | Fires when | What the user must do |
|---|---|---|
| `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_ALLOCATION_REQUIRED` | `electsLumpSumElectionMethod` is true AND `computeTaxableSocialSecurityLumpSum` returns null (typically because `lumpSumDetails` is empty or every row has zero `lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear`) | Either (a) fill in `lumpSumDetails[]` with at least one row that has a positive `lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear` plus the prior-year fields, or (b) un-check `electsLumpSumElectionMethod`. |
| `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_PRIOR_YEAR_TAXABLE_REQUIRED` | `electsLumpSumElectionMethod` is true AND any `lumpSumDetails` row has a positive `lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear` AND that same row's `priorYearTaxableBenefitsPreviouslyReported` is null | Either (a) enter the prior-year actually-reported taxable amount on that row, or (b) un-check the election. |

Both are non-overrideable per the project's ¬ß17 enforcement policy (compute returns HTTP 409 with the flag array; the user cannot click "compute anyway"). The rationale is that allowing the override would silently lose the election's tax savings ‚Äî a real user-side financial loss masquerading as a successful computation.

### 3.2 Verified-correct items

- **Opt-in semantics.** The Line 6c checkbox is `false` by default. It only becomes `true` when `electsLumpSumElectionMethod` is explicitly set to `true` on the Social Security personal form (taxpayer side). The application never auto-elects on the user's behalf, matching the IRC ¬ß86(e) "may elect" language. Locked in by the unit test `computesSocialSecurityLinesWithLumpSumElectionAndLine6d` (`TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.java`).
- **Atomic coupling of Line 6b and Line 6c.** The single `if (electsLumpSumElection && taxableLumpSum != null && taxableNormal != null && taxableLumpSum.compareTo(taxableNormal) < 0)` branch updates both fields together. There is no code path in which Line 6c can be `true` while Line 6b is set to `taxableNormal`, nor any path in which Line 6c is `false` while Line 6b is set to `taxableLumpSum`. The output is internally consistent by construction.
- **Strictly-less-than semantics on the comparison.** `taxableLumpSum.compareTo(taxableNormal) < 0` (not `<= 0`). Equal values do not trigger the election ‚Äî consistent with the spec interpretation that the election is meaningful only when it *reduces* taxable income, never when it produces the same result. Locked in by the test at line ~3367 of `TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.java`.
- **Null guards on both operands.** The AND gate evaluates `taxableLumpSum != null && taxableNormal != null` before invoking `compareTo`, preventing a `NullPointerException`. In practice both should be non-null whenever Line 6a is populated and the election is on; the null-guards are defense-in-depth that handles the (impossible-in-normal-flow) case where `computeTaxableSocialSecurityLumpSum` returns null silently. The companion `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_ALLOCATION_REQUIRED` ¬ß17 blocker catches the same condition at the user level so the silent fall-through is never reached in production.
- **¬ß17 blockers for missing data.** Both `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_ALLOCATION_REQUIRED` and `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_PRIOR_YEAR_TAXABLE_REQUIRED` are non-overrideable, so a user who elects without supplying the required data cannot reach a Line 6c value at all ‚Äî they receive a compute-time error and must either complete the data or rescind the election. This is the right disposition because allowing the override would silently lose the election's tax savings.
- **Persistence layer.** The `pf_social_security_benefits` table stores `electsLumpSumElectionMethod` and `hasLumpSumBackPaymentForPriorYears` as boolean columns; the `pf_social_security_lump_sum_detail` child table stores the repeating `lumpSumDetails` rows with FK cascade to the parent. V48 (2026-06-05, 6b.md Gap 3 + Gap 4 closure) added the three prior-year override columns. `UserDataBulkDelete.PARENT_TABLES_UID_CASCADE` includes `pf_social_security_benefits`, so the test-reset endpoint correctly wipes lump-sum data between e2e runs.

### 3.3 Gaps (where the code diverges from a strict IRS reading)

#### Gap 1 ‚Äî Silent-best-of-two convention diverges from strict IRC ¬ß86(e) ‚Äî **CLOSED 2026-06-06**

**Severity:** Low ‚Äî deliberate UX trade-off; not a numerical defect, but did silently leave the user uninformed.

**The strict IRC ¬ß86(e) rule:** Line 6c is checked whenever the user elects the lump-sum method, regardless of whether the election produces a lower amount.

**What the code does:** Line 6c is checked only when *both* the user elects *and* `taxableLumpSum < taxableNormal`. When the user elects but the recompute produces a *higher* taxable amount, the code silently drops the election (mainstream-software convention).

**Closure (2026-06-06):** Added a non-blocking advisory flag `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_ELECTION_NOT_BENEFICIAL` (`TaxReturnComputeService.java` immediately after the Line 6c AND-gate at line ~12997). The application keeps the mainstream-software convention (Line 6c stays off when not beneficial ‚Äî protects 99% of users from the "I checked the tax-saving box and my tax went up" surprise) but now tells the user what happened with the exact dollar amounts (`taxableLumpSum` vs `taxableNormal`) and points them to a tax professional if their situation requires Line 6c to be checked for audit-trail consistency with a prior amendment.

The advisory wording explicitly invokes both the mainstream convention and the strict IRC ¬ß86(e) reading so the user can make an informed judgment.

**Why non-blocking (not ¬ß17):** The mainstream-software outcome is still IRS-acceptable ‚Äî Line 6b matches what the IRS expects under either interpretation (because the election is a *cap*, never a *floor*). The Line 6c checkbox is the only thing that differs between the two readings, and that checkbox carries no dollar consequence on its own. So the advisory is informational, not corrective.

**Lock-in tests:** `lumpSumElectionNotBeneficialFiresGap1Advisory` (positive) + `lumpSumElectionBeneficialDoesNotFireGap1Advisory` (negative).

#### Gap 2 ‚Äî Prior-year override defaults silently fall back to current-year assumptions ‚Äî **CLOSED 2026-06-06 (¬ß17 non-overrideable)**

**Severity:** High ‚Äî three concrete IRS-detectable under-statement scenarios identified during audit-risk review; promoted from non-blocking advisory to ¬ß17 non-overrideable on 2026-06-06 after that review.

**Background:** V48 (2026-06-05, 6b.md Gap 3 + Gap 4 closure) added three columns to `pf_social_security_lump_sum_detail`:

- `prior_year_worksheet_line5_exclusions_for_recompute` (DECIMAL 19,4 nullable)
- `prior_year_filing_status` (NVARCHAR 32 nullable, CHECK-constrained to the five filing statuses)
- `prior_year_mfs_lived_with_spouse_any_time` (BIT nullable)

The columns are honored by `computeTaxableSocialSecurityLumpSum` when the user provides them. When the user does *not* provide them, the code falls back to:

- `priorYearWorksheetLine5ExclusionsForRecompute = null` ‚Üí treated as zero in the recompute ‚Üí omits any prior-year adoption / foreign-earned / possession exclusions
- `priorYearFilingStatus = null` ‚Üí falls back to the *current* year's filing status ‚Üí wrong when filing status changed between prior year and 2025 (marriage, divorce, widowhood, dependent gain or loss for HOH)
- `priorYearMfsLivedWithSpouseAnyTime = null` ‚Üí falls back to `false` ‚Üí understates `taxableLumpSum` when prior year was MFS and lived together

**Audit-risk analysis (2026-06-06):** A user who ignores a non-blocking advisory and files faces three IRS-detectable under-statement scenarios. The IRS has the user's prior-year Form 1040 + Form 2555 + Form 4563 + spouse data on file ‚Äî an automated cross-reference is trivial:

| # | Default | Real prior-year condition | Direction | Magnitude (typical) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | `priorYearMfsLivedWithSpouseAnyTime = false` | Prior year was MFS-with-spouse (IRC ¬ß86(c)(1)(D) restrictive branch applies) | Under-statement | Up to 0.85 √ó prior-year benefits (e.g., $7,400 on a $20k allocation) |
| 2 | `priorYearWorksheetLine5ExclusionsForRecompute = 0` | Prior year had Form 2555 / 8839 / 4563 / Puerto Rico / mirror-code exclusion | Under-statement | Order of magnitude of the exclusion √ó bracket rate (e.g., $9,600 on a $30k Form 2555 exclusion) |
| 3 | `priorYearFilingStatus = current` | Prior-year status was less generous (e.g., Single in prior year, MFJ now) | Under-statement | A few thousand $ typical (depends on bracket transition direction) |

Scenarios 1 and 2 always under-state when the actual condition obtains. Scenario 3 under-states when the transition direction increases the base amount.

**Closure (2026-06-06):** Added `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_PRIOR_YEAR_DEFAULTS_APPLIED` as a ¬ß17 non-overrideable blocker, produced by the new helper `validateLumpSumPriorYearOverridesPresent` (`TaxReturnComputeService.java`). The blocker:

- Fires only when `electsLumpSumElectionMethod` is true AND `hasLumpSumBackPaymentForPriorYears` is true (when the user isn't electing, the recompute result is unused and the wrong-default scenario is harmless).
- Iterates each `lumpSumDetails` row with a positive `lumpSumAllocatedToPriorYear`.
- For each affected row, lists which of the three V48 fields are missing and what defaults the recompute would apply (e.g., "tax year 2023 (defaulting: filing status [‚Üí current-year value], MFS-lived-with-spouse flag [‚Üí false])").
- Enumerates the three under-statement scenarios in the user-facing message with the IRS-cross-reference rationale.
- Instructs the user to populate all three fields on every affected row explicitly ‚Äî even when the defaults happen to match (entering filing status = actual prior status, MFS-with-spouse = false, worksheet line 5 = 0). This converts "silent unverified default" into "explicit user attestation" so the audit trail is unambiguous.

**Why ¬ß17 (not non-blocking):** This blocker's disposition matches the two existing lump-sum ¬ß17 blockers (`SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_ALLOCATION_REQUIRED`, `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_PRIOR_YEAR_REPORTED_REQUIRED`) ‚Äî same failure mode (silent-wrong-output of Line 6b that the IRS can detect), same disposition. The non-blocking advisory was the original draft, but the audit-risk analysis identified IRS-detectable under-statements in the failure modes above, which fit the project's ¬ß17 policy for "silent loss to user or silent audit risk."

The trade-off is a UX cliff: every user with a lump-sum back payment plus the election on must explicitly populate all three V48 fields on every row, even when the defaults are correct for their case. The mitigation is they are already in the detailed-prior-year-data workflow; three more answers per row is incremental.

**Lock-in tests:** `lumpSumElectionWithMissingPriorYearOverridesFiresGap2Section17Blocker` (positive) + `lumpSumElectionWithAllPriorYearOverridesPresentDoesNotFireGap2Section17Blocker` (negative).

#### Gap 3 ‚Äî Prior-year row assumes prior-year MFS-restrictive branch did not apply ‚Äî **CLOSED 2026-06-06 (subsumed by Gap 2 ¬ß17)**

**Severity:** High ‚Äî fully covered by Gap 2's ¬ß17 `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LUMP_SUM_PRIOR_YEAR_DEFAULTS_APPLIED` blocker (Scenario 1 in the Gap 2 audit-risk table).

The MFS-restrictive-branch fallback (`priorYearMfsLivedWithSpouseAnyTime = false` when the override is null) is the highest-magnitude of the three null-field conditions that trigger the Gap 2 ¬ß17 blocker. The blocker message explicitly names the MFS-lived-with-spouse flag and its default value, so the user cannot file without affirmatively addressing it. No separate code path needed.

**Status:** Closed as covered by Gap 2 ¬ß17 promotion.

#### Gap 4 ‚Äî Publication 915 worksheet-naming clarification ‚Äî **CLOSED 2026-06-06 (documentation correction)**

**Severity:** None ‚Äî the original gap framing was based on a misclassification of the Publication 915 worksheet naming convention.

**What the original gap claimed:** That "Worksheet 2" (companion to Pub. 915 Worksheet 1 for exclusion-add-back cases) and "Worksheet 4" (prior-year benefit repayment case) were unimplemented Line 6c paths.

**What is actually true:**

- **Worksheet 2** (or whatever variant naming is in the current Pub. 915 edition for the per-prior-year recompute): the application's `computeTaxableSocialSecurityLumpSum` performs the per-prior-year recompute by *recursively* calling `computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal`, which accepts the full `worksheetLine5` exclusion add-back parameter. V48's `priorYearWorksheetLine5ExclusionsForRecompute` column gives the user a per-prior-year channel to pass exclusion add-backs into that recursive call. So the exclusion-add-back interaction with the lump-sum election IS effectively handled ‚Äî the application uses the same worksheet logic for both the current-year leg and the per-prior-year recompute legs, with full per-leg exclusion-add-back inputs. **No code gap.**
- **"Worksheet 4 = prior-year benefit repayment case"** was a misidentification in the original 6c.md draft. Pub. 915 does not contain a worksheet specifically named "Worksheet 4 for prior-year repayments." Repayments are governed by Publication 17 Chapter 7 plus IRC ¬ß1341 (claim of right), and the application already handles them via `6a.md` Gap 1 closure (Schedule A line 16 auto-add for excess SSA/RRB repayments, with ¬ß17 blocker when standard-deduction-in-use prevents the in-return remedy). Per IRS Form 1040 line-6c instructions, **Line 6c is exclusively for the lump-sum *election method*** (back-payment scenarios), not for repayment scenarios ‚Äî so there is no Line 6c interaction missing. **No code gap.**

**Status:** Closed as a documentation correction. Both sub-claims of the original Gap 4 were either already implemented (Worksheet 2 via the recursive call) or misclassified (Worksheet 4 is not a Pub. 915 thing ‚Äî it was conflated with the separate repayment-handling rules already covered by `6a.md`).
