# Form 1040 — Line 6d — Married Filing Separately and lived apart all year checkbox

**Tax year: 2025**

This document explains how the **Line 6d** checkbox on Form 1040 is set. Line 6d is a single Yes / No box (the application stores it as a boolean true / false). It is **checked** when the taxpayer's filing status is Married Filing Separately AND the taxpayer lived apart from the spouse for the **entire** tax year. It is **unchecked** in every other situation.

Line 6d is a label-only checkbox on the printed Form 1040 — it carries no dollar amount of its own. Its purpose is to inform the Internal Revenue Service that the Social Security Benefits Worksheet was computed using the Single-equivalent $25,000 base-amount path (rather than the more restrictive Married Filing Separately path that uses a $0 base amount and forces ~85% of benefits into the taxable bucket). The dollar consequence of the checkbox is captured entirely on **Line 6b**, documented separately in `6b.md`.

The document is organized in three parts:

1. **The IRS rule.** What the controlling Internal Revenue Service publications say must happen. This is the source of truth — the application must match it.
2. **The code implementation.** What the application's backend Java code currently does.
3. **The comparison.** A side-by-side check showing whether the code follows the IRS rule honestly, and where the two diverge.

Note on terminology used throughout (defined once, used repeatedly):

- **Internal Revenue Service** is abbreviated as IRS. The IRS is the United States federal agency that collects taxes.
- **Internal Revenue Code** is abbreviated as IRC. The IRC is the body of federal tax law passed by Congress; section numbers like "IRC §86(c)(1)(C)" cite specific subsections.
- **Social Security Administration** is abbreviated as SSA. The SSA pays Social Security retirement, survivor, and disability benefits.
- **Railroad Retirement Board** is abbreviated as RRB. The RRB administers retirement benefits for railroad workers.
- **Form SSA-1099** is the year-end statement the SSA sends to anyone who received Social Security benefits.
- **Form RRB-1099** (blue) reports the Social Security Equivalent Benefit portion of Tier 1 railroad retirement benefits.
- **Married Filing Jointly** is abbreviated as MFJ.
- **Married Filing Separately** is abbreviated as MFS. A married couple may elect MFS for any of several reasons (separate spouse-side liability, separation prior to divorce, etc.). The MFS election is per-return; both spouses' returns share the choice.
- **Head of Household** is abbreviated as HOH.
- **Qualifying Surviving Spouse** is abbreviated as QSS.
- **Single** filing status is used by unmarried filers who do not qualify for HOH or QSS.
- **Adjusted Gross Income** is abbreviated as AGI. AGI is Form 1040 Line 11b.
- **Lived apart for the entire tax year** is the IRS phrase used in the worksheet and instructions. It means the taxpayer and spouse did not share a residence at any point during the tax year (January 1 through December 31). Even one night sharing a residence — for example, a spouse staying overnight during a brief visit — disqualifies the taxpayer from checking Line 6d.
- **Lived with spouse at any time during the tax year** is the inverse phrase. On a Married Filing Separately return exactly one of these two states is true: either lived-apart-all-year (Line 6d = checked) or lived-with-spouse-at-some-time (Line 6d = unchecked).
- **Base amount** is the working term used by the Social Security Benefits Worksheet for the filing-status-driven threshold below which no benefits are taxable. The Married Filing Separately "lived with spouse" base amount is **$0** per IRC §86(c)(1)(D) — a statutory anti-loophole rule that prevents married couples from using the MFS election to avoid Social Security taxation while still living together. The Single base amount is **$25,000** per IRC §86(c)(1)(C). Checking Line 6d places the Married Filing Separately filer in the Single-equivalent $25,000 base-amount path.

## Part 1 — The IRS rule (the source of truth)

### 1.1 Authority

The IRS publications used to write this document are in `C:\us-tax\docs\books\`:

- **2025 Instructions for Form 1040 / 1040-SR** (`i1040gi_2025.pdf` / `i1040gi_2025.txt`) — the printed Line 6d label reads: *"Check this box if you are married filing separately and you lived apart from your spouse for all of 2025."* The corresponding Social Security Benefits Worksheet line 9 reads: *"If you are: ... single, head of household, qualifying surviving spouse, or married filing separately AND you lived apart from your spouse for all of 2025, enter $25,000."*
- **Publication 17 — Your Federal Income Tax** (`p17.pdf`) — Chapter 7 ("Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits") narrates the same rule in plain English, including the worksheet note that MFS filers who *did* live with the spouse at any time during the year must "skip lines 9 through 16" and apply a direct 85% computation.
- **Publication 915 — Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits** — the deep reference for the Social Security Benefits Worksheet, with the same Line 9 base-amount selection and the same MFS-restrictive-branch note.
- **Internal Revenue Code §86(c)(1)(C)** — the statute that authorizes the $25,000 base amount for Single filers (and, by reference, for the Married Filing Separately filer who lived apart all year).
- **Internal Revenue Code §86(c)(1)(D)** — the companion anti-loophole statute. It sets the $0 base amount for the Married Filing Separately filer who lived with the spouse at any time during the year. Without §86(c)(1)(D), a married couple could file MFS and each claim the $25,000 Single base amount — effectively doubling the threshold while still sharing a residence. §86(c)(1)(D) prevents that by zeroing out the base amount in the lived-with-spouse case.

### 1.2 What Line 6d is (and is not)

**What it is:**

- A label-only checkbox on Form 1040 Line 6d, immediately to the right of the Line 6c lump-sum election checkbox.
- A *flag* that tells the IRS that the Social Security Benefits Worksheet was run with the Single-equivalent $25,000 base amount because the Married Filing Separately filer lived apart from the spouse for all of 2025.
- New for tax year 2025. In tax years 2024 and earlier the IRS asked the filer to write the letter "D" by hand next to the Line 6a / Line 6b amounts on the printed form (and at the top of the Social Security Benefits Worksheet line 9). The 2025 form replaces the handwritten "D" with a printed checkbox.
- Driven exclusively by two return-level facts: the filing status and the residence pattern.

**What it is not:**

- It is not a dollar amount.
- It is not relevant to non-Married-Filing-Separately filers. Single, MFJ, HOH, and QSS filers always leave Line 6d unchecked. The checkbox is only meaningful when filing status is Married Filing Separately.
- It is not a one-touch override on the worksheet base amount. The taxpayer must affirmatively assert the lived-apart-all-year fact (see §1.5 for the partial-year nuance). Silent absence of the assertion means Line 6d stays unchecked and the worksheet uses the restrictive $0 base amount.
- It is not the same as the Line 6c lump-sum election checkbox. The two checkboxes are independent — Line 6c is about IRC §86(e) lump-sum back-payment elections (see `6c.md`); Line 6d is about the residence-pattern base-amount selection.

### 1.3 The IRS rule for checking Line 6d (verbatim spec, paraphrased)

Per IRC §86(c)(1)(C)/(D) + the 2025 Form 1040 line 6d label + Publication 17 Chapter 7 + `C:\us-tax\lines\6abcd.md` §8:

```text
Definitions
-----------
filing_status            := the return-level filing status as selected on the
                            filing-status personal form
lived_apart_all_year     := the taxpayer's affirmative answer to the question
                            "Did you and your spouse live apart for the entire
                            tax year (January 1 through December 31)?"

The IRS rule
------------
line6d_checked = (filing_status == "Married filing separately")
                 AND (lived_apart_all_year == true)
```

Two conditions, both must be true. If either is false the checkbox is unchecked.

The downstream worksheet effects:

```text
if filing_status == "Married filing separately":
    if lived_apart_all_year is true:
        line6d_checked = true
        worksheet_line_9_base_amount = $25,000    # Single-equivalent path
    else:    # lived with spouse at some point during the year
        line6d_checked = false
        worksheet_line_9_base_amount = $0          # restrictive branch
                                                   # (per IRC §86(c)(1)(D))
        # worksheet line 9-16 SKIPPED; line 17 = line 8 × 0.85
```

For non-MFS filers Line 6d is structurally false and the worksheet uses the base amount appropriate to the filing status ($32,000 for MFJ, $25,000 for Single / HOH / QSS).

### 1.4 The mutual-exclusion expectation on Married Filing Separately returns

On a Married Filing Separately return, the two residence facts `lived_apart_all_year` and `lived_with_spouse_at_any_time` are **logical inverses** — they describe a single boolean state from two perspectives. Per the IRS worksheet and IRC §86(c)(1)(C)/(D), exactly one of the two is true on every Married Filing Separately return:

| Residence pattern | `lived_apart_all_year` | `lived_with_spouse_at_any_time` | Line 6d | Worksheet branch | Base amount |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Did not share residence at any point | true | false | checked | Single-equivalent | $25,000 |
| Shared residence at any point (even one night) | false | true | unchecked | Restrictive (skip lines 9–16) | $0 |

The IRS worksheet treats `lived_with_spouse_at_any_time` as the trigger for the restrictive branch. The Line 6d label keys off the inverse (`lived_apart_all_year`). The two facts are not independent — a Married Filing Separately filer cannot honestly answer "yes" to both, nor "no" to both.

### 1.5 The "all year" precision

The IRS phrase is "lived apart **for all of 2025**" — meaning the entire calendar year from January 1, 2025 through December 31, 2025. The rule has no partial-year carve-out:

- A couple that separated mid-year (say, on March 1, 2025) does **not** qualify to check Line 6d on a 2025 Married Filing Separately return. They lived together for January and February 2025.
- A couple that reconciled mid-year (say, was separated all of 2024 but moved back in together on November 15, 2025) does **not** qualify.
- A couple separated for the entire 2025 calendar year may check Line 6d even if they were not yet legally separated or divorced. The test is residence, not marital status.
- A brief overnight visit (spouse stays one night during a holiday) disqualifies the taxpayer from checking Line 6d. The test is strict.

The strictness mirrors the design intent of IRC §86(c)(1)(D): Congress wanted to prevent any meaningful cohabitation from qualifying for the more generous $25,000 base amount, so the "any time" trigger on the inverse flag is deliberately broad.

### 1.6 Reference-data constants used by Line 6d

Line 6d has **no reference-data constants of its own**. The checkbox is a pure boolean function of the filing status and the residence-pattern assertion. The thresholds that the Line 6d state selects between ($25,000 vs $0) belong to Line 6b (see `6b.md` §1.6 for the IRC §86 thresholds and their non-inflation-indexed history). The filing-status enumeration values come from the filing-status personal form, not from the Line 6d logic.

### 1.7 Inputs required (per IRS Form 1040 Line 6d label)

To compute Line 6d the IRS needs:

1. **Filing status.** Selected on the filing-status personal form. The Line 6d label is only relevant when this is Married Filing Separately.
2. **Residence-pattern answer.** Required only when filing status is Married Filing Separately. The taxpayer must affirmatively answer the question "Did you and your spouse live apart for the entire tax year?"

For non-Married-Filing-Separately returns the residence-pattern input is irrelevant because the filing-status gate already settles Line 6d as unchecked.

### 1.8 Out of scope for Line 6d

The following situations exist on the return but do not affect Line 6d directly:

- **The Line 6c lump-sum election.** Independent decision per IRC §86(e); documented in `6c.md`.
- **The Pub. 915 lump-sum recompute** (Worksheet 3). Independent of the Line 6d filing-status path; the recompute respects the prior-year filing status (per the V48 `priorYearFilingStatus` override) without affecting current-year Line 6d.
- **The Pub. 590-A IRA-deduction coordination worksheet.** Drives Line 6b through a separate path; Line 6d state is read by Pub. 590-A's worksheet but Line 6d itself does not change.
- **Spouse-side Social Security benefits on a non-MFS joint return.** On MFJ the worksheet treats the couple's combined benefits and uses the $32,000 base amount; Line 6d is structurally false and irrelevant.
- **The legacy handwritten "D" notation** from tax years 2024 and earlier. The 2025 form replaces this with the printed Line 6d checkbox; legacy notations are not produced by the application.

### 2.4 The code's implemented equation for Line 6d (plain English of the algorithm)

```text
Step 1 — Read the filing status from the filing-status personal form.
    filingStatusValue = normalizeFilingStatus(getString(filingStatus, "filingStatus"))
    isMfs = ("Married filing separately".equalsIgnoreCase(filingStatusValue))

Step 2 — Read both residence-pattern facts from the social-security taxpayer form.
    # Boolean.TRUE.equals(...) returns false for both null and Boolean.FALSE.
    # So the "missing answer" case is treated as false (conservative default — no
    # affirmative attestation, no base-amount benefit).
    livedApartAllYear = (getBoolean(socialSecurityTaxpayer,
                                    "livedApartFromSpouseEntireTaxYear") == true)
    livedWithSpouseAnyTime = (getBoolean(socialSecurityTaxpayer,
                                         "livedWithSpouseAnyTimeDuringTaxYear") == true)
    # livedWithSpouseAnyTime is read here but NOT consumed by the Line 6d gate.
    # It feeds the Line 6b restrictive-branch decision in computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal.

Step 3 — Apply the two-condition AND gate.
    line6d = isMfs AND livedApartAllYear

Step 4 — Persist into the SocialSecurityComputation record.
    socialSecurityComputation.line6dMfsLivedApartAllYear(line6d)

Step 5 — Surface to the output JSON via Income.setLine6dMfsLivedApartAllYear.
```

### 2.5 Reference-data constants used by Line 6d

Line 6d has **no reference-data constants of its own** in the code — the decision is a pure boolean comparison against a fixed filing-status string literal. The filing-status string `"Married filing separately"` is compared case-insensitively via `equalsIgnoreCase`, after normalization through `normalizeFilingStatus` (which converts internal enum forms like `MarriedFilingSeparately` to the canonical display string).

### 2.6 Defaults and safety

- **Missing filing status** → `normalizeFilingStatus(null)` returns null → `equalsIgnoreCase` returns false → `isMfs = false` → Line 6d = false. Safe default.
- **Missing residence answer** → `getBoolean(...)` returns null → `Boolean.TRUE.equals(null)` returns false → `livedApartAllYear = false` → Line 6d = false. Safe default for non-MFS filers (where Line 6d is irrelevant anyway). For MFS filers the conservative default is that the taxpayer did NOT attest to lived-apart-all-year — which routes them to the restrictive worksheet branch via the Line 6b path, costing them tax dollars if the lived-apart-all-year answer was true but unattested.
- **Missing social-security personal form** → both residence reads return null → Line 6d = false. Safe.

### 3.1 Side-by-side equation comparison

### 3.2 Verified-correct items

- **Filing-status gate is structurally correct.** Only Married Filing Separately filers can have Line 6d checked. The other four filing statuses (Single, MFJ, HOH, QSS) always have Line 6d = false because the `isMfs` leg of the AND-gate is false.
- **Conservative default for missing attestation.** A Married Filing Separately filer who leaves `livedApartFromSpouseEntireTaxYear` unanswered does not silently get the $25,000 base amount. The code's `Boolean.TRUE.equals(null) == false` semantics route the unattested filer through the restrictive worksheet branch (per the separate `livedWithSpouseAnyTime` path on Line 6b). This is the right disposition because the more-generous outcome should require an affirmative answer.
- **Atomic boolean assignment.** Line 6d is set once at the orchestrator (line 12768) and persisted into the `SocialSecurityComputation` record. No alternate code paths can mutate it. The output JSON value cannot disagree with the computed boolean.
- **No reference-data drift risk.** Line 6d has no thresholds or amounts of its own, so it is immune to changes in the IRC §86 base amounts (which Congress could theoretically raise) or to inflation-indexing decisions in future tax years.
- **String literal comparison is case-insensitive.** `"Married filing separately".equalsIgnoreCase(filingStatusValue)` correctly handles the display-form / enum-form difference after `normalizeFilingStatus`.
- **Lock-in test.** `computesSocialSecurityLinesWithLumpSumElectionAndLine6d` (`TaxReturnComputeServiceTest.java` ~line 4838) asserts that the MFS-lived-apart-all-year scenario sets `line6d = true` atomically with `line6c = true`. The corresponding contrapositive (`line6d = false` for MFJ, Single, and MFS-lived-with-spouse) is locked in by the e2e tests in `line6abcd-social-security-benefits.spec.ts` (`Tests 2, 3, 4, 6`).

### 3.3 Gaps (where the code diverges from a strict IRS reading)

#### Gap 1 — Mutual-exclusion of `livedApartAllYear` and `livedWithSpouseAnyTime` — **CLOSED 2026-06-06 (§17 non-overrideable)**

**Severity:** High after audit-risk analysis — Case B is an IRS-detectable under-payment scenario; Case A is self-harm but produces internally-inconsistent return data. Promoted from "deferred / low priority" to §17 non-overrideable per the project's audit-risk-avoidance principle.

**Background:** On a Married Filing Separately return, the two residence facts are logical inverses per IRC §86(c)(1)(C)/(D) and the IRS Social Security Benefits Worksheet. Exactly one should be true:

| Real residence pattern | `livedApartFromSpouseEntireTaxYear` (correct) | `livedWithSpouseAnyTimeDuringTaxYear` (correct) |
|---|---|---|
| Apart all year | true | false |
| Shared residence at any point | false | true |

The application's social-security taxpayer form stores them as **two independent booleans**. Without UI or backend validation, the user can save four boolean combinations on a Married Filing Separately return; only two are coherent.

**The two pathological cases (pre-closure analysis):**

1. **Both true.** The user (mistakenly) checked both "we lived apart all year" and "we lived together at some point." Code behavior:
   - Line 6d = true (because `isMfs && livedApartAllYear` is true).
   - Line 6b uses the restrictive branch (because `isMfs && livedWithSpouseAnyTime` is true — `computeTaxableSocialSecurityNormal` jumps to the IRC §86(c)(1)(D) skip-9-to-16 path with a $0 base).
   - Result: the printed Form 1040 shows Line 6d checked (asserting the $25,000 base path) but Line 6b reflects the $0 restrictive computation. The two disagree internally. **Self-harm**: user over-pays because the restrictive branch fires regardless of the Line 6d claim.
2. **Both false / null.** The user left both unanswered (or both answered No). Code behavior:
   - Line 6d = false (because `livedApartAllYear` is false).
   - Line 6b uses the normal MFS path with base $25,000 (because `isMfs && livedWithSpouseAnyTime` is false — falls through to the non-restrictive branch).
   - Result: the user enjoys the more-generous $25,000 base on Line 6b without checking Line 6d to disclose the lived-apart-all-year status. **Audit risk**: the IRS has the MFS-return + SSA-1099 facts on file and can detect the missing Line 6d disclosure → CP-2000 underreporter notice + recompute under the restrictive branch.

**Closure (2026-06-06):** Added `SOCIAL_SECURITY_LINE6D_MFS_RESIDENCE_INCONSISTENT` as a §17 non-overrideable blocker, produced by the new helper `validateLine6dMfsResidenceConsistency` (`TaxReturnComputeService.java`). The blocker:

- Fires only when filing status is Married Filing Separately AND `hadSocialSecurityBenefits` is true (the residence questions are only meaningful when both conditions hold).
- Detects both Case A (both flags true) and Case B (both flags not-true).
- Emits a long-form message that names the specific case (Case A vs Case B), explains the IRS rule (IRC §86(c)(1)(C)/(D)), describes the silent failure mode (over-payment in Case A, under-payment + audit risk in Case B), and provides the two-path resolution (answer exactly one of the two questions Yes).
- Registered in `NonOverrideableFlags.CODES` per the §17 disposition pattern (the second of the two changes required for §17 promotion — see [[non-overrideable-flags-registry]] in `MEMORY.md`).

**Why §17 (not non-blocking advisory):** The disposition mirrors the other Social Security cluster §17 blockers (`SSI_AMOUNT_REQUIRED`, `LUMP_SUM_ALLOCATION_REQUIRED`, `LUMP_SUM_PRIOR_YEAR_REPORTED_REQUIRED`, `LUMP_SUM_PRIOR_YEAR_DEFAULTS_APPLIED`). Same failure mode (silent wrong output on the return), same disposition. Resolution is a pure user-side data fix (the user must answer the residence question correctly) — no judgment call to defer.

**Lock-in tests (Java unit):**

- `mfsResidenceFlagsBothTrueFiresLine6dInconsistencySection17` — Case A positive.
- `mfsResidenceFlagsBothFalseFiresLine6dInconsistencySection17` — Case B positive.
- `mfsResidenceFlagsCoherentLivedApartDoesNotFireLine6dInconsistency` — coherent apart-all-year negative.
- `mfsResidenceFlagsBothNotTrueOnNonMfsReturnDoesNotFireLine6dInconsistency` — non-MFS exemption negative (Single filer).

The pre-existing `computesMfsLivedWithSpouseSocialSecurityUsingWorksheetBranch` test already exercises the coherent lived-with-spouse case (apart=false, withSpouse=true) and continues to pass — the §17 does not fire because the flags are coherent.

**Lock-in tests (e2e):**

- `MFS+SS taxpayer with both residence flags missing fires §17 Line 6d inconsistency blocker (closes 6d.md Gap 1)` — Case B positive case using the established `?overrideFlags=true` pattern that validates the §17 enforcement layer (per the registry-validation lesson from `test_cases/6c.md` GAP X4).

**Why no UI change in this closure:** The §17 backend block is sufficient to comply with the IRS rule, avoid audit risk, and prevent user loss. A UI single-select radio-button group (the original Option A in the deferred analysis) would be a UX-improvement layer on top of the §17 block — desirable but not required for IRS correctness. Tracked as a separate follow-up if user-experience review indicates the §17 message is hit too often in practice.

#### Gap 2 — Line 6d is always emitted as `true` or `false` even on non-MFS returns

**Severity:** Informational only — not a defect; documented here for completeness so a reader does not mistake the always-present-false value for a bug.

On a non-MFS return (Single, MFJ, HOH, QSS) the Line 6d output JSON field is `false` rather than `null` or omitted. This is consistent with the printed Form 1040 (the checkbox is always present on the form, just unchecked for non-MFS filers) and with the IRS expectation that the checkbox state is unambiguous.
