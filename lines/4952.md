Usually **one Form 4952 per return**, not one per spouse.

Form 4952 is attached to **the tax return** to compute that return’s allowable investment interest deduction and carryforward. On a **Married Filing Jointly** return, you generally combine both spouses’ investment interest expense and investment income into **one** Form 4952 attached to the joint return. The form itself is designed as an attachment to a single return (“Attach to your tax return”). ([IRS][1])

### Married Filing Jointly

* **One joint Form 4952**
* Combine both spouses’ relevant investment income and investment interest expense on that one form.

### Married Filing Separately

* Each spouse files their **own return**
* So each spouse may have **their own Form 4952** on their own separate return.

### Do you need one per spouse on a joint return?

* **No.** Not on an MFJ return.
* The only common exception-like situation is **AMT**: if the return also needs an AMT version of the investment-interest computation, the IRS says to fill out a **second Form 4952 for AMT purposes** when completing Form 6251 line 2c. That AMT Form 4952 is a computational support form, not a second per-spouse filing on a joint return. ([IRS][2])

### Developer-safe rule

```text
if filing_status == MFJ:
    create at most one regular Form4952 for the return
elif filing_status in {Single, HOH, QSS, MFS}:
    create at most one regular Form4952 per return
```

And if `AMT_requires_investment_interest_recompute == true`, generate an **AMT-side Form 4952 computation artifact** as well. ([IRS][2])

### Expense allocation note

If spouses file **separate** returns and paid investment-related amounts from a **joint account** (or community property funds), the IRS generally says to **split the deduction appropriately** between them under the normal joint-expense allocation rules. ([IRS][3])
