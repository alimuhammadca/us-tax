Yes — on a joint return, you can need **two separate Forms 4972**, one for each spouse, **if each spouse received a qualifying lump-sum distribution**. The 2025 Form 4972 instructions state that if spouses file jointly and **each** received a lump-sum distribution, you must **complete and file a separate Form 4972 for each spouse**, then **combine the tax** and include the combined amount on **Form 1040 or 1040-SR, line 16**, checking **box 2**. ([irs.gov][1])

More generally, Form 4972 is really **per plan participant**, not simply "per return." If you received **more than one qualified distribution in 2025 for the same participant**, you **combine them on one Form 4972**. If you received qualified distributions in 2025 for **more than one participant**, you file a **separate Form 4972 for each participant's distribution**. The instructions also say you can make separate elections as a beneficiary for different deceased participants. ([irs.gov][1])

## When Form 4972 can be used

Form 4972 is only for a **qualified lump-sum distribution**. The form and IRS Topic 412 describe that as a distribution in one tax year of the participant's **entire balance** from all of the employer's qualified plans of one kind, and the participant generally must have been **born before January 2, 1936**. A beneficiary can also use it if the deceased participant met that birth-date requirement, and an alternate payee spouse/former spouse under a QDRO can also qualify. ([irs.gov][1])

You **cannot** use Form 4972 for several common situations, including:

* a **partial rollover** amount,
* an **IRA** distribution,
* a **403(b)** distribution,
* a corrective distribution of excess deferrals/contributions,
* a distribution during the first 5 tax years of plan participation unless due to death,
* and, after 1986, another 5-year or 10-year election for the **same participant**. ([irs.gov][1])

## How to complete Form 4972

Use this sequence.

### 1) Gather the source statement

You generally need **Form 1099-R** from the payer. The form/instructions specifically use:

* **Box 2a** taxable amount,
* **Box 3** capital gain portion,
* **Box 6** net unrealized appreciation (NUA),
* **Box 8** current actuarial value of annuity,
* **Box 9a** percentage of distribution for multiple-recipient cases.
  If box 2a is blank, the instructions say you must figure the taxable amount yourself, referring to **Pub. 575**. ([irs.gov][1])

### 2) Complete Part I first — eligibility gate

Part I is mandatory and asks the yes/no questions that determine whether you can use the form:

* Was this the participant's **entire balance** from all qualified plans of one kind?
* Did you **roll over** any part of it?
* Was it received as beneficiary of a participant born before January 2, 1936?
* Or were **you** the participant, born before January 2, 1936, and in the plan at least 5 years?
* Did you already use Form 4972 after 1986 for a previous qualifying distribution from that same participant?
  If the answers fail these tests, you stop and do **not** use Form 4972. ([irs.gov][1])

### 3) Decide which election(s) you are using

Form 4972 can compute the tax using:

* the **20% capital gain election** in **Part II**,
* the **10-year tax option** in **Part III**,
* or **both**.
  The IRS describes these as special formulas that may reduce tax compared with reporting the whole taxable amount as ordinary income. ([irs.gov][2])

### 4) Part II — 20% capital gain election

Use Part II only for the capital-gain-eligible portion. On the 2025 form:

* **Line 6** = capital gain part from **Form 1099-R box 3**
* **Line 7** = line 6 × 20%
  If you are using only Part II and not Part III, line 7 is the amount that goes into the total on **Form 1040 line 16**, box 2 checked. ([irs.gov][1])

### 5) Part III — 10-year tax option

Part III computes a separate tax on the ordinary-income portion of the lump-sum distribution. On the 2025 form, it starts from:

* **Line 8** = box 2a, or box 2a minus box 3 if you completed Part II, with special instructions for multiple recipients and NUA,
* **Line 9** death benefit exclusion for certain old distributions,
* **Line 10** total taxable amount,
* **Line 11** actuarial value from **box 8** if any,
  then continues through the built-in 10-year averaging formula down to **line 29**. Finally:
* **Line 30** = line 7 + line 29, and this total is included on **Form 1040 line 16**, checking **box 2**. ([irs.gov][1])

### 6) NUA and multiple-recipient cases

If employer securities with **net unrealized appreciation (NUA)** are involved, the form instructs you to use the **NUA Worksheet** and possibly include part of the box 6 amount in taxable income, depending on your election. If there are **multiple recipients**, the instructions provide special adjustment steps using **box 9a** percentages before completing line 29. ([irs.gov][1])

### 7) How the 1099-R is reported if you do or do not use Form 4972

The instructions distinguish three cases:

* If you **do not** use Form 4972, report the 1099-R on **Form 1040 lines 5a and 5b** in the usual way.
* If you use **Part II only**, report the **ordinary income portion** on lines 5a/5b, and the capital gain election tax comes through Form 4972.
* If you use **Part III**, do **not** include any part of the distribution on lines 5a/5b; instead the tax is computed on Form 4972 and carried to **line 16**. ([irs.gov][1])

## How many Forms 4972 go with one return

Use this filing rule:

```text
one Form 4972 per participant whose qualifying lump-sum distributions are being elected
```

That means:

* **Same participant, multiple qualifying distributions in the same year** → **one** Form 4972, combined. ([irs.gov][1])
* **Different participants** (for example, two deceased parents as beneficiary) → **separate** Form 4972 for each participant. ([irs.gov][1])
* **Joint return where each spouse has their own qualifying lump-sum distribution** → **separate Form 4972 for each spouse**. Yes, effectively **one per spouse** in that scenario. ([irs.gov][1])

## Bottom line

To complete Form 4972, you:

1. confirm eligibility in **Part I**,
2. use **1099-R** to populate the capital-gain and/or 10-year-averaging computation,
3. compute the tax through **line 30**,
4. and carry that tax to **Form 1040 line 16**, checking **box 2**.
   On a joint return, if **both spouses** received qualifying lump-sum distributions, you file **two separate Forms 4972**, one for each spouse; but if one person received multiple qualifying distributions from the **same participant**, you combine them on **one** Form 4972. ([irs.gov][1])
