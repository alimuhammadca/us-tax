Yes — **effectively it is one Form 8615 per child return**, not one per parent return or one per spouse. Form 8615 says to **attach it only to the child’s Form 1040 or 1040‑NR**, and the instructions’ multi-child example shows that when several children are subject to kiddie tax, **each child has their own Form 8615** and each form’s line 7 picks up the other children’s line 5 amounts. So if a family has three children who each must pay kiddie tax, there are **three separate child returns**, each with **one Form 8615** attached. Also, Form 8615 does **not** apply if the child files a joint return. ([IRS][1])

## When Form 8615 is required

File Form 8615 for a child only if **all** of these are true for 2025: the child had **more than $2,700 of unearned income**; is **required to file** a return; was **under 18**, or was **18** and didn’t have earned income over half their support, or was a **full-time student age 19–23** and didn’t have earned income over half their support; **at least one parent was alive** at year-end; and the child **didn’t file a joint return**. These rules apply whether or not the child is your dependent, and they don’t apply if neither parent was alive at year-end. If the parent instead makes the **Form 8814** election, the child generally won’t file Form 8615. ([IRS][2])

## What counts as unearned income

For Form 8615, unearned income is generally income other than pay for work performed. It includes taxable interest, dividends, capital gains and capital gain distributions, rents, royalties, pension and annuity income, taxable scholarship/fellowship not on Form W‑2, unemployment compensation, alimony, the taxable part of Social Security and pension payments, and trust beneficiary income. **Tax-exempt** unearned income is not included, and taxable distributions from a **qualified disability trust** are treated as **earned** income for this form. Capital losses are netted in figuring unearned income, including the usual up-to-$3,000 reduction of other unearned income ($1,500 MFS). ([IRS][2])

## Which parent’s return you use

Use the parent return as follows:

* If the parents file **jointly**, use the joint return.
* If married but filing **separately**, use the parent with the **higher taxable income**.
* If married but **not living together**, and the custodial parent is considered unmarried, use the **custodial parent’s** return; otherwise use the parent with the higher taxable income.
* If divorced or legally separated, use the **custodial parent’s** return.
* If the custodial parent remarried, use the custodial parent/stepparent return under the remarriage rules.
* If the parents were never married but lived together with the child, use the parent with the **higher taxable income**.
* If parent and child have different tax years, use the parent’s return for the year that **ends in** the child’s tax year. ([IRS][2])

## How to complete Form 8615, line by line

### Header lines A–C

Enter the child’s name and SSN. Then enter the **parent name and SSN** determined under the rules above, and check the parent’s **filing status**. If the parents filed jointly, use the parent listed **first** on the joint return. ([IRS][1])

### Part I — Child’s net unearned income

**Line 1.** Enter the child’s unearned income. If the child had **no earned income**, line 1 is the child’s **Form 1040/1040‑NR line 11b**. If the child had earned income, use the **Child’s Unearned Income Worksheet**: child line 9 minus earned income and Schedule 1 line 18 penalty on early withdrawal. If the child files **Form 2555**, has a self-employment loss, or claims an NOL deduction, use the **Alternate Worksheet for Form 8615, line 1** instead. ([IRS][1])

**Line 2.** If the child did **not** itemize deductions, enter **$2,700**. If the child itemized, enter the **larger of**:

* **$1,350 +** the portion of Schedule A line 17 directly connected with producing the unearned income, or
* **$2,700**. ([IRS][1])

**Line 3.** `line3 = line1 − line2`. If zero or less, stop; **do not complete the rest of the form**, but still **attach Form 8615** to the child’s return. The child’s tax is then figured normally. ([IRS][1])

**Line 4.** Enter the child’s **taxable income** from **Form 1040/1040‑NR line 15**. If the child files **Form 2555**, use **line 3 of the child’s Foreign Earned Income Tax Worksheet** instead. ([IRS][1])

**Line 5.** Enter the **smaller of line 3 or line 4**. This is the child’s **net unearned income**. If zero, stop, but still attach Form 8615. ([IRS][1])

### Part II — Tentative tax based on the parent’s rate

**Line 6.** Enter the **parent’s taxable income** from **Form 1040/1040‑NR line 15**. If zero or less, enter zero. If the parent files **Form 2555**, use **line 3 of the parent’s Foreign Earned Income Tax Worksheet** instead. ([IRS][1])

**Line 7.** Enter the total of **line 5** from all the **other children’s** Forms 8615 that use the same parent return information. Do **not** include the current child’s own line 5. This is why each affected child needs a separate Form 8615. The instructions give a three-child example showing exactly how the sibling amounts cross-feed into line 7. ([IRS][1])

**Line 8.** `line8 = line5 + line6 + line7`. Before computing line 9, determine how much **net capital gain** and **qualified dividends** are included in line 8, using the instructions and the line 5 worksheets when needed. If neither the child, parent, nor other affected children have capital gain or qualified dividends, those amounts are zero. ([IRS][1])

**Line 9.** Figure the tax on **line 8** using the **parent’s filing status**. Use the regular tax table/computation worksheet unless a preferential-rate worksheet applies. If qualified dividends/capital gain rules apply, use the **Qualified Dividends and Capital Gain Tax Worksheet**; if Schedule D special-rate items apply, use the **Schedule D Tax Worksheet**; if Schedule J applies, use that instead. Check the box on line 9 if one of those special worksheets was used. ([IRS][1])

**Line 10.** Enter the parent’s tax from **Form 1040/1040‑NR line 16**, **minus AMT**. Do **not** include any tax from **Form 4972**, **Form 8814**, or education credit recapture. If the parent files Form 2555, follow the special instructions. If the parent used the qualified-dividend/capital-gain worksheet, Schedule D worksheet, or Schedule J, check the box on line 10. ([IRS][1])

**Line 11.** `line11 = line9 − line10`. If line 7 is blank, also enter this same amount on **line 13** and skip lines 12a–12b. ([IRS][1])

**Lines 12a and 12b.**

* `line12a = line5 + line7`
* `line12b = line5 ÷ line12a`
  This allocates the tentative parent-rate tax across multiple children. The instructions give an example where a child’s line 12b is the child’s line 5 divided by line 12a. ([IRS][1])

**Line 13.**

* If line 7 is blank: `line13 = line11`
* Otherwise: `line13 = line11 × line12b`
  This is the child’s **share** of the tentative tax at the parent’s rate. ([IRS][1])

### Part III — Child’s tax

**Line 14.** If **line 4 equals line 5**, enter **0** on lines 14 and 15 and go to line 16. Otherwise:
`line14 = line4 − line5`
This is the portion of the child’s taxable income **not** subject to kiddie tax. If the child has qualified dividends or net capital gain, you must determine how much of those amounts are included in line 14 before computing line 15. ([IRS][1])

**Line 15.** Figure the tax on **line 14** using the **child’s filing status**. Use the tax table / tax computation worksheet, or the **Qualified Dividends and Capital Gain Tax Worksheet**, **Schedule D Tax Worksheet**, or **Schedule J** if applicable. Check the box on line 15 if one of those special worksheets was used. ([IRS][1])

**Line 16.** `line16 = line13 + line15`
This is the child’s tax figured partly at the **parent’s rate** on net unearned income and partly at the **child’s rate** on the rest. ([IRS][1])

**Line 17.** Figure the tax on the full amount on **line 4** using the **child’s filing status** and the applicable worksheet. Check the box if you used the qualified-dividend/capital-gain worksheet, Schedule D worksheet, or Schedule J. ([IRS][1])

**Line 18.** Enter the **larger of line 16 or line 17**. This is the child’s tax. Enter it on the child’s **Form 1040/1040‑NR line 16**. If the child files **Form 2555**, do **not** carry line 18 directly to Form 1040 line 16; instead, put it on **line 4 of the child’s Foreign Earned Income Tax Worksheet** and finish that worksheet. ([IRS][1])

## Important implementation notes

If the parent’s taxable income, filing status, or the other children’s net unearned income isn’t known by the child’s original due date, the instructions say the child should generally request an extension with **Form 4868**. If the parent’s return information is unavailable, the child or legal representative can request it from the IRS after year-end. If the parent’s income later changes, or another child’s Form 8615 numbers change, the child may need to amend with **Form 1040‑X**. Also, a child subject to Form 8615 may still separately owe **AMT** or **NIIT** on the child’s own return. ([IRS][2])

## Short answer to your filing-count question

* **One Form 8615 per child return.**
* If **three children** each meet the Form 8615 rules, there are **three separate Forms 8615**, each attached to that child’s own return.
* It is **not** one per parent return, and it is **not** one per spouse.
* If the child files a **joint return**, Form 8615 does **not** apply. ([IRS][1])
